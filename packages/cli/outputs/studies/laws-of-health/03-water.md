# Study 3: Water

**Salvation is the restoration of God's image in the mind (Study 3, _The Way Home_). The brain that receives the Spirit is ~73% water. Every neuron fires in a saline bath; every thought costs hydration. Beyond drinking, water externally — hot, cold, alternating — is one of the oldest and most under-used therapeutic tools in medicine. The Designer who chose water as the symbol of cleansing, birth, and the Spirit Himself also built the body to run on it.**

## Introduction — You Are a Body of Water

- **The numbers**
  - The adult human body is ~60% water by weight — newborns are ~75%, older adults drop toward 50%
  - Blood plasma: ~90% water. Brain: ~73%. Heart: ~73%. Lungs: ~83%. Muscle: ~79%. Bone: ~31%.
  - Every biochemical reaction in your body happens in water — water is not just a beverage, it is the solvent in which life occurs
  - [→] You are not a solid that occasionally drinks water. You are a body of water wrapped in skin. Hydration is not optional maintenance; it is the medium through which everything else works.

- **The brain runs on water**
  - The brain is ~73% water and is especially sensitive to hydration changes
  - Even ~1-2% dehydration (a level most people reach without noticing) measurably impairs cognition, mood, short-term memory, and attention — particularly in tasks requiring sustained focus
  - Headaches, brain fog, and fatigue are often dehydration presenting as symptoms
  - [→] The faculty the Spirit works through is literally a water-based organ. A dehydrated brain is a dulled receiver.

[DYK🔎] In a 2011-2012 pair of studies (Ganio/Armstrong et al.), healthy young men at ~1.6% dehydration showed impaired vigilance and working memory plus more fatigue and tension; a parallel study in young women at ~1.36% dehydration showed mainly worse mood, concentration, perceived task difficulty, and headache, with less effect on objective cognitive tests. You do not have to be in a desert for water deficit to matter.

## Water Inside — What It Does

### Transport and Delivery

- **Blood is ~90% water**
  - Water is the transport medium for oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and immune cells
  - Dehydration thickens the blood, raises heart rate (the heart compensates), and reduces delivery efficiency
  - Many elderly patients diagnosed with "fatigue" or "confusion" improve dramatically when they are simply rehydrated

- **Lymphatic drainage**
  - The lymphatic system removes cellular waste — and it runs on water
  - Dehydrated lymph is sluggish; hydrated lymph flows
  - Muscle movement (Study 2) drives lymph; water supplies it. The two work together.

### Temperature Regulation

- Sweat is the body's cooling system; sweat is water; water loss is heat loss
- Without adequate water, the body cannot shed heat efficiently — core temperature rises, performance crashes, heatstroke becomes a risk
- Chronic mild dehydration predisposes to heat illness long before the dramatic symptoms appear

### Joints, Discs, and Structure

- Cartilage is ~70-80% water; intervertebral discs are ~70-90% water
- Dehydration reduces joint cushioning and contributes to low back pain and joint stiffness
- Staying hydrated protects the scaffolding you walk, sit, and sleep on

### Kidneys and Detoxification

- The kidneys produce ~180 liters of filtrate per day, reabsorb most of it, and excrete ~1-2 liters as urine
- Chronic underhydration forces the kidneys to concentrate urine, raising the risk of kidney stones, UTIs, and reduced clearance efficiency
- Pale yellow urine (like lemonade) = adequately hydrated; dark yellow = drink more

### The Gut

- Water is necessary for digestive secretions, for moving stool through the colon, and for absorbing nutrients
- Low fluid intake can worsen constipation, especially when fiber intake is increasing; hydration, fiber, movement, and regularity work together
- [EGW] "In health and in sickness, pure water is one of heaven's choicest blessings. Its proper use promotes health. It is the beverage which God provided to quench the thirst of animals and man. Drunk freely, it helps to supply the necessities of the system and assists nature to resist disease" (MH 237.1).

## Water Outside — Hydrotherapy

Scripture repeatedly uses water as a vehicle and sign of cleansing and healing. Hydrotherapy as a clinical practice belongs to physiology and Adventist medical history — and modern research keeps confirming what the Battle Creek Sanitarium was doing by the 1870s.

### The Physiology of Hot and Cold

- **Heat application** (hot pack, hot bath, sauna)
  - Dilates blood vessels, increases blood flow, relaxes muscles, lowers blood pressure temporarily
  - Raises heart rate and core temperature in a controlled way — mimicking a mild cardiovascular exercise
  - Activates heat-shock proteins, which help cells repair and resist stress

- **Cold application** (cold shower, ice pack, cold plunge)
  - Constricts blood vessels, reduces inflammation, activates the sympathetic nervous system briefly
  - Stimulates the release of norepinephrine (alertness, mood, focus)
  - Sustained or repeated cold exposure can activate brown adipose tissue (brown fat), which burns glucose for heat; brief cold showers mainly train vascular and autonomic responses

- **Contrast (alternating hot-cold)**
  - Pumps blood through tissues like a bellows — arguably the most therapeutic modality of the three
  - Reduces inflammation, speeds recovery, improves circulation
  - Used for centuries at European spas and, under EGW's counsel, at Battle Creek Sanitarium

### The Sauna Research

- **The Finnish cohort studies (Laukkanen et al., 2015 and later)**
  - Tracked 2,315 middle-aged Finnish men over ~20 years
  - Men who used sauna 4-7 times per week had roughly 50% lower fatal cardiovascular disease and ~66% lower risk of dementia compared to once-a-week users
  - Dose-response relationship: more sessions, longer duration, lower risk
  - These are observational cohorts (male, Finnish sauna culture, residual confounding possible) adjusted for major lifestyle risks — they show strong association, not proof of causation
  - [→] Still, the association is one of the larger lifestyle-mortality signals in the literature, and it aligns with plausible mechanisms: cardiovascular training effect, heat-shock protein response, improved endothelial function, lower long-term blood pressure.

[DYK🔎] The Finnish analyses adjusted for exercise, smoking, alcohol, BMI, and cardiovascular risk factors — and the sauna association persisted. Follow-up papers have extended the pattern to hypertension and stroke. None of this proves sauna itself drives the outcome; it does suggest regular heat exposure behaves like a meaningful cardiovascular stimulus in that population.

### Cold Exposure Research

- **Cold showers and sickness absence**
  - A 2016 Dutch RCT (Buijze et al., _PLoS ONE_) of 3,018 adults found that 30-90 seconds of cold water at the end of a normal shower reduced self-reported sickness absence by ~29% over 3 months
  - Interestingly, it did _not_ reduce the number of illness days — people got sick about as often, but stayed home less
  - [→] Not a cure for all ills; a cheap, repeatable nudge toward resilience, adherence, and wakefulness.

- **Cold and mood**
  - Cold exposure acutely raises norepinephrine and dopamine
  - Preliminary evidence suggests cold exposure may help with mild depression; research is early
  - Contrast showers (end cold for 30-60 seconds) are safer and more sustainable than ice baths for most people

### The Adventist Hydrotherapy Tradition

- Battle Creek Sanitarium (1866-) pioneered hydrotherapy under EGW counsel — hot/cold packs, contrast baths, fomentations, sitz baths
- The techniques were scoffed at in their day; many are now standard practice in physical therapy, sports medicine, and rehabilitation
- [EGW] "The external application of water is one of the easiest and most satisfactory ways of regulating the circulation of the blood. A cold or cool bath is an excellent tonic. Warm baths open the pores and thus aid in the elimination of impurities. Both warm and neutral baths soothe the nerves and equalize the circulation" (MH 237.1)

[EB] 2 Kings 5:1-14 — Naaman, commander of the Syrian army, expected a dramatic miracle for his leprosy. Elisha told him to dip seven times in the Jordan. Naaman was offended by the simplicity: "Are not Abana and Pharpar, rivers of Damascus, better than all the waters of Israel?" Only when he obeyed the simple instruction was he healed. God often uses water — literal, unremarkable water — as the vehicle of healing. The humble remedy is the one that works.

## Water in Scripture — The Great Symbol

- **Creation — water at the beginning**
  - "The Spirit of God moved upon the face of the waters" (Gen 1:2)
  - Water is the first substance named in creation after the earth itself
  - Before sun, before vegetation, before animals — waters
  - [→] The Spirit hovers over waters before anything else takes form. Water precedes life in the biblical account, just as water sustains life in physiology.

- **The river from Eden**
  - "A river went out of Eden to water the garden; and from thence it was parted, and became into four heads" (Gen 2:10)
  - The first garden had flowing water at its center
  - The last garden will have the same (Rev 22:1 — "a pure river of water of life")
  - [→] Scripture begins and ends beside a river. The whole story is framed by water.

- **Water as covenant sign**
  - Noah: the flood and the rainbow — water in judgment and mercy
  - Red Sea: water as the boundary between slavery and freedom
  - Jordan: the boundary into promise, and the site of Christ's baptism
  - Naaman's Jordan dip (2 Kings 5): humility at the water
  - [→] God repeatedly stages his key transitions at water. The physical substance becomes the marker of spiritual change.

- **The new birth — water and Spirit**
  - "Except a man be born of water and of the Spirit, he cannot enter into the kingdom of God" (John 3:5)
  - Water and Spirit together — a pattern we have seen (breath and Spirit, Study 6)
  - Baptism is the enacted theology: the old life drowned, the new life rising through water
  - [→] When God chose a sign for the new birth, He chose water. The body that gets baptized is the same body that needs water inside it to function. The symbol and the substance are one.

- **Living water**
  - Christ to the Samaritan woman: "Whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst; but the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life" (John 4:14)
  - Christ on the last day of the feast: "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink.... But this spake he of the Spirit" (John 7:37-39)
  - Isaiah 55:1 — "Ho, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters"
  - Revelation 22:17 — "Whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely"
  - [→] From Isaiah to Revelation, Scripture consistently figures salvation as water offered to the thirsty. The metaphor works because water _actually_ saves thirsty bodies.

[Q] **"If water is just a symbol, why does it matter what I actually drink?"**

> Symbols in Scripture are usually not arbitrary. The symbol fits the substance: water cleanses, sustains, and gives life physically; Christ uses that reality to teach what the Spirit does spiritually. Bread stands for the body of Christ because bread actually feeds. To treat the physical water as trivial is to miss the whole design: the material and the spiritual thing are related, not identical, and the physical one is not optional for bodies that still need to drink.

## What We're Actually Drinking

- **Chronic underhydration is common**
  - Many adults underdrink relative to adequate-intake targets; older adults are especially vulnerable to low-intake dehydration
  - Thirst is a late signal — by the time you're thirsty, you're already running a small deficit
  - Coffee and tea can contribute fluid overall, though caffeine is mildly diuretic at higher doses. Alcohol is dehydrating. Sugary drinks hydrate poorly as a health habit because they deliver sugar with the water.

- **Sugary drinks, sodas, fruit juices**
  - The average American drinks hundreds of calories per day in liquid form — mostly ultra-processed
  - Soda consumption predicts obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease
  - Fruit juices are sugar water with vitamins; whole fruit > fruit juice in nearly every dimension
  - [→] You cannot hydrate with sugar water and call it water. The body still needs the actual thing.

- **Alcohol**
  - Ethanol is a diuretic — it makes you urinate more than you drink
  - Alcohol depresses central nervous system function, impairs judgment, and disrupts sleep architecture (covered more in Study 5 on Temperance)
  - [EGW] Scripture consistently warns: "Wine is a mocker, strong drink is raging: and whosoever is deceived thereby is not wise" (Prov 20:1)

- **Water quality matters**
  - Tap water in many countries is safe and fine — but quality varies
  - Consider a filter (activated carbon for chlorine/VOCs, reverse osmosis for heavy metals) if local water quality is poor
  - Bottled water is not automatically better; microplastics and environmental cost are real concerns

## Practical Application

- **Baseline — drink water**
  - A reasonable default: ~2-3 liters of water per day for most adults, adjusted up for activity, heat, and body size
  - Start the day with a glass of water before anything else — you wake up mildly dehydrated from a night of breathing out water vapor
  - Don't wait for thirst; thirst is already the deficit signal

- **Between meals, not during**
  - EGW counseled avoiding drinks with meals, especially cold drinks in large amounts, and emphasized thorough chewing so saliva could do its work
  - Modern mainstream sources generally reject blanket fears about water with meals, but a defensible modern application survives: don't wash food down or chug fluids around heavy meals
  - Drink most of your water _between_ meals rather than _with_ them

- **Simple hydrotherapy at home — contrast shower**
  - End your regular shower with 30-60 seconds of cold water, then back to warm, then cold again if tolerated
  - Start gentle (cool, not shocking) and progress as comfortable
  - Benefits: circulation, mood, alertness, possible immune modulation

- **Regular heat exposure — sauna or hot bath**
  - If you have access, a 15-20 minute sauna session 2-3× per week is well-supported by the Finnish data
  - A long hot bath is a reasonable substitute if sauna is unavailable — similar cardiovascular effects at lower peak intensity
  - Follow heat with a cool rinse for contrast benefits

- **Fomentations and hot-cold therapy during illness**
  - Traditional fomentations (alternating hot and cold compresses) may support comfort, circulation, and warmth during respiratory illness; the evidence is more mechanistic and traditional than definitive clinical-outcome proof
  - Simple home protocol: hot towel to chest/back for 3-5 min, then brief cold — repeat 3-5 times — for respiratory comfort and circulation
  - [→] Cheap. Non-pharmaceutical. Physiologically reasonable. Not a cure, but a traditional supportive measure worth keeping in the toolkit.

- **Use wisdom with heat and cold**
  - Sauna, hot baths, and cold exposure are not benign for everyone
  - Avoid or adapt with: unstable cardiac disease, uncontrolled hypertension, history of syncope, pregnancy, neuropathy, frailty, recent alcohol use
  - Start gentle, progress slowly, and stop if something feels wrong
  - On hydration: heavy sweating, endurance exercise, or heat exposure calls for electrolytes, not just plain water; do not force large volumes of plain water (hyponatremia is real, especially in endurance athletes)

- **Hydrate your environment**
  - Dry indoor air (especially in winter) worsens dehydration through the lungs and skin
  - Humidifiers, houseplants, and ventilation adjustment all help

[Aside] Dr. Roger Seheult, a board-certified Adventist pulmonologist, has become one of the more widely-known advocates for hydrotherapy in mainstream medicine — citing the Finnish sauna studies, the Buijze cold shower RCT, and his own ICU experience. His observation: the old Adventist hydrotherapy traditions are consistently validated when tested under modern methodology. The counsel keeps outlasting the skepticism.

## The Verdict — Channel or Obstruction

- **Every glass either hydrates or substitutes**
  - Water hydrates. Sugar water, alcohol, and caffeine-heavy drinks take water from the bank while depositing other things
  - Chronic mild dehydration is not dramatic; it is cumulative — headache, fatigue, fog, mood dip, constipation, stiffer joints, duller brain
  - Over years it compounds into stiffer tissues, slower recovery, and a brain running on reduced margin

- **Hot and cold water, used wisely, is therapy**
  - The old Adventist tradition was right; the Finnish data confirms it
  - Most of these tools are free, require no equipment, and have been validated by independent research
  - [→] We have a two-century head start on rediscovering what mainstream medicine is now putting into wellness protocols

- **The chain**
  - Water → blood, lymph, joints, every tissue → functioning brain → capacity to behold (2 Cor 3:18) → Spirit works through a less-hindered instrument → image restored
  - Break the chain at the glass and every downstream step is harder
  - [→] Symbol and substance fit each other. The water God offers the thirsty soul passes through a body that needs actual water. Care for both.
