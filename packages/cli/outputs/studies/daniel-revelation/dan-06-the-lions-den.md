# Daniel 6 — The Lions' Den

> "Now when Daniel knew that the writing was signed, he went into his house; and his windows being open in his chamber toward Jerusalem, he kneeled upon his knees three times a day, and prayed, and gave thanks before his God, as he did aforetime." — Dan 6:10

## Where this chapter sits

- **Dan 3** — under Babylon. A worship-decree _demands_ an act of false worship (bow before the image) on penalty of fire. Three Hebrew youths refuse and are delivered.
- **Dan 6** — under Medo-Persia. A worship-decree _prohibits_ an act of true worship (prayer to God) on penalty of lions. One Hebrew elder refuses and is delivered.
- Together: the canonical pair for _worship-under-state-decree_. Dan 3 is the _commission_ version (do this idolatrous thing); Dan 6 is the _omission_ version (do not do this faithful thing). Rev 13 will run both moves at end-time scale.

> [⟲] **Worship axis (link 3).** Dan 3 demanded false worship. Dan 6 prohibits true worship. The two legal forms exhaust the worship-coercion repertoire. Rev 13 deploys both — image-worship demanded (Rev 13:14-15), buy-and-sell privilege withheld from those who refuse (Rev 13:17). Dan 3 + Dan 6 = Rev 13's full toolkit.

> [⟲] **Remnant / Seal thread (link 2).** Dan 1 set the diet pattern _behind a closed door_. Dan 6 sets the worship pattern _behind an open window_. The remnant in Daniel is consistently a person whose private practice is fixed before the public test arrives.

## Reading the chapter

The chapter is structured as a deliberate counterpart to Dan 3. The same plot beats run in nearly identical sequence under a different empire and a different penalty:

| Beat                                | Dan 3 (Babylon)                                   | Dan 6 (Medo-Persia)                                        |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| Faithful elevated                   | Three Hebrews promoted (Dan 2:49)                 | Daniel preferred above the presidents (Dan 6:3)            |
| Royal decree of worship coercion    | Image + bow + fire (Dan 3:1-6)                    | Prayer-only-to-king + 30 days + lions (Dan 6:7-9)          |
| Faithful refusal                    | "We will not bow" (Dan 3:18)                      | Daniel kneels three times a day, _as before_ (Dan 6:10)    |
| Accusation by rivals                | "Certain Chaldeans... accused the Jews" (Dan 3:8) | "These men assembled, and found Daniel praying" (Dan 6:11) |
| Reluctant king                      | Nebuchadnezzar fury (Dan 3:13, 19)                | Darius "sore displeased with himself" (Dan 6:14)           |
| Sealed in the death chamber         | Bound and cast into furnace (Dan 3:21)            | Stone laid, signet sealed (Dan 6:17)                       |
| Divine deliverance through an angel | Fourth man "like the Son of God" (Dan 3:25)       | "My God hath sent his angel" (Dan 6:22)                    |
| Pagan king's decree honoring God    | Counter-decree against blasphemy (Dan 3:29)       | "Tremble and fear before the God of Daniel" (Dan 6:26)     |
| Faithful promoted                   | Promoted in Babylon (Dan 3:30)                    | Daniel prospered under Darius and Cyrus (Dan 6:28)         |

- The chapters are _intentionally paired_. The book of Daniel is not telling two separate stories — it is establishing one pattern, twice, under two different empires.
- [→] When Scripture repeats a pattern with deliberate variation, it is teaching not just the event but the _shape_. Dan 3 + Dan 6 hand the church a portable picture of _worship under state coercion_, in two forms (positive demand, negative prohibition), with one outcome (deliverance, vindication, royal confession).

## Dan 6:1-3 — Daniel preferred

> "It pleased Darius to set over the kingdom an hundred and twenty princes, which should be over the whole kingdom; And over these three presidents; of whom Daniel was first... Then this Daniel was preferred above the presidents and princes, because an excellent spirit was in him; and the king thought to set him over the whole realm" (Dan 6:1-3)

- The empire has changed. Babylon → Medo-Persia (Dan 5:31). The administrative structure has changed. Daniel — who has been on the inner court since Dan 2 (around 603 BC) — is now over 80 years old. He has served four kings.
- "An excellent spirit was in him" — the same phrase used of him in Dan 5:12. Daniel's reputation crosses the regime change. Faithfulness has its own portability.
- "The king thought to set him over the whole realm" — Darius is preparing to put a Jewish exile _over the whole Persian administration_. The decree that follows is structurally a _coup attempt against Daniel's promotion_.
- [→] The persecution of Dan 6 is not religious in the first instance. It is _political envy_ dressed up as religious legality. Read Rev 13:15-17 the same way: the buy-and-sell penalty looks religious in form but is enforced by economic coercion. Persecutions hide their motives.

## Dan 6:4-5 — The trap is laid

> "Then the presidents and princes sought to find occasion against Daniel concerning the kingdom; but they could find none occasion nor fault; forasmuch as he was faithful, neither was there any error or fault found in him. Then said these men, We shall not find any occasion against this Daniel, except we find it against him concerning the law of his God" (Dan 6:4-5)

- "They could find none occasion nor fault... neither was there any error or fault found in him." Daniel's eight decades of public administration have produced no auditable scandal. The empire's anti-corruption investigators _cannot find anything_. This is the high-water mark of biblical character description.
- "Except we find it against him concerning the law of his God" — they will need to _manufacture_ the offense, and the only category large enough to manufacture an offense is religion. The conspirators name Daniel's _practice_ as the only available wedge.
- [→] If you cannot be successfully accused except on religious grounds, the world has run out of legitimate accusations against you. Daniel does not give the conspirators _anything else_. The pastoral implication: spend the next forty years making this true of yourself.

> [Q] If your enemies wanted to find a charge against you, would they have to invent a religious one — or could they reach for something else?

## Dan 6:6-9 — The decree

> "Then these presidents and princes assembled together to the king... All the presidents of the kingdom, the governors, and the princes, the counsellors, and the captains, have consulted together to establish a royal statute, and to make a firm decree, that whosoever shall ask a petition of any God or man for thirty days, save of thee, O king, he shall be cast into the den of lions. Now, O king, establish the decree, and sign the writing, that it be not changed, according to the law of the Medes and Persians, which altereth not. Wherefore king Darius signed the writing and the decree" (Dan 6:6-9)

- "All the presidents of the kingdom" — the conspirators _lie_ (the third president, Daniel, was not consulted). The decree is presented as unanimous when it is not.
- "Of any God or man for thirty days, save of thee, O king" — for thirty days, all petitions must go through Darius. This is a _temporary_ measure. The conspirators are not asking the king to be worshiped forever; they are asking for a 30-day religious shutdown. Compare the modern sermon-from-the-text: the test does not have to be permanent to be fatal. _Thirty days of compliance_ is enough to break a faithful person — _if the faithful person calculates compliance_.
- "Cast into the den of lions" — the Persian penalty. Where Babylon used the furnace (Dan 3), Persia uses the den. Both are state-controlled instruments of execution. The penalty form changes; the worship test is the same.
- "The law of the Medes and Persians, which altereth not" — Persian legal theory held that a royal decree was effectively unrevocable (compare Esther 1:19, 8:8). The conspirators have not just trapped Daniel; they have trapped _the king_.
- [→] Worship-coercion legislation works best when (a) it is _temporary in form_, (b) it claims _unanimity_, and (c) it is locked behind _legal irreversibility_. Dan 6 names all three mechanisms in advance. The reader who watches end-time legislation should watch for the same shape.

## Dan 6:10 — The open window

> "Now when Daniel knew that the writing was signed, he went into his house; and his windows being open in his chamber toward Jerusalem, he kneeled upon his knees three times a day, and prayed, and gave thanks before his God, as he did aforetime" (Dan 6:10)

> [KEY🔑] **The steady habit is the unbreakable line.** "He kneeled upon his knees three times a day, and prayed, and gave thanks before his God, _as he did aforetime_." Daniel did not start praying because of the decree; he did not stop praying because of the decree. The decree's effect on his practice was zero. The settled habit predates the test.

- "Knew that the writing was signed" — Daniel makes no political effort to overturn the decree. He does not seek an exemption. He does not write a counter-petition. He continues his settled practice.
- "Windows being open in his chamber toward Jerusalem" — this is Solomon's instruction at the temple dedication: when Israel is in exile, "if they pray toward their land which thou gavest unto their fathers, and toward the city which thou hast chosen, and toward the house... then hear thou their prayer" (1 Kings 8:48-49). Daniel is praying _the prayer the temple liturgy prescribed for exiles_. The window is not defiance; it is fidelity.
- "Three times a day" — Ps 55:17: "Evening, and morning, and at noon, will I pray, and cry aloud: and he shall hear my voice." The practice is canonical. Compare Acts 3:1, where Peter and John go up to the temple "at the hour of prayer, being the ninth hour."
- "As he did aforetime" — the chapter's central phrase. The faithful person is _boring_ in the precise sense that the world's coercion expects an _innovation_ and finds only continuity.
- [→] In the worship test, what saves you is what you were already doing before the test. Improvised faithfulness almost always loses to improvised compliance. The _aforetime_ habit is the unbreakable thing.

> [Q] What is your "aforetime"? If a 30-day prohibition were signed against your private worship tomorrow, what would the test reveal about the strength of your habit?

## Dan 6:11-13 — The accusation

> "Then these men assembled, and found Daniel praying and making supplication before his God. Then they came near, and spake before the king concerning the king's decree... Then answered they and said before the king, That Daniel, which is of the children of the captivity of Judah, regardeth not thee, O king, nor the decree that thou hast signed, but maketh his petition three times a day" (Dan 6:11-13)

- "Children of the captivity of Judah" — they invoke Daniel's _immigrant status_ as part of the indictment. Religious persecution in Dan 6 is wrapped in xenophobia. Compare Haman's accusation in Esth 3:8.
- "Three times a day" — the _frequency_ is the offense. They do not say "Daniel prayed once." They specify the count, because the chapter is about Daniel's _aforetime_ habit, not about a single act.
- [→] When the world starts counting the regularity of your worship as evidence of disloyalty, you are not the one losing the argument. You are the one _being witnessed_.

## Dan 6:14-17 — The king sealed against himself

> "Then the king, when he heard these words, was sore displeased with himself, and set his heart on Daniel to deliver him: and he laboured till the going down of the sun to deliver him... Then the king commanded, and they brought Daniel, and cast him into the den of lions. Now the king spake and said unto Daniel, Thy God whom thou servest continually, he will deliver thee. And a stone was brought, and laid upon the mouth of the den; and the king sealed it with his own signet, and with the signet of his lords; that the purpose might not be changed concerning Daniel" (Dan 6:14, 16-17)

- "Sore displeased with himself" — Darius is the willing victim of his own decree. He has been _trapped by the conspirators' use of his own legal theology_. The chapter does not portray him as malicious; it portrays him as _legally outflanked_.
- "Laboured till the going down of the sun" — the king tries every legal mechanism. Pilate runs the same script in John 18-19. _The Persian Pilate._ The reluctant ruler who knows the prisoner is innocent and cannot find a way to release him.
- "Thy God whom thou servest continually, he will deliver thee" — the king himself articulates Daniel's theology as the prisoner is being lowered into the den.
- "A stone was brought... and the king sealed it with his own signet" — the chapter's most resonant detail. A stone seals the chamber of the innocent man. The signet of authority guarantees that no one will interfere. Compare Matt 27:60, 66: "and rolled a great stone to the door of the sepulchre... So they went, and made the sepulchre sure, sealing the stone, and setting a watch."

> [CTRF] **Pair (link 1): the lions' den (Dan 6) vs. Christ's tomb (Matt 27).** Both close with a stone and an official seal. Both contain a man condemned by manufactured charges. Both are opened in the morning to an impossible reversal the system cannot account for: Daniel alive in the den; Christ absent from the tomb. The stone over Daniel's den is the canonical anticipation of the stone at the tomb. The Pilate-figure of Dan 6 is the unwilling administrator; the Pilate of John 18 is the same shape later.

## Dan 6:18-20 — Darius's night

> "Then the king went to his palace, and passed the night fasting: neither were instruments of musick brought before him: and his sleep went from him. Then the king arose very early in the morning, and went in haste unto the den of lions. And when he came to the den, he cried with a lamentable voice unto Daniel: and the king spake and said to Daniel, O Daniel, servant of the living God, is thy God, whom thou servest continually, able to deliver thee from the lions?" (Dan 6:18-20)

- "Passed the night fasting: neither were instruments of musick brought before him" — exactly the inversion of Dan 5's banquet. Belshazzar feasted with music while the writing wrote; Darius fasts in silence while Daniel prays in the den. The two kings' nights are mirror images.
- "Very early in the morning" — first light. Compare John 20:1: "the first day of the week cometh Mary Magdalene early, when it was yet dark, unto the sepulchre."
- "Servant of the living God" — Darius's title for Daniel anticipates the same vocabulary Rev 7:3 ("servants of our God") and Rev 22:3 ("his servants shall serve him") will use for the redeemed.
- [→] The world's reluctant administrators sometimes confess Christian theology better than the average professing Christian. Darius's night-fast for a Jew in a den is more devout than Belshazzar's banquet had been. God reaches _through_ the reluctance to a confession.

## Dan 6:21-23 — Innocency was found

> "Then said Daniel unto the king, O king, live for ever. My God hath sent his angel, and hath shut the lions' mouths, that they have not hurt me: forasmuch as before him innocency was found in me; and also before thee, O king, have I done no hurt. Then was the king exceeding glad for him, and commanded that they should take Daniel up out of the den. So Daniel was taken up out of the den, and no manner of hurt was found upon him, because he believed in his God" (Dan 6:21-23)

- "My God hath sent his angel, and hath shut the lions' mouths" — the same hand that walked in the fire with the three (Dan 3:25) shut the lions' mouths around the one. Heb 11:33-34 names both: "Who through faith... stopped the mouths of lions, quenched the violence of fire."
- "Innocency was found in me" — the courtroom vocabulary. Daniel was tried twice — once by the empire (which found nothing, Dan 6:4), once by God (which found innocency). The two verdicts agree.
- "Because he believed in his God" — the Aramaic verb (_ʾāman_, the same root the Hebrew Bible uses for "believe / faithful / sure") carries the sense of _entrusting oneself_. Daniel entrusted himself to God. Faith is the _because_ of the deliverance, not the cause of it. God delivers; faith holds the door open.
- [→] If a court tries you on the surface and finds nothing, then a court tries you in private and finds nothing, the consistency is the testimony. Daniel's life passes inspection at both levels.

## Dan 6:24 — The accusers' end

> "And the king commanded, and they brought those men which had accused Daniel, and they cast them into the den of lions, them, their children, and their wives; and the lions had the mastery of them, and brake all their bones in pieces or ever they came at the bottom of the den" (Dan 6:24)

- "The lions had the mastery of them... or ever they came at the bottom of the den" — the lions who had not touched Daniel had been hungry the whole time. The shut-mouth was specific, not general. God's restraint had been targeted at the one He had named.
- The execution of the accusers' families is a Persian state act, not a moral commendation by the chapter. Persian law extended capital penalties to relatives in cases of conspiracy. The chapter records the _fact_ without theologizing the practice.
- [→] The shape of the chapter's reversal — the pit prepared for the righteous becomes the pit of the unrighteous — is the canonical pattern of Esth 7 (Haman's gallows) and Ps 7:15-16 ("He made a pit, and digged it, and is fallen into the ditch which he made").

## Dan 6:25-27 — The third pagan-king decree of the book

> "Then king Darius wrote unto all people, nations, and languages, that dwell in all the earth; Peace be multiplied unto you. I make a decree, That in every dominion of my kingdom men tremble and fear before the God of Daniel: for he is the living God, and stedfast for ever, and his kingdom that which shall not be destroyed, and his dominion shall be even unto the end. He delivereth and rescueth, and he worketh signs and wonders in heaven and in earth, who hath delivered Daniel from the power of the lions" (Dan 6:25-27)

- "Unto all people, nations, and languages, that dwell in all the earth" — the same imperial-decree formula Nebuchadnezzar used in Dan 4:1. _Three_ pagan-king decrees in Daniel honor the most High: Dan 3:29 (Nebuchadnezzar's first), Dan 4:1-3 (Nebuchadnezzar's second, the published testimony), Dan 6:25-27 (Darius). The book stages a deliberate triad of pagan-state confession.
- "His kingdom that which shall not be destroyed" — Darius is now using _Dan 2:44_ language. The Persian king has read the same prophecy as the Babylonian king, and confesses the same kingdom.
- [→] The empire is — chapter by chapter — having to bear witness to a kingdom that will outlast it. Babylon confessed in Dan 4. Persia confesses in Dan 6. The pattern says: God will _get the testimony out of the persecutor's mouth_.

## Dan 6:28 — The reign of Cyrus

> "So this Daniel prospered in the reign of Darius, and in the reign of Cyrus the Persian" (Dan 6:28)

- "In the reign of Cyrus the Persian" — the chapter ends with the name that Isa 44:28 - 45:1 had announced 150 years earlier ("That saith of Cyrus, He is my shepherd, and shall perform all my pleasure: even saying to Jerusalem, Thou shalt be built; and to the temple, Thy foundation shall be laid... Thus saith the LORD to his anointed, to Cyrus"). The book of Daniel reaches the threshold of the return from exile.
- Daniel's career outlasts the empire that captured him. He arrives in Babylon as a teenage exile (605 BC), is still in office when Babylon falls (539 BC), and is still serving as Cyrus issues the decree of return (Ezr 1:1-4). One faithful man's career spans the entire span between the deportation and the return.
- [→] Faithfulness in a long career is a different kind of testimony than faithfulness in a single moment. Dan 3 was a moment. Dan 6 is a life. The remnant in Daniel is shown both as _the three faithful_ (Dan 3) and _the one faithful elder_ (Dan 6). Both are the canon's pictures of how God's people stand.

## Dan 6 and Rev 13's prohibition-version of the worship test — `[CTRF]`

> [CTRF] **Pair (link 2): Dan 6 (a 30-day prohibition of true worship) vs. Rev 13 (an end-time exclusion of those who refuse the false worship).** Dan 3 was the _commission_ version of Rev 13 (do this idolatrous thing). Dan 6 is the _omission_ version (do not do this faithful thing). Rev 13 deploys both.

| Dan 6 (Persia, c. 539 BC)                               | Rev 13 (end-time)                                                                       |
| ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 30-day decree against petitioning any god but the king  | Decree forbidding buy/sell to those who refuse the mark (Rev 13:17)                     |
| Conspirators trap the king with his own legal authority | Earth-beast deceives, makes an image, gives it breath (Rev 13:11-15)                    |
| Penalty: lions' den                                     | Penalty: economic exclusion + death (Rev 13:15, 17)                                     |
| One faithful elder kneels by his open window            | "Patience and faith of the saints" (Rev 13:10); 144,000 stand (Rev 14:1)                |
| God shuts the lions' mouths                             | Sea of glass mingled with fire — victors over the beast (Rev 15:2)                      |
| King's confession to all people, nations, languages     | Three angels' message to "every nation, and kindred, and tongue, and people" (Rev 14:6) |

- Where Dan 3 prefigures Rev 13's _demanded_ worship of the image, Dan 6 prefigures Rev 13's _forbidden_ worship of God.
- [→] Read both prototypes together. The end-time test is not just "they will make us do something idolatrous" (Dan 3 → Rev 13:14-15); it is also "they will make us _stop_ doing what we have always done" (Dan 6 → Rev 13:17). Settled private practice (Dan 1) and settled public stand (Dan 3) and settled lifelong habit (Dan 6) — three layers of faithfulness, all operative in the end-time test.

## Dan 6 in the great-controversy — Acts 5:29 lens

> "We ought to obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29)

- The apostles' language in Acts 5:29 is the Dan 6 logic stated as principle. When the legal authority of the state requires the cessation of true worship, the believer's allegiance to God overrides the believer's submission to the state. The principle is not anarchism (Daniel served Persia faithfully under every other directive); it is _bounded submission_.
- Rom 13:1-7's "be subject unto the higher powers" is the same theology in another mode: civil authority is honored under God, and only refused at the _one_ point where it claims what belongs to God.
- [→] Daniel's politics were _generously submissive_ and _narrowly resistant_. He did everything Persia asked except the one thing God forbade. The pattern is the church's permanent civil ethic.

## Key Takeaways

- Dan 6 is Dan 3's sister chapter — the prohibition-form of the worship test, under a new empire, with the same shape of deliverance
- The settled habit (Dan 6:10, "as he did aforetime") is the unbreakable line — improvised faithfulness rarely outlasts a manufactured legal trap
- The window toward Jerusalem is Solomon's exilic prayer instruction (1 Kings 8:48-49), not improvised defiance
- Persian legal theory ("the law of the Medes and Persians, which altereth not") is the chapter's mechanism — Darius is trapped by his own irrevocability
- The stone-and-signet over the den anticipates the stone-and-seal of Christ's tomb (Matt 27:60, 66)
- Three pagan-king decrees in Daniel publicly honor the most High (Dan 3:29; 4:1-3; 6:25-27) — the empire keeps having to bear witness
- Dan 1 + Dan 3 + Dan 6 establish three layers of faithfulness — private practice, public stand, lifelong habit — all operative in Rev 13's end-time test
- Acts 5:29's "obey God rather than men" is Dan 6 stated as principle; Rom 13's submission and Dan 6's refusal are not in tension — they are the same ethic in two modes
