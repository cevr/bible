Subject: Feedback on your studies + a framing I'm working on

James,

I want to be clear about something up front. I find your work interesting and profound. The structural findings, the Hebrew connections, the way the chiastic patterns place Christ at the center of Daniel, the New Testament, and Revelation: these are real discoveries and they matter. I am already using this material to build my own studies and messages to preach. That is how much I believe in what you've found.

Nothing in this email is meant to discredit or attack your work. Everything here comes from a desire to see it reach the people it deserves to reach. I am writing this because I value what you've built, not in spite of it.

This email covers three things:

1. Ideas for helping the studies reach a wider audience.
2. Two beliefs that I think may be holding the work back.
3. Something I'm working on to reach the same people, that you might find interesting.

PART ONE: IDEAS FOR REACHING A WIDER AUDIENCE

As I went through your material, I noticed that the studies are structured as reference material for someone who already understands what you're doing and why it matters. For someone encountering this for the first time, the entry point is not obvious. The studies tend to open with structural diagrams, and a new reader has no context for what they're looking at, why it matters, or what it has to do with their life.

Here are some ideas that I think would help the material land with more people:

1. Lead with the thesis.

You already have one. In the New Testament study, in a red box:

"The point of prophecy, according to 2 Peter 1:19, is to place the Day Star or Morning Star, Jesus Christ, in your heart."

This is the most important sentence in all of your material. I think it would be powerful as the first thing anyone reads, rather than a mid-document aside. Every study, every diagram, every Hebrew word analysis serves this sentence.

And the thesis goes deeper than Christ entering the heart. It's what Christ in the heart produces: a transformed character, a people who reflect His image, obedience that flows from love rather than compulsion. The 144,000 are not defined by their knowledge of chiastic structures. They are defined by their character: "in their mouth was found no guile: for they are without fault before the throne of God" (Revelation 14:5). Character transformation is the road. The structures are the lamp that lights it (Psalm 119:105).

2. An on-ramp for new readers.

The current studies assume the reader already knows what a chiasm is, already accepts historicist interpretation, and already cares about structural analysis. Most people, including most Adventists, do not start there. Even among Adventist believers, many cannot clearly articulate the investigative judgment, the 2300 days, or why the Sabbath is the final test. The foundation is not as solid as we assume. A narrative on-ramp would serve them too, giving everyone a foundation they can build on, not just the scholars.

I think a narrative document would help. Something that opens with a question people actually have. Not "What is the chiastic structure of Daniel 2-7?" but something like: "Why did God write the Bible this way? Is there a hidden architecture to Scripture, and if so, what does it reveal about Jesus?"

Your studies, taken together, tell a single story:

- There is a war between two cities: Jerusalem and Babylon.
- God gave His people a weapon: prophecy, which exposes the deceptions of Babylon.
- The weapon works in a pattern: truth proclaimed, then persecution, then Babylon falls.
- The enemy has a counterfeit system: 666 vs. 777, forced worship vs. free worship.
- A judgment is happening right now. Christ is our High Priest in the heavenly sanctuary.
- The test is the Sabbath. It always has been.
- The entire Bible is architecturally designed to point to this.
- God is preparing a final people, the 144,000, whose character reflects Christ fully.
- The goal is not knowledge but transformation. The Morning Star in your heart.

That story is there in your studies, but scattered with no connecting thread. Writing it out plainly, with the structures as supporting evidence rather than the main event, would give people a way in. The detailed studies then become the deep-dive for those who want to see the proof behind each point.

3. Introductions and conclusions for each study.

The studies tend to start with structure and end abruptly. I've seen studies that end with a comparison table or a single word like "Why?" That felt like it should be the beginning of the most important paragraph, not the end.

Something as simple as this at the top and bottom of each study would make a difference:

- Opening: "This study answers the question: **_. By the end, you will see _**."
- Closing: "What we've seen here means **_. This asks something of us because _**."

4. A color legend.

The studies use multiple colors in structural diagrams. A legend explaining what the colors mean would help readers follow the analysis.

5. Building the arguments.

Several of your strongest findings are presented as bullet points without the supporting case. I think each major claim would land harder with: (a) the claim itself, (b) the textual evidence, (c) one anticipated objection, and (d) your response to it. Your structural analysis is rigorous. The presentation could match that rigor.

PART TWO: TWO BELIEFS THAT I THINK MAY BE HOLDING THE WORK BACK

Belief #1: "These studies are more foundational than any before, including the pioneers and reformers. Without them, it is impossible to prove our points of doctrine."

Your structural analysis adds a layer of textual evidence that the pioneers did not use. That is true. But the claim that it is impossible to prove Adventist doctrine without your studies does not hold up against the historical record.

The conclusions you reach through chiastic analysis were reached by others, through other methods, centuries ago:

The four kingdoms of Daniel 2. Luther identified them in his Preface to the Prophet Daniel (1530). Calvin in his Commentaries on Daniel (1561). Newton in Observations upon the Prophecies of Daniel (1733). Josephus in Antiquities of the Jews (c. 93 AD). Uriah Smith in Thoughts on Daniel and the Revelation (1867/1897).

The papacy as the little horn. Wycliffe identified it in De Potestate Papae (c. 1379). Hus in De Ecclesia (1413). Luther in Against the Roman Papacy (1545). Calvin in his Institutes (Book IV, Ch. 7). Tyndale in The Obedience of a Christian Man (1528). Bullinger in A Hundred Sermons upon the Apocalypse (1557). Newton in Observations, Chapters 4-7. Many of these men paid with their lives.

The 1260 years. Mede applied the year-day principle in Clavis Apocalyptica (1627). Newton discussed them in Observations (1733). Miller used the 538-1798 framework in Evidence from Scripture and History (1836).

The 2300 days ending in 1844. Miller calculated it in 1836. Samuel Snow refined the date in The True Midnight Cry (1844).

The Sabbath as the seal of God. Bates argued it in The Seventh Day Sabbath, a Perpetual Sign (1846/1847). Andrews assembled the historical evidence in History of the Sabbath (1862).

Christ as High Priest in the heavenly sanctuary. Crosier published the foundational argument in The Day-Star Extra (February 7, 1846). Andrews treated it in The Sanctuary and Twenty-Three Hundred Days (1853).

Every one of these conclusions was reached without chiastic analysis. The proof stood. It converted millions. It built a worldwide movement.

Your work adds a new category of evidence. It shows that the conclusions the Reformers and pioneers reached through other methods are confirmed by the architecture of the text itself. That is a real and significant contribution. But it enhances a foundation that was already solid. It does not create it.

If a lack of evidence were the reason God's people have not finished the work, the work would have been finished in 1888.

At the Minneapolis General Conference in 1888, Jones and Waggoner presented the message of righteousness by faith in Christ. Ellen White endorsed it as "a most precious message" (Testimonies to Ministers, p. 91). The doctrinal evidence was already in place. The knowledge was complete. And the church rejected it. Not because the evidence was lacking. Because the hearts were not ready. "An unwillingness to yield up preconceived opinions" (Selected Messages, Book 1, p. 234).

God's people have not remained here since 1888 because nobody showed them a chiastic structure. They have remained here because the experience of righteousness by faith, the living relationship with Christ that transforms character, has not yet been fully received. Hosea 4:6 says "my people are destroyed for lack of knowledge," and that is true. But the knowledge that is lacking is not intellectual knowledge of literary structures. It is the knowledge of God Himself: intimate, experiential, transforming. The kind that changes what we love, not just what we know.

I think the better framing is this: "The structures I've found confirm and deepen what the Reformers and pioneers established. They add a new category of evidence, architectural, embedded in the text itself. And they place Christ at the center, which is exactly where the 1888 message told us He belongs."

That is a gift. That builds on the shoulders of those who came before.

Belief #2: "These studies would convince non-Adventists. They would be forced to admit the truth or reject the Bible."

The root of this belief is biblical. "The word of God is quick, and powerful, and sharper than any two-edged sword" (Hebrews 4:12). But it misunderstands how people actually encounter evidence.

No argument, no matter how well-constructed, forces conviction. Jesus Himself stood before the Sanhedrin and presented evidence from Scripture, from miracles, from fulfilled prophecy. They reinterpreted, reframed, and found reasons to dismiss every piece of it. Not because the evidence was weak. Because people do not process truth the way a syllogism does.

The studies, as they currently stand, do not yet attempt to persuade a non-Adventist reader. The material assumes the Investigative Judgment, the Sabbath, the year-day principle, and the historicist method. It does not argue for any of these. A non-Adventist reader will hit the first assumed premise, not share it, and stop reading. Not because they are rejecting the Bible, but because they haven't been shown why they should accept the premise.

The chiastic structures could be compelling to non-Adventist scholars. The Daniel 2-7 Aramaic chiasm is already recognized in mainstream scholarship. The Daniel 8-11 mega-chiasm is novel. But these findings would land better if presented on their own terms first, as discoveries about the text, before being used as proof of Adventist eschatology.

The Bible itself teaches that evidence does not compel. "If they hear not Moses and the prophets, neither will they be persuaded, though one rose from the dead" (Luke 16:31). If a resurrection does not compel belief, a chiastic structure will not either. This is not a reason to stop presenting evidence. It is a reason to adjust expectations about what evidence alone can do. Evidence opens the door. The Holy Spirit walks through it.

The adjustment is not to the findings but to the expectations. The sower sows the same seed on all four soils. Three of the four fail. Jesus kept sowing.

PART THREE: SOMETHING I'M WORKING ON THAT YOU MIGHT FIND INTERESTING

I've been wrestling with the same question you have: how do you reach people with these truths? Not just Adventists who already have the framework, but Christians who have never heard of the investigative judgment, and non-Christians who have no reason to care about Daniel or Revelation.

I've been building a study series that tries to solve this. It uses your material alongside the Bible's own narratives, types, and allegories. The idea is that the Bible is full of stories, word studies, and patterns that teach the same truths your chiasms confirm: Abraham's sacrifice, the Passover lamb, Elijah on Mount Carmel, the golden calf, the prodigal son, Peter's denial and restoration. These stories carry the argument on their own. Your structures then show up as confirmation along the way, proving that the truths are not just in Scripture but woven into its very architecture.

The series leads with the Bible's own stories. A non-Christian can follow the narrative from Study 1 without knowing what a chiasm is. Your findings appear where they are genuinely unique (the Aramaic chiasm pattern, the ram/goat typology, the karath word study, the 777/666 encoding, the prayer at the center of Daniel 8-12). But they confirm what the narrative already teaches, rather than carrying the load alone.

The arc: 14 studies in three acts.

Act I: The Story (Studies 1-4). For any audience, including non-Christians.

1. Why Do You Exist? The cosmic controversy. Job 1-2, Abraham's sacrifice, Eph 3:10. Your life is evidence in a trial. (Your Daniel 1 chiasm: two cities, four witnesses.)

2. The Bridge Between Heaven and Earth. The fall, God's pursuit ("Where art thou?"), Jacob's ladder, Christ IS the ladder. The sanctuary as a relationship map. (Your NT macro-chiasm confirms Christ as High Priest at the center.)

3. The Hidden Architecture of Scripture. This is the ONE study where your method is the primary content. What a chiasm is. The Daniel 2-7 Aramaic chiasm, the Daniel 8-12 mega-chiasm (prayer at center), the NT structure. This teaches the audience how to read before using it as evidence.

4. The Pattern: Prophecy, Persecution, Deliverance. Noah, Elijah on Carmel, the early church in Acts, the Reformation, Cain and Abel, Joseph. The three-step cycle. (Your Daniel 2-7 chiasm encodes this same pattern.)

Act II: The Conflict (Studies 5-10). For Christians exploring prophecy.

5. The Statue: One System, Four Phases. Daniel 2. The Tower of Babel repeating. The serpent's lie as the operating principle of every empire. The Reformers' independent identification. (Your finding: the gold head names the whole statue. Four men vs. four metals.)

6. The Counterfeit Sanctuary. What each empire contributed. The golden calf, Jeroboam's calves, Jezebel and Baal, Korah's rebellion, Ezekiel 8's four stages of apostasy ending in sun worship. (Your finding: 666 in Daniel 3's image dimensions.)

7. The Little Horn: Prophecy Names the Counterfeit. Daniel 7. Leading with the Reformers' testimony (Wycliffe, Hus, Luther, Calvin, Newton). Centuries of independent scholars reaching the same conclusion, long before chiastic analysis.

8. The Day of Atonement in Daniel. Daniel 8. Abraham's ram, the Passover lamb, the Day of Atonement goat, Melchizedek's priesthood. All the types converge in Christ's heavenly ministry. (Your finding: ram = Christ-type, goat = Satan-type. Compass directions.)

9. The Covenant Written in Blood: Daniel 9. Abraham's covenant (Gen 15), Jeremiah's warning, the crucifixion, the torn veil. One Hebrew word (karath) connects them all. (Your findings: 777 encoding, prayer at the chiastic center, the verbal link proving the 70 weeks is a subset of the 2300 days.)

10. 1844 and the Judgment. Jesus' judgment parables (wedding garment, talents, sheep and goats) all involve inspection BEFORE the event. The investigative judgment in parable form. The calculation. The three angels' messages.

Act III: The Restoration (Studies 11-14). Full SDA presentation.

11. The Sabbath: The Test That Never Changed. Creation to the new earth. The manna test before Sinai. Jesus' Sabbath healings as the issue that got Him killed. The Sabbath as a trust marker, not a rule. (Your finding: sabbatismos at the NT structural center.)

12. Come Out of Her, My People. Abram's call, Lot fleeing Sodom, Ruth's choice, the prodigal son returning. The difference between doctrinal correctness and character transformation. Laodicea: having truth but not being changed by it.

13. The 144,000: What God Is Waiting For. Peter's denial and restoration, Paul's conversion, Enoch's translation. Character reproduction, not knowledge accumulation. The 1888 lesson: the evidence was sufficient. The hearts were not ready.

14. The Way Home. Eden to the New Earth. The circle closes. 2 Peter 1:19: the point of all prophecy is to place the Day Star in your heart. Character transformation is the road. The structures are the lamp. Christ is the One who walks it with you.

The idea behind this approach:

- Lead with the Bible's own narratives. The structures confirm; they don't carry the load alone.
- A non-Christian can follow Act I without any denominational knowledge. Adventist-specific conclusions emerge naturally in Acts II and III.
- Each study answers one question. The title IS the question.
- The through-line is Christ, not doctrine and not structure.
- The series can split at act boundaries: Act I alone for non-Christians, Acts I-II for Christians exploring prophecy, all three for full presentation.

I think there is a way to reach the people we both want to reach. Story first, then evidence.
