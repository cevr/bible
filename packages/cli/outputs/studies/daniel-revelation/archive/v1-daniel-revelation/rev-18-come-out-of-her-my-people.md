# Revelation 18 — Come Out of Her, My People

**Rev 17 named the system. Rev 18 calls God's people out of it. Another angel comes down from heaven, having great power, and the earth is lightened with his glory. He cries mightily: "Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird." A second voice from heaven follows: "Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues." Then heaven's verdict — reward her double; she said in her heart "I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow"; her plagues come in one day. Three "alas, alas" laments from earth-dwellers — kings, merchants, sailors — describe the smoke of her burning, the merchandise that no man buys, the trade that vanishes in one hour. A mighty angel takes up a stone like a great millstone and casts it into the sea: "Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all." The chapter ends with a final indictment: "in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth." Rev 18 is the Final Loud Cry — earth's last invitation, heaven's final verdict, and Babylon's economic-religious ruin.**

- Genesis 19:15-26 (Lot called out of Sodom — the come-out pattern)
- 2 Corinthians 6:14-18 ("Come out from among them, and be ye separate")

## Before the Verses — Where Rev 18 Sits

Rev 17 unveiled the harlot. Rev 18 announces and executes her fall. The second angel's brief announcement of Rev 14:8 ("Babylon is fallen, is fallen") is now expanded into a 24-verse close-up. **Rev 18 is the Final Loud Cry of the third angel** — the closing-message announcement that calls God's people out of every system Babylon has touched. **Rev 19** then opens with heaven's hallelujah at her destruction. Rev 18 is the bridge between identity (Rev 17) and execution (Rev 19).

- **Two angels speak; two voices speak.** First a mighty angel from heaven with a voice that lights the earth (vv. 1-3); then "another voice from heaven" (v. 4) calling God's people out. **The chapter is structured around announcement and call.**
- **The chapter recycles Jeremiah 50-51 deliberately.** Multiple direct allusions: the doubled "Babylon is fallen, is fallen" (Jer 51:8, Rev 18:2); "habitation of devils... cage of every unclean bird" (Jer 50:39, Rev 18:2); "Come out of her, my people" (Jer 51:45, Rev 18:4); "reward her even as she rewarded you, double unto her double" (Jer 50:29, Rev 18:6); "I sit a queen... shall see no sorrow" (Isa 47:7-8, Rev 18:7); the millstone cast into the river (Jer 51:63-64, Rev 18:21). **Rev 18 is the eschatological version of Jer 50-51's destruction-of-Babylon prophecy.**
- **The chapter has a triple "alas, alas"** structure (vv. 10, 16, 19) — kings, merchants, sailors — each lamenting "that great city" and "in one hour" she is destroyed. **The triple lament structurally mirrors the threefold beatitude on her destruction in v. 20** ("rejoice... heaven, and... holy apostles and prophets").
- **The economic catalog (vv. 12-13) is the pulse of the chapter.** A list of 28 commodities ending with "slaves, and souls of men" — Babylon's merchandise is comprehensive, climaxing in the trafficking of human souls. **The chapter is not just religious; it is also economic, political, and commercial.** The fall touches every domain Babylon has touched.
- **The come-out call is a salvation-call to people still inside Babylon at the close of probation.** Rev 18:4 — "Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins" — implies God still has His people inside the system at the moment of the call. **The Loud Cry is the final ingathering before the door closes** (cf. Rev 15:8 / Rev 22:11).

[KEY🔑] **Rev 18 is the chapter that gives the closing-message its content and its loudest moment.** The "another angel" of v. 1 is the angel of the Loud Cry — a heaven-sent climactic announcement following the three angels' messages of Rev 14:6-12. The earth is lit with his glory; the message is doubled ("Babylon is fallen, is fallen"); the second voice of v. 4 calls God's people out. **Rev 18 is the salvation-side of the seventh-vial chapter.** Rev 16 was the wrath-side (the plagues actually pouring out); Rev 18 is the call-side (every honest soul still inside Babylon hearing "Come out"). The chapter says three things at once: Babylon is fallen — come out of her — heaven has avenged the saints. **The Final Loud Cry is not optional missionary expansion; it is the message Adventism is commissioned to carry, the everlasting gospel of Rev 14:6 reaching its final volume.**

## Rev 18:1 — Another Angel, Earth Lighted with His Glory

> "And after these things I saw another angel come down from heaven, having great power; and the earth was lightened with his glory." (Rev 18:1)

- "**After these things**" — Greek _meta tauta_. **A scene-shift from the Rev 17 angelic exposition** to the Rev 18 announcement. The same Babylon; a new chapter; a new angel.
- "**Another angel come down from heaven**" — Greek _allon angelon katabainonta ek tou ouranou_. **Compare Rev 10:1** ("a mighty angel come down from heaven, clothed with a cloud... his face was as it were the sun") and **Rev 14:6** ("another angel fly in the midst of heaven"). **The Rev 18 angel is the climactic "another angel" of the closing-message sequence.**
- "**Having great power**" — Greek _exousian megalēn_. **Authority-power, not just strength.** The angel comes with delegated divine commission.
- "**The earth was lightened with his glory**" — Greek _hē gē ephōtisthē ek tēs doxēs autou_. **The earth's glory does not light the angel; the angel's glory lights the earth.** Compare Eze 43:2 — "the earth shined with his glory" — at the return of the LORD's glory to the temple. **Rev 18:1's angel carries the same doxa-radiance** — a heaven-sent illumination at the end of probation.
- **This is the Loud Cry.** The Spirit-filled final-warning message of the closing crisis, going to every person still inside Babylon. The "earth lighted" is the global reach of the message; the "great power" is the Spirit's enduement; the "another angel" is the structural successor to the three angels of Rev 14:6-12.

[⟲] **The angel-from-heaven line carries the everlasting gospel into its final volume.** Rev 14:6 (first angel — the everlasting gospel to every nation, kindred, tongue, people). Rev 14:8 (second angel — "Babylon is fallen, is fallen"). Rev 14:9 (third angel — the warning against the mark). **Rev 18:1 — another angel with great power, the earth lightened with his glory.** This is the same gospel, the same Babylon-warning, the same mark-warning, now empowered by the Spirit's final outpouring (the "latter rain" of Joel 2:23-29; James 5:7) for the closing crisis. **The Loud Cry is the three angels' messages amplified by the Spirit's final manifestation.** The volume rises because the time is short.

## Rev 18:2-3 — Babylon Is Fallen, Habitation of Devils

> "And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird. For all nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication, and the kings of the earth have committed fornication with her, and the merchants of the earth are waxed rich through the abundance of her delicacies." (Rev 18:2-3)

- "**Cried mightily with a strong voice**" — Greek _ekraxen ischyra phōnē_. **Same loud-voice motif as Rev 14:7 / Rev 14:9** (the first and third angels' "loud voice" = _phōnē megalē_). **The Loud Cry's volume.**
- "**Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen**" — Greek _epesen epesen Babylōn hē megalē_. **Doubled aorist, identical phrasing to Rev 14:8.** **Rev 14:8 was the prophetic announcement; Rev 18:2 is the announcement at the moment of full execution.** The same words at two phases — heaven's double-witness that the verdict is certain.
- "**Habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird**" — Greek _katoikētērion daimoniōn kai phylakē pantos pneumatos akathartou_. **Direct echo of Isa 13:21-22** (Babylon's desolation — wild beasts and demons inhabit) and **Jer 50:39; 51:37** (Babylon a dwelling-place for jackals and ostriches). **A spiritual moat:** every foul thing has gathered into the abandoned shell. **The "cage of unclean birds"** ties to Mt 13:32's Christian-era warnings about birds sheltering in the kingdom-tree's branches — the system that had compromised the gospel becomes the dwelling-place of every counterfeit spirit.
- "**All nations have drunk of the wine of the wrath of her fornication**" — Greek _ek tou oinou tou thymou tēs porneias autēs_. **The cup of fornication of Rev 17:4 has now spread to "all nations."** Universal compromise; universal stupefaction. The drunkenness of v. 3 is what made the come-out-of-her of v. 4 necessary.
- "**The kings of the earth have committed fornication with her**" — political-religious adultery, repeated from Rev 17:2. **State-church alliance has been the engine of Babylon's reach.**
- "**The merchants of the earth are waxed rich through the abundance of her delicacies**" — Greek _ek tēs dynameōs tou strēnous autēs_. **_Strēnos_** = luxurious living, ostentation, sensual indulgence. **Babylon is also the global commercial empire** that enriches the merchants who supply her appetites. Rev 18 will detail the merchandise in vv. 12-13.

## Rev 18:4-5 — Come Out of Her, My People

> "And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues. For her sins have reached unto heaven, and God hath remembered her iniquities." (Rev 18:4-5)

- "**Another voice from heaven**" — Greek _allēn phōnēn ek tou ouranou_. **A second voice** distinct from the angel of v. 1. The first voice announced; the second calls. **The chapter's first imperative.**
- "**Come out of her, my people**" — Greek _exelthate ho laos mou ex autēs_. **Direct quotation of Jer 51:45** ("My people, go ye out of the midst of her"); **Jer 51:6** ("Flee out of the midst of Babylon"); **Isa 48:20** ("Go ye forth of Babylon"). **The pattern is OT-deep:** every faithful captive must leave the falling city. Lot called out of Sodom (Gen 19:15-26); Israel called out of Babylon under Cyrus (Ezra 1:5; Isa 48:20; Jer 50:8); the early Christians called out of Jerusalem before AD 70 (Lk 21:21). **Rev 18:4 is the eschatological climax of the come-out pattern.**
- "**My people**" — Greek _ho laos mou_. **God still has His people inside Babylon at the moment of this call.** The come-out implies the in-status. Rev 18:4 is the Spirit-borne message that finds them.
- "**That ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues**" — Greek _hina mē synkoinōnēsēte tais hamartiais autēs_. **Two reasons to come out:** (1) participation-in-sin avoidance; (2) plague-avoidance. **The plagues of Rev 16 fell on Babylon's adherents;** those who come out before Rev 15:8's close of probation / before the seven last plagues are poured out are spared. After Rev 15:8 the door is closed; before, every honest soul is being called.
- "**Her sins have reached unto heaven**" — Greek _ekollēthēsan autēs hai hamartiai achri tou ouranou_. **The sins are stacked.** Compare Gen 11:4 (Babel's tower reaching unto heaven) and Gen 18:20-21 (Sodom's outcry come up to the LORD). **Babylon's sins replicate Babel's reach** — and meet the same response.
- "**God hath remembered her iniquities**" — Greek _emnēmoneusen ho theos ta adikēmata autēs_. **Same verb as Rev 16:19** ("great Babylon came in remembrance before God"). **Heaven's books are read.**

## Rev 18:6-8 — Double Unto Her Double; Plagues in One Day

> "Reward her even as she rewarded you, and double unto her double according to her works: in the cup which she hath filled fill to her double. How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow. Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong is the Lord God who judgeth her." (Rev 18:6-8)

- "**Reward her even as she rewarded you**" — Greek _apodote autē hōs kai autē apedōken_. **Lex talionis applied to the saints' centuries of suffering.** Compare Jer 50:29 — "recompense her according to her work; according to all that she hath done, do unto her: for she hath been proud against the LORD." **Rev 18:6 is the eschatological enforcement of Jer 50:29.**
- "**Double unto her double**" — Greek _diplōsate ta dipla_. **In the OT, "double" appears in two distinct senses.** Isa 40:2 announces comfort to Jerusalem after she "hath received of the LORD'S hand double for all her sins" (a completed punitive measure followed by restoration). Job 42:10 gives a direct double restoration ("the LORD gave Job twice as much as he had before"). **Rev 18:6 inverts the double motif onto Babylon as judgment** — Babylon is repaid in the inverse currency, judged in measures matching the centuries of saints' suffering.
- "**In the cup which she hath filled fill to her double**" — Babylon's golden cup of Rev 17:4 returns. **The cup that filled the nations with fornication-wine is now filled with double-strength wrath** — the cup of the wine of the fierceness of God's wrath of Rev 16:19.
- "**She saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow**" — Greek _hoti basilissa kathēmai kai chēra ouk eimi_. **Direct echo of Isa 47:7-8** (Babylon's boast: "I shall be a lady for ever... I shall not sit as a widow, neither shall I know the loss of children"). **Rev 18:7 quotes Isa 47:8 almost verbatim.** Babylon's confidence is the same in both ages: untouchable, invulnerable, perpetual.
- "**Therefore shall her plagues come in one day**" — Greek _en mia hēmera_. **Compressed-time judgment.** The system that took centuries to build collapses in one day. **The "one hour" of vv. 10, 17, 19 zooms in further;** the "one day" frames the larger collapse.
- "**Death, and mourning, and famine**" — three of the four classic-judgment categories (sword/death, mourning/exile, famine, pestilence) of Lev 26 / Deut 28. **Comprehensive judgment on the comprehensive system.**
- "**Utterly burned with fire**" — Greek _katakauthēsetai en pyri_. **The fate of the harlot under the OT covenant** (Lev 21:9). The same destination Rev 17:16 named (the ten horns burn her with fire).
- "**Strong is the Lord God who judgeth her**" — Greek _ischyros kyrios ho theos ho krinas autēn_. **The judgment is by name and by power.** Babylon is not destroyed by accident or by political collapse alone; she is judged by the strong Lord God.

## Rev 18:9-10 — Kings' Lament: Alas, Alas, That Great City

> "And the kings of the earth, who have committed fornication and lived deliciously with her, shall bewail her, and lament for her, when they shall see the smoke of her burning, Standing afar off for the fear of her torment, saying, Alas, alas, that great city Babylon, that mighty city! for in one hour is thy judgment come." (Rev 18:9-10)

- "**The kings of the earth**" — same kings of Rev 17:2 / 17:18 / 16:14. **The political beneficiaries of Babylon's alliance now stand afar off and weep.** They had committed fornication with her; they had lived deliciously with her; they cannot now save her.
- "**Shall see the smoke of her burning**" — Greek _ton kapnon tēs pyrōseōs autēs_. **Compare Gen 19:28** ("the smoke of the country went up as the smoke of a furnace" — Sodom and Gomorrah burning) and **Isa 34:10** (Edom's smoke goes up forever). **The smoke is the public, visible, undeniable evidence of the system's destruction.**
- "**Standing afar off for the fear of her torment**" — the kings are too afraid to approach. **They cannot help; they can only watch from a distance.** The same posture is taken by the merchants (v. 15) and the sailors (v. 17).
- "**Alas, alas, that great city Babylon, that mighty city**" — Greek _ouai ouai hē polis hē megalē Babylōn hē polis hē ischyra_. **First of three "alas, alas" laments** (vv. 10, 16, 19). **_Ouai_** = woe / alas. **The chapter's three woes are mourner-laments;** they are not God's woes. The mourners are not repenting; they are mourning their own loss. **Their woe is for Babylon; God's woe was on Babylon.**
- "**In one hour is thy judgment come**" — Greek _en mia hōra ēlthen hē krisis sou_. **First of three "one hour" notices** (vv. 10, 17, 19). **The compressed time-frame of Babylon's collapse.** The system that took centuries to build collapses in a single hour of crisis.

## Rev 18:11-13 — Merchants Weep: The Great Cargo List

> "And the merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn over her; for no man buyeth their merchandise any more: The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet, and all thyine wood, and all manner vessels of ivory, and all manner vessels of most precious wood, and of brass, and iron, and marble, And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men." (Rev 18:11-13)

- "**The merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn**" — Greek _hoi emporoi tēs gēs klausousin kai penthēsousin_. **The merchants weep, but for their own loss, not for Babylon's wickedness.** Their mourning is commercial, not moral.
- "**No man buyeth their merchandise any more**" — when the system that consumed the goods is destroyed, the suppliers lose their market. **The merchants are exposed as parasites of the system,** not independent operators.
- "**The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet**" — the first 8 items overlap heavily with the harlot's adornments of Rev 17:4 (purple, scarlet, gold, precious stones, pearls), with silver, fine linen, and silk added as the supply-chain-feeding luxury trade. **Babylon's clothing was not just symbolic; it was sourced from a global luxury trade.**
- "**Thyine wood**" — exotic North-African aromatic timber (citron-wood / sandarac). **Vessels of ivory... most precious wood... brass, iron, marble** — building materials, furnishings, and architectural luxuries.
- "**Cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense**" — incense and aromatics (compare Ex 30:34's frankincense in the holy incense; Ex 30:23-25 names cinnamon in the holy anointing oil). **Babylon counterfeits even the sanctuary's perfume trade.**
- "**Wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat**" — staples-as-luxuries; the basic foods of the elite class.
- "**Beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots**" — livestock and transport.
- "**And slaves, and souls of men**" — Greek _kai sōmatōn kai psychas anthrōpōn_. **Two distinct categories.** _Sōmata_ ("bodies") = slaves in commercial Greek; _psychai anthrōpōn_ ("souls of men") = a separate, more disturbing trafficking category. **Babylon's commerce climaxes in human trafficking** — physical slavery and the corruption of human souls. **The list of 28 items moves from precious metals through staples to people, and the people are the climax.** The most comprehensive condemnation of the chapter is the final pair: a system that finally trades in human bodies and human souls is the system God burns.

[DYK🔎] **The Rev 18:12-13 cargo list is patterned on Eze 27:12-25's catalog of Tyre's merchandise.** Ezekiel's lament over Tyre lists (in this approximate order): silver, iron, tin, lead (Tarshish); slaves and brass vessels (Javan, Tubal, Meshech); horses, horsemen, mules (Togarmah); ivory and ebony (Dedan); emeralds, purple, fine linen, coral (Syria); wheat, honey, oil, balm (Judah and Israel); wine, white wool (Damascus); cassia, calamus (Dan and Javan); precious clothes, costly cedar-chests (Dedan); lambs, rams, goats (Arabia); spices, precious stones, gold (Sheba and Raamah); blue clothes, broidered work, cedar chests (Haran, Canneh, Eden, Sheba, Asshur, Chilmad). **Rev 18:13 specifically adds "souls of men"** to Ezekiel's pattern. Ezekiel's Tyre traded slaves (_nephesh adam_, "persons of men," Eze 27:13); Rev 18:13 splits the OT phrase into _sōmata_ (slave-bodies) AND _psychai anthrōpōn_ (souls of men). **The Rev 18 system traffics in two layers:** physical slavery (the Eze 27:13 pattern) AND the spiritual trafficking that captures souls — the harlot's _porneia_ catching humanity into religious-political bondage. **Rev 18 is what Ezekiel's Tyre-lament becomes when the merchant city becomes a spiritual-religious system.** The chapter is not merely commercial polemic; it is the OT merchant-city judgment expanded to include the soul-trafficking of the end-time religious-political composite.

## Rev 18:14-16 — All Things Dainty Departed

> "And the fruits that thy soul lusted after are departed from thee, and all things which were dainty and goodly are departed from thee, and thou shalt find them no more at all. The merchants of these things, which were made rich by her, shall stand afar off for the fear of her torment, weeping and wailing, And saying, Alas, alas, that great city, that was clothed in fine linen, and purple, and scarlet, and decked with gold, and precious stones, and pearls!" (Rev 18:14-16)

- "**The fruits that thy soul lusted after**" — Greek _hē opōra tēs epithymias tēs psychēs sou_. **_Opōra_** = the autumn-fruit harvest, the high-point of the year. **_Epithymia_** = lust, intense desire. **The objects of Babylon's appetite are gone in a single hour;** what was the harvest of the year disappears in 60 minutes.
- "**Stand afar off for the fear of her torment**" — repeated from v. 10. **The merchants take the kings' posture** — too afraid to approach, and too dependent to walk away unaffected.
- "**Alas, alas, that great city**" — second of the three woes. **The merchants' lament names the same wardrobe Rev 17:4 named for the harlot:** fine linen, purple, scarlet, gold, precious stones, pearls. **The merchants weep over the loss of the harlot's wardrobe** because their trade made it possible. The lament confirms the identification: the city the merchants mourn is the same harlot Rev 17 unmasked.

## Rev 18:17-19 — Sailors Weep: What City Is Like This?

> "For in one hour so great riches is come to nought. And every shipmaster, and all the company in ships, and sailors, and as many as trade by sea, stood afar off, And cried when they saw the smoke of her burning, saying, What city is like unto this great city! And they cast dust on their heads, and cried, weeping and wailing, saying, Alas, alas, that great city, wherein were made rich all that had ships in the sea by reason of her costliness! for in one hour is she made desolate." (Rev 18:17-19)

- "**Every shipmaster, and all the company in ships, and sailors**" — the third lamenting class. **Maritime trade** depended on Babylon's volume. **Eze 27:29-32 is the OT template** — the sailors mourn over Tyre's sinking. Rev 18:17-19 is the eschatological version of Eze 27.
- "**Cast dust on their heads**" — Greek _ebalon choun epi tas kephalas autōn_. **OT mourning gesture** (Josh 7:6; Job 2:12; Lam 2:10; Eze 27:30). **Total grief in OT idiom.**
- "**What city is like unto this great city**" — Greek _tis homoia tē polei tē megalē_. **An ironic inversion of Rev 13:4's beast-acclaim** ("Who is like unto the beast?"). **The same incomparability-claim that made the beast feel invincible is now a lament:** there is no city like this — and there will be no replacement.
- "**Alas, alas, that great city**" — third of the three woes, completing the triple lament. **All three groups (kings, merchants, sailors) say the same words: "alas, alas, that great city"** — the identical refrain showing that the ruin is universal, not selective.
- "**In one hour is she made desolate**" — third "one hour" notice. **The compressed-time-frame of the collapse is sealed by triple repetition.**

## Rev 18:20 — Rejoice Over Her, Heaven

> "Rejoice over her, thou heaven, and ye holy apostles and prophets; for God hath avenged you on her." (Rev 18:20)

- **The chapter's hinge.** Three earthly woes (vv. 10, 16, 19) are answered by one heavenly rejoicing. **The contrast is total:** earth weeps over its lost partner; heaven rejoices over the verdict.
- "**Thou heaven, and ye holy apostles and prophets**" — Greek _ho ouranos kai hoi hagioi kai hoi apostoloi kai hoi prophētai_. **The Greek lists four groups:** heaven, saints, apostles, prophets — covering the heavenly host, the body of believers, the NT apostles, and the OT prophets (KJV groups "saints + apostles" with the modifier "holy" producing "holy apostles"). **All are called to rejoice because the system that persecuted them across the centuries is now judged.**
- "**God hath avenged you on her**" — Greek _ekrinen ho theos to krima hymōn ex autēs_. **Direct answer to Rev 6:10's fifth-seal cry** ("How long, O Lord... dost thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth?"). **The wait is over;** the verdict is rendered. **Compare Lk 18:7-8** (Jesus's parable of the unjust judge: "shall not God avenge his own elect, which cry day and night unto him?... I tell you that he will avenge them speedily").
- **Rev 19:1-6 will then give heaven's four-fold Alleluia** (vv. 1, 3, 4, 6) at this same fall.

[CTRF] **The triple "alas, alas" of earth (vv. 10, 16, 19) vs. the rejoicing of heaven (v. 20) — the chapter's central counterfeit pair.** Earth grieves because Babylon's loss is earth's economic, political, and maritime loss. Heaven rejoices because Babylon's loss is the saints' vindication. **The same event produces opposite responses,** and the response identifies the responder. **Whoever weeps with the kings, merchants, and sailors over Babylon's fall has not heard the "Come out of her, my people" of v. 4.** Whoever rejoices with heaven and the apostles and prophets has answered the call. **The chapter forces the reader to choose a side at the moment of the verdict** — and the side chosen reveals which voice the reader has been listening to. **Babylon's mourners are Babylon's; heaven's rejoicers are heaven's.**

## Rev 18:21-23 — The Millstone Cast Into the Sea

> "And a mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone, and cast it into the sea, saying, Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all. And the voice of harpers, and musicians, and of pipers, and trumpeters, shall be heard no more at all in thee; and no craftsman, of whatsoever craft he be, shall be found any more in thee; and the sound of a millstone shall be heard no more at all in thee; And the light of a candle shall shine no more at all in thee; and the voice of the bridegroom and of the bride shall be heard no more at all in thee: for thy merchants were the great men of the earth; for by thy sorceries were all nations deceived." (Rev 18:21-23)

- "**A mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone, and cast it into the sea**" — direct re-enactment of Jer 51:63-64. **In Jeremiah's prophecy** (already 600+ years old when Revelation was written), the prophet Seraiah was instructed to bind Jeremiah's scroll-of-Babylon-judgment to a stone, cast it into the Euphrates, and say: "Thus shall Babylon sink, and shall not rise from the evil that I will bring upon her." **Rev 18:21 is the eschatological enactment of Jer 51:63-64.** Same prophetic gesture; same target; final fulfillment.
- "**Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all**" — Greek _ou mē heurethē eti_. **A six-fold "no more at all"** structure follows in vv. 21-23: no more (1) found, (2) harpers/musicians, (3) craftsmen, (4) millstone, (5) candle, (6) bridegroom/bride. **The repetition seals the absoluteness of the judgment.**
- "**The voice of harpers, and musicians, and of pipers, and trumpeters, shall be heard no more at all**" — culture and entertainment. **The sound that filled Babylon's halls is silenced.**
- "**No craftsman, of whatsoever craft**" — industry and skill. **The making-of-things ceases.**
- "**The sound of a millstone**" — daily-life domesticity. **Even the household routine stops.** Compare Mt 24:41 (two women grinding at the mill — eschatological judgment imagery).
- "**The light of a candle shall shine no more**" — the lamps go out. **Compare Jer 25:10** ("the voice of the bridegroom, and the voice of the bride, the sound of the millstones, and the light of the candle" — predicted of historical Babylon). **Rev 18:23 reuses Jeremiah's exact litany.** What was prophesied for literal Babylon is now executed on Mystery Babylon.
- "**The voice of the bridegroom and of the bride**" — weddings and family-formation cease. **The structural irony:** the harlot whose forehead-name made her the counterfeit-bride loses every echo of the genuine bride-and-bridegroom celebration. The harlot's collapse silences the very institution she counterfeited.
- "**Thy merchants were the great men of the earth**" — Greek _hoi megistanes tēs gēs_. **The merchants are not small traders; they are the world's elite.** Babylon's commercial top-tier was the earth's power-class.
- "**For by thy sorceries were all nations deceived**" — Greek _en tē pharmakeia sou eplanēthēsan panta ta ethnē_. **_Pharmakeia_** = sorcery, drug-induced spiritual enchantment, magical arts (cf. Gal 5:20, Rev 9:21, Rev 21:8, Rev 22:15). **Babylon's deception is supernatural** — not merely persuasion but enchantment. **The "wine of her fornication" of v. 3 was _pharmakeia_** in liquid form; the deception is sorcery dressed as religion.

---

## Rev 18:24 — In Her Was Found the Blood of Prophets and Saints

> "And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth." (Rev 18:24)

- "**In her was found**" — Greek _en autē haima... heurethē_. **Found at the verdict-stage.** The forensic verb: the evidence is on her premises.
- "**The blood of prophets, and of saints**" — three categories:
  - **Prophets** — OT prophets killed by Israel (Mt 23:35) and by Babylon's persecutors throughout history
  - **Saints** — the body of believers across the church-history era (cf. Rev 6:9; 13:7; 17:6)
  - **All that were slain upon the earth** — the universal category. **Babylon is named as the responsible party for every martyr-blood throughout history.** Compare Mt 23:35 — Jesus's verdict on Jerusalem: "that upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, from the blood of righteous Abel."
- **The chapter ends with the weight of accumulated guilt.** Babylon is the consummation of Cain's line; she is responsible for every blood-guilt across redemptive history that traces to the persecuting religious-political principle. **The verdict of v. 8 ("strong is the Lord God who judgeth her") is justified by the indictment of v. 24.**

## After the Verses — What Rev 18 Settles

- **Babylon falls in one hour.** The compressed-time language (vv. 10, 17, 19) is structural, not approximate. **The system that took centuries to build collapses in the closing-crisis hour.**
- **The Loud Cry is the last salvation-call.** Rev 18:1's earth-lighted angel and Rev 18:4's "Come out of her, my people" are the closing-crisis ingathering. **God still has His people inside Babylon at the moment of the call;** the message finds them; some hear, some refuse. **The Spirit's final outpouring** (the latter rain, Joel 2:28-29 / James 5:7) gives the message its volume.
- **The chapter is the OT Babylon-judgment chapters' eschatological fulfillment.** Jer 50-51, Isa 13, Isa 47, Eze 26-28 — all the merchant-city judgment language is concentrated here. **The OT prophets prophesied the pattern; Rev 18 completes it.**
- **Babylon is religious AND economic AND political.** The harlot of Rev 17 is the harlot of Rev 18. The same system. **Religious authority + commercial empire + political alliance = the composite Babylon Revelation has been describing.** Modern readers who isolate the religious dimension miss the chapter's economic and political dimensions; modern readers who isolate the economic miss the religious.
- **The triple "alas, alas" identifies sides.** Whoever mourns with the kings, merchants, and sailors has not come out. Whoever rejoices with heaven and the apostles and prophets has answered the call. **The chapter forces the reader to take a side at the verdict.**
- **The come-out call still speaks before the close of probation.** Rev 18:4 is current — every honest soul still inside Babylon's framework is being called now. **Once Rev 15:8's temple-doors close and Rev 22:11's character-fixing decree applies,** the call ceases. **The chapter is for now.**

[→] **Cross-anchor.** Rev 18 (Babylon's fall and the come-out call) is the salvation-side of the Rev 14:8 second-angel's-message and the third-angel-warning amplified by the latter rain. Hold Rev 14:6-12 + Rev 18 + the closing crisis of Rev 13:15-17 together; do not isolate Rev 18 from the three-angels-message frame.

## Key Takeaways

- Rev 18 is the chapter that gives the closing-message its content and its loudest moment — the Final Loud Cry
- The angel of v. 1 with great power and earth-lighting glory is the Spirit-empowered final-warning angel, structurally the climactic "another angel" after the three of Rev 14:6-12
- "Babylon is fallen, is fallen" (v. 2) repeats Rev 14:8 — prophetic announcement now executed at the closing crisis
- The chapter recycles Jeremiah 50-51 deliberately; Rev 18 is the eschatological version of the OT Babylon-judgment prophecies
- "Come out of her, my people" (v. 4) implies God still has His people inside Babylon at the call's moment
- The come-out pattern runs OT-deep: Lot from Sodom (Gen 19), Israel from literal Babylon (Isa 48:20; Jer 51:6, 45), Christians from Jerusalem before AD 70 (Lk 21:21), Rev 18:4 as the eschatological climax
- Triple "alas, alas" laments (vv. 10, 16, 19) — kings, merchants, sailors — match three "one hour" notices for a compressed-time judgment
- The 28-item cargo list (vv. 12-13) climaxes in "slaves, and souls of men" — physical and spiritual trafficking. The list patterns on Eze 27:12-25's Tyre catalog
- "By thy sorceries were all nations deceived" (v. 23) — Babylon's deception is _pharmakeia_, supernatural enchantment, not mere persuasion
- The mighty angel's millstone-cast (v. 21) re-enacts Jer 51:63-64's prophetic sign-act
- "In her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth" (v. 24) — Babylon as the consummation of every persecuting blood-guilt across history
- The chapter forces the reader to choose: weep with earth's three lament groups, or rejoice with heaven and the apostles and prophets
