# Study 4: The Sanctuary Key — Leviticus 16

**Dan 8:14 said the sanctuary will be cleansed. Study 3 dated that event to 1844. But what _is_ the sanctuary, and why does Scripture make it the place where the entire plan of salvation unfolds? The answer Scripture gives is one chapter long — Leviticus 16, the Day of Atonement. Its three compartments traced the gospel: cross, intercession, judgment. Its one holy day pictured what Revelation will later announce as "the hour of his judgment." We will walk Leviticus 16 verse by verse. If you understand this chapter, every thread in Daniel and Revelation has a coherent place to stand.**

## Prep Reading

- Leviticus 16 (the Day of Atonement — entire chapter)
- Hebrews 8 (the priestly ministry in the heavenly sanctuary)
- Hebrews 9 (the two tabernacles compared)
- Hebrews 10:19-25
- Psalm 77:13
- Revelation 11:19; 14:6-7; 15:5-8

---

## Before the Verses — "Thy Way, O God, Is in the Sanctuary"

Listen to Asaph before we open Leviticus. The psalm begins in crisis — "I cried unto God with my voice... my sore ran in the night" (Ps 77:1-2). Asaph asks whether God has forgotten mercy, whether His promises have failed. Then he turns the corner:

- "I will remember the works of the LORD: surely I will remember thy wonders of old" (Ps 77:11)
- "I will meditate also of all thy work" (Ps 77:12)
- "Thy way, O God, is in the sanctuary: who is so great a God as our God?" (Ps 77:13)

- [→] Asaph's answer to doubt is not theology-from-nowhere. It is the sanctuary. Where does God's character come clear? In the sanctuary service. Every question about God's justice, God's mercy, God's mission — the sanctuary shows the answer in physical form. Dan 8:14 did not pick the sanctuary arbitrarily. The sanctuary is _the_ place God uses to reveal Himself across the Old Testament; the closing book lands all of it on sanctuary soil (Rev 11:19; 14:6-7; 15:5-8; 21:22).

## Before the Verses — A Copy of the Real Thing

Moses did not design the tabernacle. He was _shown_ it.

- Ex 25:8-9, 40 — "Let them make me a sanctuary; that I may dwell among them... according to all that I shew thee, after the pattern of the tabernacle... See, saith he, that thou make all things according to the pattern shewed to thee in the mount."
- Heb 8:1-5 — "We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens; a minister of the sanctuary, and of the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man... who serve unto the example and shadow of heavenly things."

- The implication: there is a _real_ sanctuary in heaven. The earthly tabernacle Moses built was a scale model, a "shadow" (_skia_, outline/silhouette) of that real one. Every piece of furniture, every garment, every ritual was a teaching model of something Christ would do in the heavenly sanctuary above.

- `[KEY🔑]` **The earthly sanctuary is a _pattern_ of the heavenly, not a replacement for it.** Hebrews makes this the axis of its entire theology (Heb 8:1-5; 9:11-12, 23-24; 10:1). Hold this distinction and the rest of Daniel and Revelation opens. Collapse it and we are stuck looking for earthly temples to rebuild.

[DYK🔎] The Greek word Hebrews uses for the earthly sanctuary is _hypodeigma_ (Heb 8:5; 9:23) — "a specimen, a pattern, a copy." Paired with _skia_ ("shadow") it means: the earthly was never the point. The earthly told you what to look for in the heavenly. When Christ ascended (Heb 9:24) He did not enter "the holy places made with hands, which are the figures of the true; but into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us." The figures had purpose for a time; the real thing has taken over.

## Before the Verses — The Three Compartments

The earthly tabernacle had three zones, each with a different function and a different level of access. Lev 16 will move the high priest through all three of them in one day; reading the chapter without this map leaves the action floating.

```
   ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │                     OUTER COURTYARD                         │
   │                                                             │
   │     ┌─ Brazen altar ─┐         ┌─ Laver ─┐                  │
   │     │   (sacrifice)  │         │ (wash)  │                  │
   │     └────────────────┘         └─────────┘                  │
   │                                                             │
   │  ┌────────────────────────────┐ ┌──────────────┐            │
   │  │        HOLY PLACE          │ │ MOST HOLY    │            │
   │  │                            │ │              │            │
   │  │ Table of     Golden        │ │  Ark of the  │            │
   │  │ shewbread    candlestick   │ │  covenant    │            │
   │  │                            │ │  (mercy seat)│            │
   │  │    Altar of incense        │ │              │            │
   │  │     (before the veil)      │ │              │            │
   │  └────────────────────────────┘ └──────────────┘            │
   │                              ↑                              │
   │                      the second veil                        │
   └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Compartment                                    | Furniture                                       | Function                | What it pictures                                                     |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Courtyard** (open to all Israel)             | Brazen altar, laver                             | Sacrifice and washing   | The cross: Christ's death, confession and cleansing                  |
| **Holy Place** (priests, daily)                | Lampstand, table of shewbread, altar of incense | Daily priestly ministry | Christ's ongoing intercession in heaven since the ascension          |
| **Most Holy Place** (high priest, once a year) | Ark of the covenant, mercy seat                 | Annual Day of Atonement | Final judgment — sins examined, records reviewed, covenant confirmed |

[Q] **"Why three compartments instead of one altar?"**
→ Because salvation is not a single moment. The cross is the foundation, but it is not the whole work. After the lamb's death, sin was still ritually _transferred_ to the sanctuary (Lev 4:5-7) and had to be _cleansed out_ on a later day (Lev 16:30). The tabernacle's three zones teach: the cross is paid, but the _administration_ of salvation continues until the books are closed. The Holy Place is Christ's ongoing ministry (Heb 7:25 — "he ever liveth to make intercession"). The Most Holy is the final review. Three compartments, three phases, one gospel.

## Before the Verses — The Daily (_tamid_)

From the moment the tabernacle was dedicated, the priests' main task was continuous: day by day, morning and evening, forever. Lev 16 is the _annual_ day; it sits on top of a _daily_ ministry already running. The two are inseparable, and Dan 8 named them both — the _tamid_ in vv. 11-13, the cleansing in v. 14.

- **What _tamid_ ("continual") covered** — Ex 29-30, Lev 6, Num 28
  - The morning and evening burnt offerings (Ex 29:38-42)
  - The lamps burning before the LORD continually (Ex 27:20-21; Lev 24:2-4)
  - The shewbread set out continually (Ex 25:30; Lev 24:5-8)
  - Incense burned on the altar morning and evening (Ex 30:7-8)
  - Sin offerings for individuals confessing throughout the year (Lev 4)
- "Continual" — _tamid_ — describes _all of it_, not just one ritual. This is the word Dan 8:11-13 uses when the little horn "takes away the daily."
- The daily pictured Christ's _continual_ ministry in heaven (Heb 7:25 — "Christ... ever liveth to make intercession"). The earthly lampstand, shewbread, and incense were visual aids for a heavenly reality that would start at the cross and continue until the judgment.

# Walk-Through — Leviticus 16

## Lev 16:1-2 — The cost of casual approach

> "And the LORD spake unto Moses after the death of the two sons of Aaron, when they offered before the LORD, and died; And the LORD said unto Moses, Speak unto Aaron thy brother, that he come not at all times into the holy place within the vail before the mercy seat, which is upon the ark; that he die not: for I will appear in the cloud upon the mercy seat." (Lev 16:1-2)

- The chapter opens by naming a death. Aaron's two sons Nadab and Abihu had offered "strange fire" (Lev 10:1-2) and were killed instantly. Lev 16 begins by saying: this is the chapter that came _because of_ that incident. The whole Day of Atonement framework was given immediately after the priesthood learned the cost of treating the holy place casually.
- "Come not at all times into the holy place within the vail." Aaron is forbidden routine access to the Most Holy Place. The Most Holy is _restricted_; only one entrance per year is permitted, and only with the protocol Lev 16 will spell out.
- "I will appear in the cloud upon the mercy seat." God's manifest presence is _located_ — between the cherubim on the ark cover. The mercy seat is the throne (cf. Ex 25:22 — "there I will meet with thee"). Lev 16 is the protocol for entering the throne room.
- [→] The architecture of fear is in place before the architecture of grace is shown. You cannot read Lev 16's mercy without remembering Lev 10's death.

## Lev 16:3-4 — Aaron prepares: bullock, ram, linen garments

> "Thus shall Aaron come into the holy place: with a young bullock for a sin offering, and a ram for a burnt offering. He shall put on the holy linen coat, and he shall have the linen breeches upon his flesh, and shall be girded with a linen girdle, and with the linen mitre shall he be attired: these are holy garments; therefore shall he wash his flesh in water, and so put them on." (Lev 16:3-4)

- "A young bullock for a sin offering, and a ram for a burnt offering." The first sin offering Aaron brings is _for himself_ (v. 6). Before he can mediate for anyone, his own atonement must be made.
- "Linen coat... linen breeches... linen girdle... linen mitre." On every other day of the year Aaron wore the high priest's "garments of glory and beauty" (Ex 28:2) — gold, blue, purple, scarlet, the breastplate with twelve stones. Today, _only_ linen. Plain. White. The garments of a servant.
- "Wash his flesh in water." Bathing first — the Day of Atonement is the only day Scripture commands the high priest to bathe before _and_ after entering (cf. v. 24).
- [→] The mediator humbles himself first. Phil 2:7-8 — Christ "made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: and being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death." The high priest in linen pictures the Christ who serves before He reigns.

## Lev 16:5-7 — Two goats, presented at the door

> "And he shall take of the congregation of the children of Israel two kids of the goats for a sin offering, and one ram for a burnt offering. And Aaron shall offer his bullock of the sin offering, which is for himself, and make an atonement for himself, and for his house. And he shall take the two goats, and present them before the LORD at the door of the tabernacle of the congregation." (Lev 16:5-7)

- "Two kids of the goats for a sin offering." Note the singular: "for a sin offering" — both goats are part of one sin-offering ritual, even though only one will be killed. The chapter treats them as two halves of a single act.
- "One ram for a burnt offering." This ram is for the people (separate from the ram in v. 3 for Aaron). Both rams will be offered later in v. 24.
- Aaron's first task: atone for himself and his house (v. 6). The mediator's own standing is settled before the people's day proceeds.
- "Present them before the LORD at the door of the tabernacle." Both goats stand together at the doorway. The lots will be cast in front of everyone — neither destiny is chosen by Aaron, neither by the goats. The LORD's lot decides which goat does what.

## Lev 16:8-10 — Lots cast: one for the LORD, one for Azazel

> "And Aaron shall cast lots upon the two goats; one lot for the LORD, and the other lot for the scapegoat. And Aaron shall bring the goat upon which the LORD'S lot fell, and offer him for a sin offering. But the goat, on which the lot fell to be the scapegoat, shall be presented alive before the LORD, to make an atonement with him, and to let him go for a scapegoat into the wilderness." (Lev 16:8-10)

- "One lot for the LORD, and the other lot for the scapegoat" (Hebrew _Azazel_). The Hebrew word _Azazel_ is debated, but the structural opposition in this verse is unmistakable: one lot for _Yahweh_, one lot for _Azazel_. Two named destinations stand opposite each other.
- The LORD's goat dies as a sin offering — its blood will go into the Most Holy Place (v. 15). The Christ-type.
- The Azazel goat is _kept alive_ — it is "presented alive before the LORD" — and waits for the rest of the day's work to complete. It does not bear sin yet; it is held in reserve for the closing act after atonement is finished (vv. 20-22).
- "To let him go for a scapegoat into the wilderness." _Wilderness_ — _midbar_, wilderness/open desert country; v. 22 adds that the destination is "a land not inhabited." The destination is desolation, not a sacred place. The Azazel goat is sent _away_ from the camp; the LORD's goat is brought _in_ to the holy place.

[DYK🔎] The scapegoat (Hebrew _Azazel_) is _not_ a Christ-symbol. Christ's blood is pictured by the _first_ goat (the LORD's goat, vv. 9, 15), which is killed and whose blood enters the sanctuary. The Azazel goat receives no sin-bearing role until _after_ atonement is complete (vv. 20-21); it bears away sins that have _already_ been reconciled, and it is led to "a land not inhabited" (v. 22) — a desolation picture, not a Calvary picture. Jewish apocalyptic sources (1 Enoch 8-10), early-Christian readers (Origen among them), and the historicist Adventist tradition identify Azazel with Satan: the one who originated sin has its weight rolled back onto him when the judgment is finished. Rev 20 will show this picture literally: Satan bound in the desolated earth for a thousand years while the saints are in the city. The scapegoat reaches across the whole Bible.

## Lev 16:11-13 — Aaron's atonement and the cloud of incense

> "And Aaron shall bring the bullock of the sin offering, which is for himself, and shall make an atonement for himself, and for his house, and shall kill the bullock of the sin offering which is for himself: And he shall take a censer full of burning coals of fire from off the altar before the LORD, and his hands full of sweet incense beaten small, and bring it within the vail: And he shall put the incense upon the fire before the LORD, that the cloud of the incense may cover the mercy seat that is upon the testimony, that he die not." (Lev 16:11-13)

- Aaron kills the bullock for himself first (v. 11). The mediator does not skip his own atonement.
- "Burning coals of fire from off the altar before the LORD." The coals come from the courtyard's brazen altar — the altar of sacrifice. The fire that lit the incense was kindled by the same fire that consumed the sin offering. Mediation begins at the altar.
- "Bring it within the vail." Aaron crosses from the Holy Place into the Most Holy Place. This is the first crossing; he will cross multiple times today.
- "The cloud of the incense may cover the mercy seat... that he die not." The incense cloud is _protection_. Aaron does not approach the throne unmediated — even on this day, with this protocol, with this preparation, he approaches behind a cloud. Compare Rev 8:3-4 — "another angel came and stood at the altar, having a golden censer; and there was given unto him much incense, that he should offer it with the prayers of all saints upon the golden altar." The incense is the prayers of the saints carried into the throne room. The cloud that lets Aaron live is the same picture as the prayers that let us approach.

## Lev 16:14 — Blood of the bullock sprinkled

> "And he shall take of the blood of the bullock, and sprinkle it with his finger upon the mercy seat eastward; and before the mercy seat shall he sprinkle of the blood with his finger seven times." (Lev 16:14)

- Aaron carries his own sin offering's blood _into_ the Most Holy Place. _On_ the mercy seat once, _before_ it seven times. Eight applications of priestly blood.
- "Eastward" — the entrance side. The blood faces the door, witnessing the way of approach.
- "Seven times." Seven is the number of completion (Gen 2:1-3). Sevenfold sprinkling = total cleansing. The same number will appear in Rev 5:6 (the Lamb's "seven horns and seven eyes"), Rev 8 (seven trumpets), Rev 15-16 (seven last plagues). Seven structures the whole sanctuary-judgment economy.

## Lev 16:15 — The LORD's goat killed; blood within the veil

> "Then shall he kill the goat of the sin offering, that is for the people, and bring his blood within the vail, and do with that blood as he did with the blood of the bullock, and sprinkle it upon the mercy seat, and before the mercy seat:" (Lev 16:15)

- Now the LORD's goat dies. Its blood — the people's sin offering — is carried into the Most Holy Place exactly as the bullock's blood was. Same gestures. Eight applications. Same throne.
- The Christ-type comes into focus. Aaron's bullock-blood (v. 14) atoned for the priesthood; the goat's blood atones for the people (v. 15). _Both_ entered the Most Holy. Heb 9:12 picks up the pattern: "Neither by the blood of goats and calves, but by his own blood he entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption for us." Christ fulfills both — His own blood replaces both the bullock and the goat.
- [→] The LORD's goat does not bear sin _back into the camp_. It is killed. Its blood goes into the throne room. The Azazel goat (held alive, v. 10) is what bears sin _outside_ the camp later. Christ's death is what the LORD's goat pictures; Christ does not carry the residual guilt of His enemies into eternity. He pays for sin and ends it.

## Lev 16:16-17 — The sanctuary itself is cleansed

> "And he shall make an atonement for the holy place, because of the uncleanness of the children of Israel, and because of their transgressions in all their sins: and so shall he do for the tabernacle of the congregation, that remaineth among them in the midst of their uncleanness. And there shall be no man in the tabernacle of the congregation when he goeth in to make an atonement in the holy place, until he come out, and have made an atonement for himself, and for his household, and for all the congregation of Israel." (Lev 16:16-17)

- **Key move:** the atonement is made _for the holy place_. The sanctuary itself is the object of cleansing. Throughout the year, when Israelites confessed sins over their offerings in the courtyard, the sins were ritually transferred into the sanctuary via the blood (Lev 4:5-7, 16-18). The sanctuary accumulated sin-record across the year. On this day, the sanctuary's records are dealt with.
- "Because of the uncleanness of the children of Israel, and because of their transgressions in all their sins." Three categories of sin (uncleanness, transgressions, sins) — the sweep is comprehensive. Nothing confessed is left unaddressed; nothing brought to the altar across the year is overlooked here.
- "There shall be no man in the tabernacle of the congregation when he goeth in." Aaron is alone. The Day of Atonement is a day of solitary mediation. Heb 9:7 — "into the second [tabernacle] went the high priest alone once every year, not without blood." The mediator stands alone before the throne with the people's blood.
- [→] This is what Dan 8:14 pictures: "unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be _cleansed_." Same operation as Lev 16:16. Same ceremony. Same function. Just executed in the heavenly sanctuary, at the end of a 2300-year timeline.
- `[⟲]` **Sanctuary thread:** Lev 16 → Dan 8:11-14 → Heb 8-9 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 15:5-8. Lev 16:16's "atonement for the holy place" is the source-language for the entire chain.

## Lev 16:18-19 — The altar cleansed too

> "And he shall go out unto the altar that is before the LORD, and make an atonement for it; and shall take of the blood of the bullock, and of the blood of the goat, and put it upon the horns of the altar round about. And he shall sprinkle of the blood upon it with his finger seven times, and cleanse it, and hallow it from the uncleanness of the children of Israel." (Lev 16:18-19)

- "The altar that is before the LORD" — in Lev 4:7, 18 this phrase names the **golden altar of incense** that stands inside the Holy Place "before the vail," distinguished from the brazen altar at the door. Aaron now cleanses that incense altar — the very altar whose smoke had covered the mercy seat in v. 13.
- "Both bloods" — bullock and goat mixed. The mediator's own blood and the people's blood applied together. The altar of intercession itself is cleansed by the same mediator's-and-people's blood.
- Seven sprinklings again. Completion. The cleansing is total.
- [→] The atonement does not stop at the throne. It works back outward — throne, altar, congregation. The judgment that begins in the Most Holy reaches every place sin had touched.

## Lev 16:20-22 — The live goat: confession and exile

> "And when he hath made an end of reconciling the holy place, and the tabernacle of the congregation, and the altar, he shall bring the live goat: And Aaron shall lay both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions in all their sins, putting them upon the head of the goat, and shall send him away by the hand of a fit man into the wilderness: And the goat shall bear upon him all their iniquities unto a land not inhabited: and he shall let go the goat in the wilderness." (Lev 16:20-22)

- "When he hath made an end of reconciling." Atonement is _finished_ before the live goat is touched. The Azazel goat acts only after every cleansing has already been made. This is critical: the live goat is not part of the atoning work. It is what happens _after_ atonement.
- "Lay both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities." Two-handed laying-on is rare and emphatic — total transfer. Confession names the sins out loud. The goat receives the named guilt.
- "Send him away by the hand of a fit man into the wilderness." The goat is taken out — far out. Jewish tradition (Mishnah Yoma 6.4-6) records the practice of leading the goat to a remote precipice; the historical detail is later, but the chapter's plain reading already points the goat into uninhabited territory.
- "Bear upon him all their iniquities unto a land not inhabited" (v. 22). _A land not inhabited._ Desolation. Compare Rev 20:1-3 — Satan bound a thousand years in the abyss / desolated earth; only confessed-and-cleansed sin reaches him, after the cleansing is complete; he is held alone in a land not inhabited.
- [→] The structure: Christ's blood (LORD's goat, vv. 9, 15) atones; once atonement is _finished_, originated guilt rolls back onto the originator (Azazel goat, vv. 20-22). The cross does not absorb Satan's responsibility for inventing sin. The cross redeems sinners; the post-atonement scapegoat ritual returns Satan's authored guilt to him. Rev 20 will play this out at history's close.

## Lev 16:23-24 — Bathing and burnt offerings

> "And Aaron shall come into the tabernacle of the congregation, and shall put off the linen garments, which he put on when he went into the holy place, and shall leave them there: And he shall wash his flesh with water in the holy place, and put on his garments, and come forth, and offer his burnt offering, and the burnt offering of the people, and make an atonement for himself, and for the people." (Lev 16:23-24)

- Aaron exchanges the linen for his "garments of glory and beauty" (Ex 28:2). The Day-of-Atonement humility ends; he resumes the high-priestly identity for the closing burnt offerings.
- A second bathing. Beginning and end. The mediator is washed entering and washed leaving the day's work.
- "Offer his burnt offering, and the burnt offering of the people" — the rams from v. 3 and v. 5. Burnt offerings express total dedication; they close the day with a public, total surrender.

## Lev 16:25-28 — Fat burned; cleansings of helpers

> "And the fat of the sin offering shall he burn upon the altar. And he that let go the goat for the scapegoat shall wash his clothes, and bathe his flesh in water, and afterward come into the camp. And the bullock for the sin offering, and the goat for the sin offering, whose blood was brought in to make atonement in the holy place, shall one carry forth without the camp; and they shall burn in the fire their skins, and their flesh, and their dung. And he that burneth them shall wash his clothes, and bathe his flesh in water, and afterward he shall come into the camp." (Lev 16:25-28)

- Two helpers — the man who led the Azazel goat away, and the man who burned the bullock and LORD's goat outside the camp — must wash before re-entering. Even handling the cleansing apparatus carries a residue that requires bathing.
- "Carry forth without the camp... burn in the fire" — Heb 13:11-13 picks this up: "the bodies of those beasts, whose blood is brought into the sanctuary by the high priest for sin, are burned without the camp. Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate." Christ's crucifixion outside Jerusalem fulfills the type. The bodies of the sin-offering animals were not consumed inside the camp; they were taken out and burned. Calvary is the antitype.

## Lev 16:29-31 — Statute forever: afflict your souls

> "And this shall be a statute for ever unto you: that in the seventh month, on the tenth day of the month, ye shall afflict your souls, and do no work at all, whether it be one of your own country, or a stranger that sojourneth among you: For on that day shall the priest make an atonement for you, to cleanse you, that ye may be clean from all your sins before the LORD. It shall be a sabbath of rest unto you, and ye shall afflict your souls, by a statute for ever." (Lev 16:29-31)

- "The seventh month, on the tenth day" — the tenth of Tishri. _Yom Kippur_ in the Hebrew calendar. The only day each year the Most Holy Place was entered.
- "Afflict your souls" — _innah napshotaykem_. Fasting, confession, self-examination. Lev 23:29 sharpens it: "whatsoever soul it be that shall not be afflicted in that same day, he shall be cut off from among his people." On the day the high priest mediates inside, the people grieve outside. _Together._
- "Do no work at all... a sabbath of rest." Even on the tenth day of the seventh month, the Day of Atonement is called _sabbath of sabbaths_ (_shabbat shabbaton_, Lev 23:32). The day of judgment is the day of rest. The work is the priest's, not the people's.
- "That ye may be clean from all your sins before the LORD" (v. 30). The promise is total cleansing. Not partial. Not provisional. _Clean from all your sins before the LORD._
- [→] On the heavenly Day of Atonement opened in 1844, the same call is going out — afflict souls, fast, confess, examine, rest. Rev 14:7's first angel says, "Fear God, and give glory to him; for the hour of his judgment is come." Rev 14:12 names the saints' response: patience, the commandments, the faith of Jesus. The Day of Atonement posture lands in the three angels' messages.

## Lev 16:32-34 — Anointed priest, statute everlasting

> "And the priest, whom he shall anoint, and whom he shall consecrate to minister in the priest's office in his father's stead, shall make the atonement, and shall put on the linen clothes, even the holy garments: And he shall make an atonement for the holy sanctuary, and he shall make an atonement for the tabernacle of the congregation, and for the altar, and he shall make an atonement for the priests, and for all the people of the congregation. And this shall be an everlasting statute unto you, to make an atonement for the children of Israel for all their sins once a year. And he did as the LORD commanded Moses." (Lev 16:32-34)

- "Whom he shall anoint." The mediator is _anointed_ — same root as _mashiyach_, Messiah. Dan 9:24's "anoint the most Holy" reads back into this. The high priest at the Day of Atonement is the anointed one; Christ's heavenly Day of Atonement work is the work of the Anointed One.
- "Make an atonement for the holy sanctuary... for the tabernacle... for the altar... for the priests... for all the people." The full sweep: Most Holy, Holy Place, courtyard, mediators, congregation. _Everything_ inside the camp is brought into the cleansing.
- "Once a year." The annual cadence. Hebrews will say Christ's offering of Himself was _once for all_ (Heb 9:26-28; 10:10-14) — a single death, never repeated. The annual repetition of Lev 16 was the type confessing its own incompleteness; the death of Christ is the antitype that does the work the type pictured.
- "He did as the LORD commanded Moses." The chapter ends in obedient execution. The pattern is given _and done_.

## After the Verses — Dan 8:14 Meets Rev 14:7

Three texts lay side by side and say the same thing.

- **Dan 8:14** — "Unto two thousand and three hundred days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed"
- **Lev 16:30** — "For on that day shall the priest make an atonement for you, to cleanse you"
- **Rev 14:6-7** — "And I saw another angel fly in the midst of heaven, having the everlasting gospel... saying with a loud voice, Fear God, and give glory to him; for _the hour of his judgment is come_"

- [→] Three witnesses, one event. Lev 16 says: the cleansing is a judgment, done in the sanctuary, on a specific day. Dan 8 says: the cleansing arrives at the end of 2300 days. Rev 14 says: when the time comes, the world is told "the hour of his judgment is come." Three angles on the same event — the final phase of Christ's priestly ministry.
- `[⟲]` **Judgment thread:** Dan 7:9-14 courtroom → Rev 4-5 throne → Rev 11:15-19 temple opened → Rev 14:6-7 judgment announced → Rev 20:11-12 books opened. Lev 16 is the sanctuary's Day-of-Atonement scaffolding, registered in the Sanctuary chain rather than the Judgment chain. The sanctuary and the judgment are not two threads; they are two angles on the same redemptive accounting.

## After the Verses — What 1844 Is and Is Not

In Study 3 we reached the date. Here we place what actually starts that year.

- **What 1844 _is_** (Heb 9:23-24; Rev 11:19; 14:6-7)
  - The start of the heavenly Day of Atonement — Christ's entrance into the final phase of His priestly work
  - The beginning of an investigative judgment on the records of all who have claimed His name (cf. 1 Pet 4:17 — "judgment must begin at the house of God")
  - The prophetic event Rev 14:7 announces to the world as "the hour of his judgment"
  - The temple's Most Holy Place opened in heaven — "And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament" (Rev 11:19)

- **What 1844 is _not_**
  - It is _not_ the Second Coming (Christ does not return to earth then)
  - It is _not_ Christ's first entrance into God's presence — Heb 9:24 places that at the ascension. What starts in 1844 is the final judgment/cleansing phase of His already-ongoing priestly ministry, not the ministry itself
  - It is _not_ a "second chance at probation" starting then (probation opened at the cross, not 1844)
  - It is _not_ a new gospel — the gospel has always been Christ's blood; 1844 is a phase-change in the _administration_ of that gospel
  - It is _not_ primarily about fear for the believer — the High Priest appears "in the presence of God for us" (Heb 9:24). Presence. Advocacy. Not opposition.

[Q] **"Does an investigative judgment mean God does not already know who is His?"**
→ God does not need the investigation. The _universe_ does. The cosmic trial Satan opened (Job 1-2; Rev 12:10) requires a public accounting. When Dan 7:10 says "the judgment was set, and the books were opened" — the books are opened _before angels_. "Unto the principalities and powers in heavenly places might be known by the church the manifold wisdom of God" (Eph 3:10). The judgment is not for God's benefit. It vindicates His administration before every created being. That is why records matter. That is why advocacy matters. That is why the Lamb slain stands in the throne (Rev 5:6) — to be seen.

## After the Verses — `[CTRF]` Pair #5: An Earthly Sanctuary System Built Over the Heavenly One

The _tamid_ being "taken away" (Dan 8:11) is one move inside a larger parody. Hebrews 8-9 names the original: Christ ministering in "the true tabernacle, which the Lord pitched, and not man" (Heb 8:2), with His own blood "into the holy place... having obtained eternal redemption for us" (Heb 9:12).

- The medieval substitute is the whole apparatus — an earthly priesthood, an earthly altar, a recurring sacrifice, a confessional in place of the throne of grace, a Marian mediator in place of "one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Tim 2:5). Same shape, wrong location, wrong priest, wrong blood.
- `[CTRF]` **Pair #5: an earthly sanctuary system / the heavenly sanctuary (Heb 8-9).** The parody system is not built _instead of_ the heavenly sanctuary; it is built _over the top of it_, hiding what Christ is doing. Dan 8:12's "cast down the truth to the ground" names the effect. The 2300-day cleansing of Dan 8:14 is the answer: the heavenly sanctuary's truth is restored to view.

## After the Verses — The Whole New Testament Builds to the High Priest

Hebrews is not the only New Testament book that ends up in the sanctuary. Read for its chiastic layout, the entire canon is shaped around it.

- The four Gospels open the canon with four faces of Christ — Matthew (King), Mark (Servant), Luke (Man), John (God) — and Rev 4:7's four living creatures close the canon with the same four faces (lion, calf, man, eagle). The bookends match.
- Acts is, in effect, "Acts of the Holy Spirit" — Pentecost's tongues of fire (Acts 2:1-4) pair with Rev 4:5's "seven lamps of fire" before the throne. The Spirit poured out at the start, the Spirit pictured at the throne at the end.
- Paul's church-letter cities group as seven (Rome, Corinth, Galatia, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae/Laodicea linked by Col 4:13-16, Thessalonica), lining up with Rev 2-3's seven churches. Paul writes to seven; John writes to seven.
- At the structural center sit two books that say the same thing in different keys: **Hebrews and Revelation 1**. Both describe Christ as High Priest. Both reference the Sabbath (Heb 4:9; Rev 1:10). Both use the same image of His word as a two-edged sword (Heb 4:12-13; Rev 1:16; 19:15). Hebrews places Him at the right hand of the throne in the true tabernacle (Heb 8:1-2); Revelation 1 shows Him among the seven candlesticks in priestly garments (Rev 1:13). One book gives the doctrine; the other gives the vision.

- [DYK🔎] **The center of the New Testament is Christ as High Priest in the heavenly sanctuary.** Not the cross alone. Not the Second Coming alone. The structural heart is the room He is serving in _right now_. The chiastic NT layout makes the sanctuary's load-bearing role unmistakable: this is not a footnote in Adventist theology — it is the architectural center of the New Testament's witness about Christ.

[DYK🔎] At the very center of Hebrews' high-priestly argument sits one Greek word: _sabbatismos_ (Heb 4:9 — "There remaineth therefore a rest [_sabbatismos_] to the people of God"). It is a New Testament hapax — used nowhere else. Embedded in the structural center of the structural-center book is "a Sabbath-keeping." Three verses later the same passage names the standard the High Priest measures lives by: "the word of God is quick, and powerful, and sharper than any twoedged sword... and is a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart. Neither is there any creature that is not manifest in his sight: but all things are _naked and opened_ unto the eyes of him with whom we have to do" (Heb 4:12-13). That is the room the Day of Atonement opens. The High Priest at the center is a Sabbath-keeping High Priest, serving a Sabbath-keeping people, with a word that reads them through. When the rest of Revelation later traces a controversy that comes down to a Sabbath issue (Studies 14-15), this is not a side-trail — it is the New Testament's own architectural marker surfacing.

[DYK🔎] Matthew lays out the gospel as a sanctuary walk. **Egypt → Passover** = altar of sacrifice. **Red Sea baptism** = laver. **Wilderness manna test** = table of shewbread (Christ's "Stone-to-Bread" wilderness test, Matt 4:3-4, lands here). **Sinai** = the Most Holy Place where the law was given. Then Matthew 5-7 — the Sermon on the Mount — opens with Christ on a mountain, sitting (Matt 5:1) like the Lawgiver, expanding the same ten commandments from a higher vantage. The Sermon on the Mount is _the Sermon from the Most Holy Place_. The voice from the ark, in flesh.

Before we leave the sanctuary, notice how saturated the second half of Daniel is with Day of Atonement imagery.

[DYK🔎] The ram in Dan 8 is not just attacked — Dan 8:7 says the he-goat "cast him down to the ground, and stamped upon him." Inside Daniel 8's Day-of-Atonement animal frame (ram + he-goat = Lev 16:5's pair), the sacrificial-victim side bears the marks of violence done to it. Now place that against Rev 5:6's throne-room: the One John sees in the midst of the throne is "a Lamb as it had been slain" — a Lamb visibly bearing the marks of having been killed. The crucified Christ — wounds and all — is the Christ who appears in the judgment. This is why Heb 9:24 says He "appears in the presence of God _for us_." For the believer, the judgment opens with the marks of Calvary already on the throne. The same Person fills both ends of Daniel 8's cleansing prophecy: the One trodden down at the start is the One in the midst of the throne at the end.

## Key Takeaways

- "Thy way, O God, is in the sanctuary" (Ps 77:13) — the sanctuary is Scripture's chosen vehicle for revealing how God saves
- The earthly sanctuary is a _pattern_ (_hypodeigma_) of the heavenly, not a replacement for it (Heb 8:1-5; 9:23-24 — defined above)
- Three compartments = three phases of Christ's work: **courtyard** (cross), **Holy Place** (continual intercession), **Most Holy** (Day of Atonement / judgment)
- The _tamid_ (continual daily ministry) covered morning/evening offerings, lamps, shewbread, incense — together they pictured Christ's continual intercession; Dan 8:11's "taking away of the daily" targets that symbol
- Lev 16's Day of Atonement protocol moves the high priest through all three compartments in one day; the chapter sits on top of the daily ministry already running
- The Day of Atonement cleansed _the sanctuary itself_ (Lev 16:16, 33) — the same operation Dan 8:14 names: "then shall the sanctuary be cleansed"
- Dan 8:14 + Lev 16:30 + Rev 14:6-7 describe one event from three angles; Rev 11:19 and Rev 15:5-8 complete the sanctuary thread
- 1844 is the opening of the heavenly Most Holy Place phase — not the Second Coming, not a new gospel, and not an event to dread for the believer
- The scapegoat (Azazel) is _not_ a Christ-symbol; it bears guilt _after_ atonement is complete, into "a land not inhabited" (Lev 16:22), a desolation picture Rev 20 will repeat literally
- Dan 8-12 is _framed_ by Day of Atonement imagery — animal set, linen garments, sanctuary cleansing, books of record; political sequence material runs alongside the priestly frame
- The center of the New Testament is **Christ as High Priest in the heavenly sanctuary** (defined above) — Hebrews + Rev 1 form the structural heart, with _sabbatismos_ (Heb 4:9) at the center of the center
- `[CTRF]` Pair #5: an **earthly papal sanctuary system** (priesthood, altar, recurring sacrifice, confessional, mediatrix) is built _over the top of_ the **heavenly sanctuary** (Heb 8-9), hiding what Christ is doing
- The crucified Christ — Lamb-as-it-had-been-slain (Rev 5:6) — is the Person who appears in the judgment; the ram cast down (Dan 8:7) and the Lamb in the midst of the throne are the same Person (Heb 9:24 — "for us")
