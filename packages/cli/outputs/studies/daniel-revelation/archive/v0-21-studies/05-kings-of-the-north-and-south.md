# Study 5: Kings of the North and South — Daniel 10-11

**Daniel's final vision is the most detailed predictive prophecy in the Bible. Three weeks of fasting. A Man clothed in linen. A twenty-one-day angelic war behind political decisions. Then a prophecy that walks from Persia through Greece through Rome all the way to "the time of the end" — naming kings' policies, marriages, wars, tax collectors, invasions, and betrayals so precisely that skeptics insist it must have been written _after_ the events. That skepticism is the strongest testimony to how specific the prophecy is. We will walk Dan 10:1 through Dan 11:45 verse by verse — confident where the text and history converge, and clear about where the end-time section is debated.**

## Prep Reading

- Daniel 10 (entire chapter)
- Daniel 11 (entire chapter)
- Jude 1:9
- Revelation 12:7-9
- 1 Thessalonians 4:16

---

## Before the Verses — The Single Vision That Frames Daniel 10-12

Daniel 10-12 is _one_ vision. The chapter divisions are later editorial; the prophet receives a single revelation that begins in Dan 10:1 and runs through Dan 12:13. The bookends frame the whole.

- **Dan 10:1-11:1** opens with "a certain man clothed in linen" by the Tigris (Hiddekel)
- **Dan 12:5-7** closes with "the man clothed in linen" above the waters of the river

Inside that frame, the prophecy moves: typical Persia (11:2) → typical Greece's north-vs-south (11:3-15) → **Rome at the center (11:16-22)** → anti-typical Greece's north-vs-south (11:23-40a) → anti-typical Persia, the final attack (11:40b-12:2). The Man in Linen — Christ our High Priest, established in Study 4 — is the structural bookend, and on the chiastic-center reading His own birth and crucifixion are encoded inside the Rome section: Augustus the "raiser of taxes" (Dan 11:20; cf. Lk 2:1) and "the prince of the covenant... shall be broken" (Dan 11:22b) on the cross. Daniel's prophecy keeps Christ at the center.

[Q] **"Why a single vision running across three chapters?"**
→ Because the Man in Linen is making _one_ point: every empire across this whole arc is stage-set for His own ministry. The Persian, Greek, and Roman phases — and beyond — exist to deliver the prophet (and us) to the High Priest at the center and the resurrection at the end (Dan 12:1-3). One vision, one Person, one trajectory.

## Dan 10:1 — A thing was revealed; the time appointed was long

> "In the third year of Cyrus king of Persia a thing was revealed unto Daniel, whose name was called Belteshazzar; and the thing was true, but the time appointed was long: and he understood the thing, and had understanding of the vision." (Dan 10:1)

- "Third year of Cyrus" — about 536/535 BC. Two years after Cyrus' decree that let the Jews return home (Ezra 1, ~538/537 BC). Daniel is now in his mid-eighties.
- The first wave of returnees has gone back. The rebuilding has stalled under local opposition (Ezra 4:1-5). Daniel is not with them — too old, perhaps — and he is grieving for the work that has stopped.
- "The thing was true, but the time appointed was long." Two qualities of every Daniel prophecy: it _will_ happen (true), and the timeframe stretches across centuries (long). Read every chapter through both lenses.
- "He understood the thing." Dan 8 ended with Daniel _astonished_ and the vision _not understood_ (Dan 8:27). Dan 9 corrected one piece. Dan 10-12 fills in much of the rest — though Dan 12:8 will show the final timing remains sealed beyond him until the time of the end.

## Dan 10:2-3 — Three full weeks of mourning

> "In those days I Daniel was mourning three full weeks. I ate no pleasant bread, neither came flesh nor wine in my mouth, neither did I anoint myself at all, till three whole weeks were fulfilled." (Dan 10:2-3)

- Twenty-one days of partial fasting. No pleasant food, no meat, no wine, no anointing oil. Like Lev 16:29-31's Day-of-Atonement self-affliction extended across three weeks.
- "Mourning" — _abal_, the same verb used for grieving the dead. Daniel grieves over the stalled rebuilding and the fragile community.
- The Man in Linen is about to come to a man who is already living the rhythm of the Day He serves. As we saw closing Study 4: the closer your life gets to His Day-of-Atonement work, the more clearly His face appears.

## Dan 10:4-6 — A certain man clothed in linen

> "And in the four and twentieth day of the first month, as I was by the side of the great river, which is Hiddekel; Then I lifted up mine eyes, and looked, and behold a certain man clothed in linen, whose loins were girded with fine gold of Uphaz: His body also was like the beryl, and his face as the appearance of lightning, and his eyes as lamps of fire, and his arms and his feet like in colour to polished brass, and the voice of his words like the voice of a multitude." (Dan 10:4-6)

- "Four and twentieth day of the first month" — the 24th of Nisan. Passover (14th) and Unleavened Bread (15th-21st) have just finished. Daniel's three weeks of fasting overran the festival; he kept mourning while Israel was supposed to be celebrating, because for him the deliverance was incomplete.
- "By the great river, which is Hiddekel" — the Tigris. Eden's third river (Gen 2:14). The Man appears at one of the rivers of paradise.
- "Clothed in linen" — _bad_, the same fabric Lev 16:4 specifies for the high priest on the Day of Atonement. The Man wears the Day-of-Atonement garments.
- "Whose loins were girded with fine gold of Uphaz" — golden girdle, like Rev 1:13's "girt about the paps with a golden girdle."
- "Body... like the beryl... face as lightning... eyes as lamps of fire... arms and feet like polished brass... voice... like the voice of a multitude." Five descriptors. Compare Rev 1:14-16 — "his eyes were as a flame of fire; And his feet like unto fine brass... and his voice as the sound of many waters... and his countenance was as the sun shineth in his strength." Feature for feature. **This is Christ.** The Old Testament's most extended Christophany, before Calvary.
- [→] Daniel 10 and Rev 1 show the same Christ — our High Priest — at two different moments. Dan 10 shows Him entering the priestly ministry of this final vision; Rev 1 shows Him conducting it among the candlesticks of the seven churches. Study 7 will return to this.

## Dan 10:7-9 — The men flee; Daniel falls as dead

> "And I Daniel alone saw the vision: for the men that were with me saw not the vision; but a great quaking fell upon them, so that they fled to hide themselves. Therefore I was left alone, and saw this great vision, and there remained no strength in me: for my comeliness was turned in me into corruption, and I retained no strength. Yet heard I the voice of his words: and when I heard the voice of his words, then was I in a deep sleep on my face, and my face toward the ground." (Dan 10:7-9)

- Daniel's companions sense something but do not see the figure. Compare Acts 9:7 — the men with Saul on the Damascus road heard the sound but did not see Christ. Vision is selective; presence is not.
- "My comeliness was turned in me into corruption." Daniel's natural strength is unmade by the Man's presence. The same effect Isa 6:5 reports ("Woe is me! for I am undone") and John reports in Rev 1:17 ("I fell at his feet as dead").
- The pattern: encounter with Christ → unmaking of self → strengthening from above. The mediator's first work in this vision is to take Daniel apart and put him back together.

## Dan 10:10-11 — A hand sets him on his knees

> "And, behold, an hand touched me, which set me upon my knees and upon the palms of my hands. And he said unto me, O Daniel, a man greatly beloved, understand the words that I speak unto thee, and stand upright: for unto thee am I now sent. And when he had spoken this word unto me, I stood trembling." (Dan 10:10-11)

- A different speaker now — likely the angelic messenger (Gabriel from Dan 8:16; 9:21), distinct from the Man in Linen. The Christophany has stunned Daniel; the angel resumes the dialogue.
- "Greatly beloved" — _chamudoth_, "preciously desired one." Three times Daniel hears this name in chapters 9-10 (Dan 9:23; 10:11; 10:19). Heaven's verdict on Daniel's character.
- "Understand the words... stand upright." The angel speaks Daniel back into posture. Worship-grade collapse is right; permanent collapse is not. Christ does not address ghosts; He addresses sons.

## Dan 10:12-13 — The 21-day delay

> "Then said he unto me, Fear not, Daniel: for from the first day that thou didst set thine heart to understand, and to chasten thyself before thy God, thy words were heard, and I am come for thy words. But the prince of the kingdom of Persia withstood me one and twenty days: but, lo, Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me; and I remained there with the kings of Persia." (Dan 10:12-13)

- "From the first day... thy words were heard." God answered the prayer the moment it left Daniel's lips. The delay was not in being heard.
- "The prince of the kingdom of Persia withstood me one and twenty days." The "prince of Persia" is not a human king — no human delays an angel. It is an angelic power aligned against God, working through the Persian court to shape policy against the Jews (cf. Eph 6:12 — "we wrestle... against principalities... against spiritual wickedness in high places").
- "Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me." Heaven's military reinforcement. The combat was not preached over the Persian court — it was waged. Cyrus' policies on Jewish rebuilding were the visible side of an invisible contest.
- The twenty-one days of Dan 10:13 and Daniel's three weeks of fasting (Dan 10:2-3) match by design. _Prayer moves heaven; heaven's answer sometimes has to move earth._
- [→] Compare Eph 6:12. Behind every political decision that affects God's people, there is angelic war. Daniel sees the wiring.

## Dan 10:14 — What shall befall thy people in the latter days

> "Now I am come to make thee understand what shall befall thy people in the latter days: for yet the vision is for many days." (Dan 10:14)

- "Latter days" — _achariyth ha-yamim_, the same idiom Dan 2:28 used for Nebuchadnezzar's image. The vision reaches to the end.
- "Yet for many days" — same expression Dan 8:26 used for the 2300-day vision: it stretches far. Dan 11 is not a near-term oracle for the 530s BC; it is a sweep across centuries.

## Dan 10:15-19 — Daniel strengthened a second time

> "And when he had spoken such words unto me, I set my face toward the ground, and I became dumb. And, behold, one like the similitude of the sons of men touched my lips: then I opened my mouth, and spake, and said unto him that stood before me, O my lord, by the vision my sorrows are turned upon me, and I have retained no strength. For how can the servant of this my lord talk with this my lord? for as for me, straightway there remained no strength in me, neither is there breath left in me. Then there came again and touched me one like the appearance of a man, and he strengthened me, And said, O man greatly beloved, fear not: peace be unto thee, be strong, yea, be strong. And when he had spoken unto me, I was strengthened, and said, Let my lord speak; for thou hast strengthened me." (Dan 10:15-19)

- Three touches — Dan 10:10, Dan 10:16, Dan 10:18. Heaven keeps coming back to put Daniel together. The mediator's care is patient.
- "Be strong, yea, be strong." Doubled command. Compare Joshua 1:6, 7, 9 — "be strong and of a good courage." Heaven's vocabulary for sending people into hard knowledge.
- "Let my lord speak; for thou hast strengthened me." The pattern: strengthening _precedes_ disclosure. God does not give heavy revelation to weak vessels; He strengthens first, then speaks.

## Dan 10:20-21 — Two more princes; the scripture of truth

> "Then said he, Knowest thou wherefore I come unto thee? and now will I return to fight with the prince of Persia: and when I am gone forth, lo, the prince of Grecia shall come. But I will shew thee that which is noted in the scripture of truth: and there is none that holdeth with me in these things, but Michael your prince." (Dan 10:20-21)

- "I will return to fight with the prince of Persia... the prince of Grecia shall come." Two angelic adversaries named — the spiritual side of the political handover from Persia to Greece that Dan 8 already showed historically. The political shift is the surface of an angelic war.
- "Michael your prince." Daniel's people have a defender. Dan 11 will name the political shifts; Dan 10:21 names the One holding the line for God's people throughout.
- "The scripture of truth" — Hebrew _ketav emet_. Heaven keeps records. There is a written script of truth, and the angel is about to read from it.

[DYK🔎] Michael appears three times by name in Daniel (Dan 10:13; 10:21; 12:1) and twice in the NT (Jude 1:9; Rev 12:7) — five named occurrences, every one of them in combat with Satan or as the defender of God's people. Jude 1:9 calls Him "the archangel." 1 Thess 4:16 says the Lord Himself descends "with the voice of the archangel." Michael (Heb. _Mi-ka-El_ = "who is like God?") is the pre-incarnate Christ acting as heaven's captain. The name itself poses the Christ-question: _who_ is like God? Answer: only the One who is God.

`[⟲]` **Michael thread:** Dan 10:13 (this link) → Dan 10:21 (same chapter, second appearance) → Dan 12:1 (Study 6) → Jude 9 → Rev 12:7 (Study 12). Five named appearances trace the Warrior-Prince's combat for God's people through both Testaments.

## Dan 11:1 — Standing to confirm and strengthen

> "Also I in the first year of Darius the Mede, even I, stood to confirm and to strengthen him." (Dan 11:1)

- The angel speaks as one who has been on the line for years. "First year of Darius the Mede" = ~538 BC, immediately after Babylon's fall. The angel was already standing then to "confirm and strengthen" — likely Darius/Cyrus, as God moved Persian policy toward releasing the Jews.
- The angel's testimony: he has been working the political wiring of empire on Israel's behalf since Babylon fell.

## Dan 11:2 — Four Persian kings; the fourth stirs up Greece

> "And now will I shew thee the truth. Behold, there shall stand up yet three kings in Persia; and the fourth shall be far richer than they all: and by his strength through his riches he shall stir up all against the realm of Grecia." (Dan 11:2)

- After Cyrus (already reigning): three more kings + one "far richer" who provokes Greece.
- Historically: **Cambyses** (530-522 BC) → **Pseudo-Smerdis** (522 BC) → **Darius I** (522-486 BC) → **Xerxes I** (486-465 BC) — the famously wealthy king who invaded Greece in 480 BC. His defeat at Salamis planted the grudge Alexander would later settle.
- Dan 11:2 predicts the exact sequence and the fourth's character about 55 years before Xerxes crossed the Hellespont. Some commentators list more Persian kings; Daniel highlights the four who shape the great-controversy turn from Persia to Greece, with Xerxes as the specific provoker.

## Dan 11:3-4 — Alexander rises; his empire splits four ways

> "And a mighty king shall stand up, that shall rule with great dominion, and do according to his will. And when he shall stand up, his kingdom shall be broken, and shall be divided toward the four winds of heaven; and not to his posterity, nor according to his dominion which he ruled: for his kingdom shall be plucked up, even for others beside those." (Dan 11:3-4)

- "A mighty king" — Alexander the Great (336-323 BC). Empire built in 13 years; collapse in months.
- "His kingdom shall be broken" — Alexander dies at 32 with no clear successor. His infant son and half-brother are murdered.
- "Divided toward the four winds of heaven" — exactly four successor kingdoms, just as Dan 8:8 already foretold: **Cassander** (Macedonia), **Lysimachus** (Thrace/Asia Minor), **Seleucus** (Syria/east), **Ptolemy** (Egypt).
- "Not to his posterity" — none of Alexander's family inherited. Both specifics (four-way division _and_ no family inheritance) named 200+ years in advance. Both happened.

## Before the verses — North, South, and the Glorious Land

Dan 11:5-20 narrows to two of Alexander's four successor kingdoms:

- **King of the south** = Ptolemaic Egypt (south of Israel)
- **King of the north** = Seleucid Syria (north of Israel)
- Israel sits in between — Dan 11:16 calls it "the glorious land" — so every time north and south collide, Israel is the battlefield

The titles shift as history advances. In the early verses, "north" and "south" name specific kingdoms. As those kingdoms are absorbed, the titles attach to whoever inherits the same _role_ — whoever is pressuring Israel from that direction. That is how the prophecy keeps its meaning as the political map changes.

[DYK🔎] North and south carry theological freight, not just geography. North = the heavens, the dwelling-place of God (Ps 48:2 — "Mount Zion, on the sides of the north"; Isa 14:13 — Lucifer's boast: "I will sit also upon the mount of the congregation, _in the sides of the north_"). South = the human/lower realm, with Egypt as the prototypical "house of bondage." So **king-of-the-north vs. king-of-the-south** is not a flat compass map; it is a **claim-of-divine-authority vs. man-exalting-axis** conflict written into the geography. Whoever presses on God's people while **claiming God's authority** is "the king of the north"; whoever presses from the **man-exalting side** is "the king of the south." Dan 11 is a chapter-long working-out of which power is sitting where on that axis.

## Dan 11:5 — King of the south strong; one of his princes stronger

> "And the king of the south shall be strong, and one of his princes; and he shall be strong above him, and have dominion; his dominion shall be a great dominion." (Dan 11:5)

- **Ptolemy I Soter** of Egypt (king of the south) is strong; **Seleucus I Nicator**, one of Alexander's successors who later served under Ptolemy before retaking Babylon, breaks away and builds the Seleucid empire that becomes greater than Ptolemy's own. ~323-280 BC.
- The verse's structure (south first, then a north that grows above the south) sets the pattern for the whole sequence.

## Dan 11:6 — A marriage that ends in murder

> "And in the end of years they shall join themselves together; for the king's daughter of the south shall come to the king of the north to make an agreement: but she shall not retain the power of the arm; neither shall he stand, nor his arm: but she shall be given up, and they that brought her, and he that begat her, and he that strengthened her in these times." (Dan 11:6)

- Ptolemy II's daughter **Berenice** married **Antiochus II Theos** (king of the north) as a peace treaty. ~250 BC.
- Antiochus II had to set aside his prior wife Laodice; she was banished, plotted, returned, and engineered the deaths of Antiochus II, Berenice, and Berenice's child and attendants. Berenice's father Ptolemy II had already died; her brother Ptolemy III then avenged her in vv. 7-9. The "agreement" cost _every_ figure named in v. 6.
- The "agreement" failed. Politics by marriage cannot bind what God has not joined.

## Dan 11:7-9 — Berenice's brother strikes back

> "But out of a branch of her roots shall one stand up in his estate, which shall come with an army, and shall enter into the fortress of the king of the north, and shall deal against them, and shall prevail: And shall also carry captives into Egypt their gods, with their princes, and with their precious vessels of silver and of gold; and he shall continue more years than the king of the north. So the king of the south shall come into his kingdom, and shall return into his own land." (Dan 11:7-9)

- "A branch of her roots" — Berenice's own brother, **Ptolemy III Euergetes** (246-221 BC).
- He invaded Syria 246-241 BC, captured the Seleucid capital, and carried Egyptian gods and treasures back to Egypt — gods that Cambyses had taken from Egypt centuries earlier. Dan 11:8 names the cargo: gods, princes, vessels of silver and gold.
- "He shall continue more years than the king of the north." Ptolemy III outlived Seleucus II by four years. Specific.

## Dan 11:10-12 — Sons of the north stirred up; the Battle of Raphia

> "But his sons shall be stirred up, and shall assemble a multitude of great forces: and one shall certainly come, and overflow, and pass through: then shall he return, and be stirred up, even to his fortress. And the king of the south shall be moved with choler, and shall come forth and fight with him, even with the king of the north: and he shall set forth a great multitude; but the multitude shall be given into his hand. And when he hath taken away the multitude, his heart shall be lifted up; and he shall cast down many ten thousands: but he shall not be strengthened by it." (Dan 11:10-12)

- Seleucus II's sons — **Seleucus III** (briefly) and **Antiochus III the Great** — "stirred up." Antiochus III campaigned aggressively from 222 BC.
- "The king of the south shall be moved with choler" — **Ptolemy IV Philopator** met Antiochus III at the Battle of Raphia (217 BC) with a massive army. The king of the north's "great multitude" was given into Ptolemy's hand — a stunning south-victory.
- "Heart lifted up... cast down many ten thousands: but he shall not be strengthened by it." Ptolemy IV won the battle and lost his empire's nerve. He returned to a life of vice; Egypt declined under him.

## Dan 11:13-15 — Antiochus III returns greater; takes the fenced cities

> "For the king of the north shall return, and shall set forth a multitude greater than the former, and shall certainly come after certain years with a great army and with much riches. And in those times there shall many stand up against the king of the south: also the robbers of thy people shall exalt themselves to establish the vision; but they shall fall. So the king of the north shall come, and cast up a mount, and take the most fenced cities: and the arms of the south shall not withstand, neither his chosen people, neither shall there be any strength to withstand." (Dan 11:13-15)

- "After certain years" — about 17 years later, Antiochus III returned with a far larger army.
- "Robbers of thy people shall exalt themselves to establish the vision; but they shall fall." Apostate Jews allied with Antiochus to throw off Ptolemaic rule, claiming to fulfill prophecy by force; they failed. **A pattern Daniel keeps surfacing: Israelites who try to make prophecy happen by political action lose.**
- **Battle of Panium, 200 BC.** Antiochus III crushed Egyptian forces; Sidon fell after siege. The "most fenced cities" were taken.

## Dan 11:16 — He shall stand in the glorious land

> "But he that cometh against him shall do according to his own will, and none shall stand before him: and he shall stand in the glorious land, which by his hand shall be consumed." (Dan 11:16)

- **198 BC.** Antiochus III takes Palestine from Ptolemaic control. "The glorious land" is now in northern hands.
- "By his hand shall be consumed" — Israel becomes the contested ground between the kingdoms; Jerusalem changes hands repeatedly; the land is exhausted.
- [→] "The glorious land" / "the pleasant land" appears at Dan 8:9, 11:16, 11:41, and points toward Dan 11:45's "the glorious holy mountain." The land where God's name is set keeps surfacing as the prophecy's pivot point.

## Dan 11:17 — The daughter of women, corrupting her

> "He shall also set his face to enter with the strength of his whole kingdom, and upright ones with him; thus shall he do: and he shall give him the daughter of women, corrupting her: but she shall not stand on his side, neither be for him." (Dan 11:17)

- **193 BC.** Antiochus III gave his daughter **Cleopatra I** to **Ptolemy V** in marriage, intending her to turn Egypt against itself from the inside.
- She sided with her husband and Egypt against her father. The plan failed.
- A second marriage-as-policy in Dan 11 (after Berenice in v. 6); a second failure.

## Dan 11:18-19 — Antiochus turns west; falls in a temple

> "After this shall he turn his face unto the isles, and shall take many: but a prince for his own behalf shall cause the reproach offered by him to cease; without his own reproach he shall cause it to turn upon him. Then he shall turn his face toward the fort of his own land: but he shall stumble and fall, and not be found." (Dan 11:18-19)

- "Turn his face unto the isles" — Asia Minor, the Aegean. Antiochus III's western campaign.
- "A prince for his own behalf" — **Lucius Cornelius Scipio (Asiaticus)**, with his brother Scipio Africanus advising. Battle of Magnesia, 190 BC. Earlier, **Manius Acilius Glabrio** had defeated Antiochus at Thermopylae (191 BC). Rome turns the reproach back on Antiochus.
- The Treaty of Apamea (188 BC) stripped Antiochus' western holdings.
- "Stumble and fall, and not be found." Antiochus III, broke and humiliated, was killed in 187 BC trying to plunder a temple of Bel at Elymaïs.
- [→] **Rome enters the prophecy as the agent that breaks the king of the north.** From Dan 11:18 forward, Rome is increasingly visible.

## Dan 11:20 — A raiser of taxes, then destroyed

> "Then shall stand up in his estate a raiser of taxes in the glory of the kingdom: but within few days he shall be destroyed, neither in anger, nor in battle." (Dan 11:20)

- **Seleucus IV Philopator** (187-175 BC) inherited a debt-burdened kingdom and dispatched his treasurer **Heliodorus** to plunder the Jerusalem temple — the canonical "raiser of taxes."
- "Within few days... destroyed, neither in anger, nor in battle" — Heliodorus assassinated him.
- [DYK🔎] On the chiastic-center reading of Dan 11, v. 20 _also_ telescopes forward. The Rome section of Dan 11 (vv. 16-22) sits at the chapter's center, and v. 20's "raiser of taxes in the glory of the kingdom" rhymes with **Caesar Augustus** of Lk 2:1 ("a decree from Caesar Augustus, that all the world should be taxed"). Dan 11's near layer is Hellenistic; the structural overlay points at the Augustus/Christ moment, with the next verse's "prince of the covenant... shall be broken" landing on Calvary. Both readings can hold — the immediate historical referent is Seleucus IV; the chiastic-center function of the verse points at Augustus.

## Dan 11:21-22 — A vile person, peaceful entry, the prince of the covenant broken

> "And in his estate shall stand up a vile person, to whom they shall not give the honour of the kingdom: but he shall come in peaceably, and obtain the kingdom by flatteries. And with the arms of a flood shall they be overflown from before him, and shall be broken; yea, also the prince of the covenant." (Dan 11:21-22)

- **Antiochus IV Epiphanes** (175-164 BC) is the immediate Hellenistic referent — he seized the throne deceptively after Seleucus IV's assassination, bypassing the legitimate heir.
- But **vv. 21-22 is the pivot where the king-of-the-north role transfers to Rome.** Antiochus IV is the surface; Rome is the trajectory. Three reasons:
  1. Dan 11:22's "prince of the covenant... shall be broken" finds its full weight only in **Christ crucified** (cf. Dan 9:25-26's "Messiah the Prince"). Onias III, the high priest deposed under Antiochus IV, is too small a referent for "the prince of the covenant" in a chapter whose architecture is leading to Calvary.
  2. The "arms of a flood" (massive, multi-armed military overflow) outstrips Antiochus IV's actual range and matches Rome's republican expansion.
  3. From v. 23 onward the king-of-the-north's behavior fits Rome and post-Constantinian/papal Rome, not Antiochus IV.
- **The transition is telescoping, not abrupt.** Dan 11 traces a single continuous line of northern anti-covenant power whose identity shifts as history advances: Seleucid → pagan Rome → papal Rome → final-phase king of the north.
- [→] "The prince of the covenant... shall be broken." Christ is the Prince of the new covenant; His death on the cross _is_ the cutting of that covenant (Dan 9:27's _karath_, Study 3). Dan 11:22 plants Calvary at the structural center of the chapter.

`[⟲]` **Sanctuary thread (one thread visible here).** Dan 11:22's "prince of the covenant... broken" lands the cross inside the same chapter that will say "take away the daily" (v. 31). The cross is the original; the daily-taken-away is the counterfeit. Lev 16 → Dan 8:11-14 → Dan 9:24-27 → Dan 11:22, 31 → Heb 8-9 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 15:5-8.

## Dan 11:23-24 — Strong with a small people; flattery and forecasting

> "And after the league made with him he shall work deceitfully: for he shall come up, and shall become strong with a small people. He shall enter peaceably even upon the fattest places of the province; and he shall do that which his fathers have not done, nor his fathers' fathers; he shall scatter among them the prey, and spoil, and riches: yea, and he shall forecast his devices against the strong holds, even for a time." (Dan 11:23-24)

- "Strong with a small people" — **Rome** began as a city-state. By treaty and alliance more than conquest, Rome built into a dominant power. The Roman _foedus_ (treaty) was the primary instrument.
- "Do that which his fathers have not done" — Rome introduced a way of building empire (alliance + selective force + assimilation) that the older Hellenistic kings did not practice.
- "Scatter among them the prey, and spoil, and riches" — Rome distributed the spoils of conquest as patronage, building loyal client networks across the Mediterranean.

## Dan 11:25-28 — Wars with the king of the south; heart against the holy covenant

> "And he shall stir up his power and his courage against the king of the south with a great army; and the king of the south shall be stirred up to battle with a very great and mighty army; but he shall not stand: for they shall forecast devices against him. Yea, they that feed of the portion of his meat shall destroy him, and his army shall overflow: and many shall fall down slain. And both these kings' hearts shall be to do mischief, and they shall speak lies at one table; but it shall not prosper: for yet the end shall be at the time appointed. Then shall he return into his land with great riches; and his heart shall be against the holy covenant; and he shall do exploits, and return to his own land." (Dan 11:25-28)

- Rome's wars with Egypt, climaxing in the Battle of Actium (31 BC) — Octavian against Mark Antony and Cleopatra. The end of the Ptolemaic kingdom.
- "Both these kings' hearts shall be to do mischief, and they shall speak lies at one table." The diplomatic theater of late-republican Rome and Hellenistic survivor states — alliances formed across tables that everyone knew were lies.
- "His heart shall be against the holy covenant" — Rome's posture toward the Jewish people. Pompey desecrated the temple in 63 BC; the exploits of Roman administration unfolded across the next century.

## Dan 11:29-30 — Ships of Chittim; indignation against the holy covenant

> "At the time appointed he shall return, and come toward the south; but it shall not be as the former, or as the latter. For the ships of Chittim shall come against him: therefore he shall be grieved, and return, and have indignation against the holy covenant: so shall he do; he shall even return, and have intelligence with them that forsake the holy covenant." (Dan 11:29-30)

- "Ships of Chittim" — _Kittim_, the western Mediterranean (Cyprus and beyond). Num 24:24 already used the phrase to mean a western maritime power that breaks Asshur (the eastern empire).
- The historical hinge: the "Day of Eleusis" (168 BC), when Roman envoy **Popillius Laenas** drew a circle in the sand around Antiochus IV near Alexandria and demanded he withdraw before stepping out of it — the famous "line in the sand." Antiochus retreated, full of fury, and turned that fury against the Jews on his way home: the desecrations of 167 BC that triggered the Maccabean revolt.
- **On the historicist transition reading:** Dan 11:29-30 names a moment when Rome itself begins to direct northern policy toward Israel. The near layer is Antiochus IV's reaction; the larger layer is the western power (Rome) increasingly steering events against the holy covenant.
- "Have intelligence with them that forsake the holy covenant" — collaboration with apostate insiders. Played out through Hellenized priestly factions in the 160s BC, Herodian and Sadducean compromises in the first century, and ultimately the handover of Christ to Rome (Jn 19:15 — "we have no king but Caesar").

## Dan 11:31 — Pollute the sanctuary; take away the daily; abomination set up

> "And arms shall stand on his part, and they shall pollute the sanctuary of strength, and shall take away the daily sacrifice, and they shall place the abomination that maketh desolate." (Dan 11:31)

- Three targets: (1) **pollute the sanctuary**, (2) **take away the daily**, (3) **set up the abomination that desolates**.
- **Antiochus IV** desecrated the Jerusalem temple in December 167 BC — sacrificed a pig on the altar, set up an altar to Zeus inside the sanctuary, stopped the daily Jewish offering. The near-historical referent is undeniable.
- **But Christ Himself reused this language for AD 70.** Matt 24:15 and Mark 13:14 quote "the abomination of desolation, spoken of by Daniel the prophet"; Luke 21:20 gives the parallel application — "when ye shall see Jerusalem compassed with armies, then know that the desolation thereof is nigh." Antiochus IV's desecration was a _type_ of the desolating principle that recurs.
- **Historicist trajectory:** the same pattern — northern power polluting the sanctuary, removing the daily, setting up an abomination — moves from Antiochus IV (167 BC, type) → pagan Rome at the temple (AD 70) → papal Rome's substitution of an earthly priesthood and recurring sacrifice for Christ's heavenly continual ministry. Each iteration is the same shape: an earthly system claiming the sanctuary's authority, hiding the heavenly original. Dan 8:11-14's "_tamid_ taken away" is the same operation viewed from another angle (Study 3).
- `[CTRF]` Pair #3 (registered, Study 3): **the daily taken away = counterfeit of Christ's continual heavenly intercession** (Heb 7:25, 8:1). The little horn / king of the north substitutes an earthly continual for the heavenly continual.

## Dan 11:32 — Corrupt by flatteries; people that know their God shall be strong

> "And such as do wickedly against the covenant shall he corrupt by flatteries: but the people that do know their God shall be strong, and do exploits." (Dan 11:32)

- Two responses to religious pressure — **compromise** ("corrupt by flatteries") or **stand** ("strong, and do exploits").
- "The people that do know their God." Not the people who know _about_ God; the people who _know_ Him. The same Hebrew construction Jeremiah uses for covenantal knowledge (Jer 9:24; 31:34).
- **The Maccabees** are the immediate near-history. Mattathias and his sons stood; their exploits broke Antiochus' control of Judea (164 BC, temple cleansed — the origin of Hanukkah).
- Across the long centuries, every faithful generation under pressure stands by the same dynamic: knowing God personally is what produces public strength.

## Dan 11:33-35 — Instructors fall by sword and flame; refining to the time of the end

> "And they that understand among the people shall instruct many: yet they shall fall by the sword, and by flame, by captivity, and by spoil, many days. Now when they shall fall, they shall be holpen with a little help: but many shall cleave to them with flatteries. And some of them of understanding shall fall, to try them, and to purge, and to make them white, even to the time of the end: because it is yet for a time appointed." (Dan 11:33-35)

- "Instruct many" — _maskilim_, "those who give understanding." Dan 12:3 picks this up: "they that be wise [_maskilim_] shall shine as the brightness of the firmament... and they that turn many to righteousness as the stars."
- "Fall by the sword, and by flame, by captivity, and by spoil, many days." Long persecution. Maccabean martyrdoms; early-church martyrs under Roman emperors; Dark Ages martyrs under papal pressure (the 1260 years already in view from Dan 7:25).
- "Helped with a little help... many shall cleave to them with flatteries." When persecution lifts, opportunists join the church for advantage — the Constantinian effect.
- "**To try them, to purge, and to make them white, even to the time of the end.**" The persecution has a refining purpose. The same vocabulary Daniel will repeat in 12:10 — "Many shall be purified, and made white, and tried." The faithful are not abandoned; they are forged.
- [→] Persecution's purpose is purification. Rev 13:5-7 (1260 days) and Rev 12:13-17 trace the same dynamic on the Revelation side.

## Before the verses — The Willful King (Dan 11:36-45)

This is the most debated section in Daniel. Honest historicism: the main identification has been worked through for centuries, with careful interpreters landing in different places. Three readings have weight.

- **View A: Papal Rome's final phase.** Held by a long stream of Protestant historicists (Keith, Barnes, and many later Adventist writers). The "king" is the same little horn of Dan 7, now in its final manifestation; Dan 11 continues to trace papal Rome from v. 31 through v. 45.
- **View B: A secular/atheistic power.** Uriah Smith's reading — v. 36's king is revolutionary/atheistic France and its successors; held by a stream of Adventist writers and modern historicists who take v. 37's "regard no god" at face value.
- **View C: A composite end-time coalition.** A final alliance of religious-political (papal Rome's revived influence) and secular power, pressuring God's people in the last crisis. Fits Rev 13's dragon + sea beast + earth beast and Rev 16:13's "three unclean spirits."

We will read the verses with the text's required features in view, note where each view finds its footholds, and commit to what the text demands.

## Dan 11:36 — Speaks marvellous things against the God of gods

> "And the king shall do according to his will; and he shall exalt himself, and magnify himself above every god, and shall speak marvellous things against the God of gods, and shall prosper till the indignation be accomplished: for that that is determined shall be done." (Dan 11:36)

- "Do according to his will" — total self-determination. The same phrase Dan 11:3 used of Alexander, but now turned against God.
- "Exalt himself, and magnify himself above every god" — self-deification.
- "Speak marvellous things against the God of gods" — direct echo of Dan 7:25's little horn ("speak great words against the most High"). **Same phrase-cluster, same target.** The willful king is in continuity with the Dan 7 little horn.
- "Shall prosper till the indignation be accomplished" — time-limited success. The king prospers _only_ until God's permitted period ends.
- [→] The Sabbath connection: Dan 7:25 includes "speak great words against the most High... and think to change times and laws." The willful king's blasphemy targets the Creator's authority over time — and the Sabbath is the seventh-day signature of that authority (Gen 2:1-3; Ex 20:8-11). Hitting "the God of gods" hits the Creator-Lord's signs in time.

## Dan 11:37 — Regards not the God of his fathers, nor the desire of women

> "Neither shall he regard the God of his fathers, nor the desire of women, nor regard any god: for he shall magnify himself above all." (Dan 11:37)

- "Regard not the God of his fathers" — rejects inherited religion.
- "Nor the desire of women" — _chemdat nashim_. Set in a list of things the king "regards not," it carries disregard for the institution of marriage / the male-female pairing God built into creation.
- "Nor regard any god" — read straight, this rejects all gods (View B's strongest foothold).
- "Magnify himself above all" — self above every category, including all worship.
- [→] Dan 11:36-37 attacks **both Creation institutions** in order: **the Sabbath** (v. 36 — "speak marvellous things... change times and laws," cf. Dan 7:25) and **marriage** (v. 37 — "neither... the desire of women"). Eden's two pillars hit consecutively. Sabbath = the image-in-time; marriage = the image-in-relationship (Gen 2:1-3; 2:21-24; cf. Eph 5:31-32). The willful king goes after the two creational signatures God left of Himself.

## Dan 11:38-39 — The god of forces

> "But in his estate shall he honour the God of forces: and a god whom his fathers knew not shall he honour with gold, and silver, and with precious stones, and pleasant things. Thus shall he do in the most strong holds with a strange god, whom he shall acknowledge and increase with glory: and he shall cause them to rule over many, and shall divide the land for gain." (Dan 11:38-39)

- "The God of forces" — _Eloah ma'uzzim_, "god of fortresses / strongholds / military power." Where the true God's authority operates by covenant and conscience, the counterfeit power's god operates by force.
- "Honour with gold, and silver, and with precious stones, and pleasant things." Compare Ezek 16:17 — Israel takes God's gold and silver and fashions "images of men." A god worshipped with material wealth and military glory.
- "Cause them to rule over many, and shall divide the land for gain." Land redistributed for political loyalty. Patronage in place of justice.
- [→] **The God of gods (v. 36) / the god of forces (v. 38).** Inside the willful king's own verses, Daniel sets up a parody pair as a corollary of the registered counterfeit-sanctuary system (CTRF pair #5, Study 4). The Creator is "the God of gods" — whose authority is signed into creation by Sabbath and marriage. The willful king "speaks marvellous things against" Him, then "honours the god of forces." The same parody pattern at work since Dan 3 (Nebuchadnezzar's image worshipped under threat of furnace) culminates here.

[Q] **"Why do all three views (papal, secular/atheistic, composite) find textual footholds in Dan 11:36-39?"**
→ Because the willful king is **two-headed**. Vv. 36-37 read as a parallel polarity, not a sequence:

```
v. 36b-37a    ANTI-CREATOR              "magnify himself above every god"
                                         "speak marvellous things against the God of gods"
                                         "neither shall he regard the God of his fathers"
                                                  ↕
v. 37b-38     PRO-MAN                   "nor the desire of women"
                                         "honour the god of forces"
                                         "a god whom his fathers knew not"
```

The willful king is **anti-Creator** _and_ **pro-man** in the same posture. Atheism that exalts the human becomes a religion of force; force-religion that exalts itself becomes atheism with rituals. The two collapse into each other. View A reads the religious head; View B reads the secular head; View C reads the composite. All three are reading the same two-headed power from different angles. The text demands a power blasphemous against God _and_ exalting man-and-force; whether that arrives as a continuous papal-Rome culmination, a secular successor, or an alliance, the chapter ends the same way: at "the glorious holy mountain," with "none to help him."

## Dan 11:40 — Time of the end: south pushes; north comes like a whirlwind

> "And at the time of the end shall the king of the south push at him: and the king of the north shall come against him like a whirlwind, with chariots, and with horsemen, and with many ships; and he shall enter into the countries, and shall overflow and pass over." (Dan 11:40)

- "**At the time of the end**" — same phrase used in Dan 8:17, 11:35. The clock has reached its last segment.
- "**The king of the south** shall push at him." A southern (man-exalting) push. Historicist readings have variously identified this as France of 1798 (Smith's reading), modern secular movements, or a final southern coalition.
- "**The king of the north** shall come against him like a whirlwind." A massive northern response — chariots, horsemen, ships, multi-domain force.
- The verse contains both the southern push _and_ the northern response. The last conflict opens.

## Dan 11:41 — He shall enter into the glorious land

> "He shall enter also into the glorious land, and many countries shall be overthrown: but these shall escape out of his hand, even Edom, and Moab, and the chief of the children of Ammon." (Dan 11:41)

- "The glorious land" — the same phrase from v. 16 and Dan 8:9. Israel — in physical and/or spiritual sense — is drawn into the final conflict.
- "Edom, and Moab, and the chief of the children of Ammon shall escape." Three peoples east of the Jordan, often historically opposed to Israel. Their escape has multiple historicist readings: literal regions outside the king's reach, or symbolic groups peripheral to the central conflict. The text demands they escape; the identification is a secondary detail.

## Dan 11:42-43 — Egypt, Libyans, Ethiopians at his steps

> "He shall stretch forth his hand also upon the countries: and the land of Egypt shall not escape. But he shall have power over the treasures of gold and of silver, and over all the precious things of Egypt: and the Libyans and the Ethiopians shall be at his steps." (Dan 11:42-43)

- "The land of Egypt shall not escape." The southern center falls under northern control.
- "Treasures of gold and silver... precious things of Egypt." Wealth captured.
- "Libyans and Ethiopians at his steps." North African peoples moving with him — either literal regions or symbolic for groups aligned with the final power.

## Dan 11:44 — Tidings out of the east and the north

> "But tidings out of the east and out of the north shall trouble him: therefore he shall go forth with great fury to destroy, and utterly to make away many." (Dan 11:44)

- News from "the east and the north" disturbs him. Historicists have read the east as biblical-direction shorthand for Christ's coming (Matt 24:27; Rev 7:2 — angel from the east) and/or for a literal eastern political development. The "tidings" are bad news for the willful king.
- His response: "great fury... utterly to make away many." Persecutory rage as the end closes in. Compare Rev 12:12 — Satan "knowing that he hath but a short time."

## Dan 11:45 — Between the seas, in the glorious holy mountain; none shall help him

> "And he shall plant the tabernacles of his palace between the seas in the glorious holy mountain; yet he shall come to his end, and none shall help him." (Dan 11:45)

- "Between the seas, in the glorious holy mountain." The dual-sea geography points at Israel — Mediterranean and Dead Sea — with "the glorious holy mountain" = Mount Zion.
- The Dan 8 chiasm (Study 3) paired the he-goat stamping the ram (Dan 8:7) with this ending: the chapter that opened with the sanctuary's enemy crushing the sacrifice closes with the enemy himself crushed at the holy mountain. **The end of Dan 11 mirrors and answers the beginning of Dan 8.**
- "**Yet he shall come to his end, and none shall help him.**" The willful king falls. No allies, no rescue. Compare Dan 7:11 (the beast's body destroyed, "given to the burning flame") and Rev 19:19-21 (the beast and false prophet thrown into the lake of fire).
- [→] Dan 11:45's fall and Dan 12:1's rising are back-to-back. _"At that time shall Michael stand up."_ The willful king's end is the cue for the resurrection.

[DYK🔎] "Between the seas, in the glorious holy mountain" places the final fall at/near Jerusalem/Zion — not a vague generic end-time battle. The location matters because the **next chapter opens with Christ acting and the resurrection beginning** (Dan 12:1-2). Whatever else is debated about vv. 40-45, the geographical and structural signals point toward the final confrontation _at_ the place where Christ's name is set, and the immediate sequel is the dead rising. Dan 11:45 ends the long northern aggression; Dan 12:1 begins eternal life.

## After the Verses — Why This Chapter Matters for the Reader

Dan 11 is not a collection of ancient trivia. It is a **test** and a **consolation**.

- **The test:** can God predict history accurately? Dan 11:2-20 is the answer. Verses this specific, written 350+ years in advance (Daniel's 536/535 BC setting to Seleucus IV's death in 175 BC), strain accidental-prediction explanations. Either Daniel is divinely given, or critical scholars must insist on a late (2nd-century BC) authorship — which still does not explain the material this study reads beyond the Antiochus layer: Rome's centuries, the cross, and the papal rise. No one in 165 BC could see those.
- **The consolation:** God is not surprised. Empires rise, wars roll over Israel, alliances shift, persecution stretches "many days" — and every move is tracked in advance. "My counsel shall stand, and I will do all my pleasure" (Isa 46:10). The faithful are "purged" and "made white" (v. 35), not abandoned. The willful king of v. 45 "shall come to his end, and none shall help him" — nothing pursuing the saints has the last word.

[→] If you are tempted to panic about current events, Dan 11 is the prescription. **The details change. The pattern does not. The end is written.**
