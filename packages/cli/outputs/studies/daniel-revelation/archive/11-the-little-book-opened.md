# Study 11: The Little Book Opened — Revelation 10-11

**Between the sixth and seventh trumpets, everything pauses. A mighty angel descends clothed in a cloud, a rainbow on his head, his face like the sun, his feet like pillars of fire. In his hand is _a little book open_ — the book Daniel was told to seal "till the time of the end." The angel sets one foot on the sea and one on the land, raises his hand to heaven, and swears by the living God that "there should be time no longer." John is told to eat the book. It is sweet in his mouth and bitter in his belly. Then he is told: "Thou must prophesy again." In those two chapters Revelation narrates the Advent movement — its 1844 expectation, its disappointment, and its second-stage mission. Then chapter 11 gives us the two witnesses in sackcloth for 1260 years, killed for three-and-a-half days in the streets of "the great city," and raised in the Reformation's aftermath. The seventh trumpet sounds at last: "The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord." The Most Holy Place opens. The judgment begins.**

## Prep Reading

- Revelation 10 (entire chapter)
- Revelation 11 (entire chapter)
- Daniel 12:4-9 (the sealed book)
- Ezekiel 2:9-3:4 (eating the scroll)

## The Mighty Angel with the Little Book

Revelation's narrative stops. Before the seventh trumpet sounds, a scene is inserted.

- **The angel** (Rev 10:1-3)
  - "Another mighty angel come down from heaven, clothed with a cloud: and a rainbow was upon his head, and his face was as it were the sun, and his feet as pillars of fire"
  - "He had in his hand a little book open"
  - "He set his right foot upon the sea, and his left foot on the earth"
  - "Cried with a loud voice, as when a lion roareth"
  - The description matches Christ Himself: clothed with a cloud (Rev 1:7; Dan 7:13); rainbow on the head (Rev 4:3 — the rainbow around the throne); face as the sun (Rev 1:16); feet as pillars of fire (Rev 1:15). Many historicist/SDA interpreters read this as Christ appearing in angelic/messenger form to deliver a post-incarnation announcement about the open book

- **"A little book open"** (Rev 10:2)
  - The Greek is _biblaridion_ — a diminutive form, a _little_ book, distinguished from the sealed scroll (_biblion_) of Rev 5:1 that the Lamb took from the Father's hand
  - This little book is _open_. Something that was previously closed has been unsealed
  - Historicist reading: this is the book Daniel was told to seal "till the time of the end" (Dan 12:4, 9). By Rev 10, the time of the end has come, and the book is open. The prophecies Daniel could not yet apply — the 2300 days, the closing parts of the time-line — are now open for the people of God to read

- **The foot on sea and land** (Rev 10:2)
  - The angel claims universal jurisdiction — the whole earth and sea fall under His authority
  - Echo of Dan 12:7 where the man in linen stood above the river's waters and swore "by him that liveth for ever"
  - In fact, Rev 10's oath (vv. 5-6) _reproduces_ Dan 12:7 — same gesture, same oath-formula, now being _answered_

- **"Time no longer"** (Rev 10:6)
  - "Sware by him that liveth for ever and ever... that there should be time no longer"
  - The Greek _chronos_ can mean time generally or delay; modern translations often render it "delay no longer." In historicist/SDA reading, because Rev 10 so closely echoes Dan 12:7's oath above the river, _chronos_ is read as the close of further prophetic time-periods (not the end of history itself)
  - On this reading, after the 2300-day prophecy runs out (1844), no new prophetic time-period is to be calculated toward. The great prophetic clock has run out; what remains is the unfolding of the final events
  - This is why post-1844 attempts to set new date-prophecies have been, in the Adventist tradition, consistently discouraged

- Dan 12:4 → Rev 10:2 → Rev 22:10 is its own sealed-book pattern (not a registered recapitulation chain): Daniel's book is sealed, Rev 10 opens it, Rev 22 forbids sealing it again. Worth seeing; not mandatory breadcrumb material.

[DYK🔎] **Living Word / Written Word — Rev 10 and Rev 11 are paired.** The same Living-Word / Written-Word pairing that structured Rev 7 (Study 9 — 144,000 sealed with the Living Word, Great Multitude sealed with the Written Word) returns here in larger form. Rev 10:1-11:1 — the mighty angel with the open little book — reads structurally as the _Living Word_ scene: Christ Himself (the descriptors match Rev 1:15-16), holding the open book, planting His foot on sea and land, swearing by the living God. Rev 11:3-13 — the two witnesses in sackcloth and their resurrection — reads as the _Written Word_ scene: the two Testaments (in Smith's reading) prophesying through the long persecution, killed and raised. Rev 10 is Christ holding His Word open. Rev 11 is His Word doing the work in the world while He holds it open. Forward: Rev 14:12's remnant "keep the commandments of God _and_ have the testimony of Jesus" — Living Word and Written Word together in the people who will not bend at the end.

[DYK🔎] **The book opens at the time of the end — and Rev 10 places itself _this side_ of that opening.** Dan 12:4 commanded: "shut up the words, and seal the book, even to the time of the end: many shall run to and fro, and knowledge shall be increased." Smith reads Rev 10's open book as Daniel's book — but the chronology is what's striking. The angel is described as already standing with the book _open_; the book's opening is presupposed, not announced. So Rev 10's announcement must be sounded "this side of the commencement of the time of the end" (Smith DAR 489.1). The 1798 endpoint of the 1260 days had cracked the seal — the prophetic book of Daniel was now readable in a way it had not been for a thousand years — and within a generation William Miller and others were proclaiming what Rev 10's angel had been holding open all along. The "knowledge shall be increased" of Dan 12:4 is the _running and reading_ of an opened book.

## The Seven Thunders Sealed

Before the angel's great announcement, something else happens.

- **Rev 10:3-4** — "Seven thunders uttered their voices"
- "When the seven thunders had uttered their voices, I was about to write: and I heard a voice from heaven saying unto me, Seal up those things which the seven thunders uttered, and write them not"

- **What the thunders are**
  - John hears and understands. He is about to record. Heaven stops him: _do not write this_
  - This is the _only_ material in Revelation John is explicitly told to seal. The seven thunders' content is withheld from the reader
  - Various historicist interpretations: (a) the specifics of events leading up to and through 1844 that were revealed to the Advent pioneers in process but not prescribed in the fixed text; (b) details of the final crisis that were not yet the church's to know; (c) a deliberate gap marking the limit of even this open book

- **The principle**
  - Even in the book that unseals Daniel's prophecies, God retains the right to seal some things. Revelation gives the church what the church needs to do its work, not everything the apostle saw
  - The seven thunders' silence should chasten any reader who claims to have figured everything out

## The Bittersweet Book

The angel has announced the oath. Now John is told to participate.

- **John eats the book** (Rev 10:8-10)
  - "Go and take the little book which is open in the hand of the angel"
  - "Take it, and eat it up; and it shall make thy belly bitter, but it shall be in thy mouth sweet as honey"
  - "I took the little book out of the angel's hand, and ate it up; and it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter"

- **Eating the book — the Ezekiel precedent** (Eze 2:9-3:4)
  - Ezekiel was given a scroll "written within and without: and there was written therein lamentations, and mourning, and woe" (Eze 2:9-10)
  - Eze 3:3 — "Eat this roll... Then did I eat it; and it was in my mouth as honey for sweetness"
  - To eat the book = to _internalize_ the prophetic message before proclaiming it. The messenger must first be filled with the word he is to carry
  - Rev 10 adds what Ezekiel did not have: the _bitter_ aftertaste

- **The Advent-movement reading**
  - Historicist/SDA reading: the sweet-then-bitter experience describes the Millerite/Advent movement's encounter with the open book of Daniel between ~1831 and 1844
  - _Sweet in the mouth_: the Advent hope. The 2300-day calculation opened. The pioneers — Miller, Wolff, Lacunza, Irving, Fitch, Himes — preached a soon coming of the Lord. Revivals spread. Thousands converted. The message was honey-sweet to those who received it

[DYK🔎] **The 1840 expectation was not just a New England movement.** Joseph Wolff, the "missionary to the world" (a converted Jew turned Anglican missionary), traveled through Persia, Bokhara, Afghanistan, India, Yemen, and Abyssinia preaching the second coming. In his own _Journal of the Rev. Joseph Wolff_, he records finding remote Asian peoples — Arabs at Yemen, Jewish tribes in Bokhara — who already held the expectation that great events would occur "in the year 1840." Lacunza, a Jesuit in Spain, had reached similar conclusions a generation earlier. Edward Irving was preaching the same expectation from London. The honey-sweet 1830s-1840s expectation was a remarkably global phenomenon — the sealed book of Daniel had cracked open in multiple places at once. The Advent movement that the Adventist tradition centers in upstate New York was the most documented of those branches, but not the only one.

- _Bitter in the belly_: the Great Disappointment. October 22, 1844, came and Christ did not visibly return. The expectation had been right about the _date_ (the 2300 days ended) but wrong about the _event_ (they expected the Second Coming; what actually happened was the opening of the heavenly Most Holy Place — see Study 3)
- The bitterness was the disappointment's public humiliation. The movement lost most of its adherents. Those who remained had to rethink what the prophecy had actually predicted

- **"Thou must prophesy again"** (Rev 10:11)
  - "Thou must prophesy again before many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings"
  - After the disappointment, the _same_ movement is commissioned to prophesy _again_ — not a new prophecy, but a continuation, now with a corrected understanding
  - This is Rev's own commentary on what followed 1844: the post-disappointment remnant studied the sanctuary prophecy again, recognized that 1844 had begun the heavenly Day of Atonement (not the Second Coming), and took up a worldwide mission to "many peoples, and nations, and tongues, and kings." The same fourfold formula (nation/kindred/tongue/people, in varied order) returns in Rev 14:6 when the first angel flies with the everlasting gospel — linking Rev 10's post-disappointment commission to Rev 14's global proclamation

- [→] Rev 10 is Revelation's own narration of what the Advent movement would walk through. The pioneers discovered, _during_ and _after_ the disappointment, that Rev 10 had been describing their own experience all along. SDA historicists see this not as a detached proof-text, but as a pattern the Advent movement recognized from within its own experience as the events unfolded

[DYK🔎] The "ate the book" image is not unique to John. Jeremiah also ate God's words: "Thy words were found, and I did eat them; and thy word was unto me the joy and rejoicing of mine heart" (Jer 15:16). Ezekiel ate a scroll (Eze 3:1-3). John eats the little book (Rev 10:9-10). In every case the prophet internalizes the word before proclaiming it. The prophet cannot carry what he has not first consumed. Rev 10 adds the bitter layer: the message will taste sweet because it is true, and bitter because its unfolding will include disappointment — the messengers' own and the world's.

## The Two Witnesses — Rev 11:1-14

Rev 11 opens with John measuring the temple — the scene shifts from the little book to the long witness of the Word under the 1260-year persecution.

- **The measuring** (Rev 11:1-2)
  - "There was given me a reed like unto a rod: and the angel stood, saying, Rise, and measure the temple of God, and the altar, and them that worship therein"
  - "But the court which is without the temple leave out, and measure it not; for it is given unto the Gentiles: and the holy city shall they tread under foot forty and two months"
  - _Measuring_ in OT prophecy (Eze 40-43; Zech 2) = surveying for judgment and restoration
  - Historicist reading: the measuring corresponds to the investigative judgment (Study 3) — the assessment of the temple, altar, and worshipers begins the post-1844 Most Holy Place work. The outer court is "given to the Gentiles" for 42 months — the 1260-year persecution period now viewed from the sanctuary's side

- **Forty-two months = 1260 years**
  - `[⟲]` 1260 / 42 months / time, times, half a time — Dan 7:25 (little horn); Dan 12:7 (sealed book); Rev 11:2 (outer court trodden); Rev 11:3 (two witnesses in sackcloth); Rev 12:6 (woman in wilderness); Rev 12:14 (same, as time/times/half); Rev 13:5 (beast's authority). Seven textual references across six scene-units — AD 538 to 1798 (see Study 6)
  - The consistency across six visions is the point: the same medieval persecution period is traced from multiple angles (the little horn's suppression of saints, the sealed understanding, the outer-court treading, the sackcloth witnesses, the wilderness woman, the beast's authority)

- **The two witnesses** (Rev 11:3-6)
  - "I will give power unto my two witnesses, and they shall prophesy a thousand two hundred and threescore days, clothed in sackcloth"
  - "These are the two olive trees, and the two candlesticks standing before the God of the earth" (Rev 11:4) — quoting Zech 4:2-3, 14 (the two anointed ones)
  - Powers: "fire proceedeth out of their mouth, and devoureth their enemies"; they shut heaven (cf. Elijah, 1 Kgs 17:1); turn waters to blood and smite with plagues (cf. Moses)
  - Historicist/SDA reading: the two witnesses = Scripture itself, especially the Old Testament (Law and Prophets) and the New Testament (Gospels and Apostolic Writings) — the two portions of the Bible that testify of Christ (Luke 24:44; John 5:39). Some historicists identify the witnesses more broadly with the whole witnessing church across the persecution period; the Scripture identification is the dominant SDA reading, taken up by Uriah Smith and common in the wider historicist tradition. Smith himself names two main sources behind his Rev 11 chapter: George Croly for the OT/NT identification of the witnesses, and George Storrs ("for the statistics and many of the foregoing thoughts on the two witnesses, we are indebted to an exposition of the subject of The Two Witnesses, by the late George Storrs" — DAR 505.1). Storrs (1796-1879) was a contemporary Millerite/Adventist preacher; Croly (1780-1860) an Anglican commentator. Smith's Rev 11 chapter is a synthesis from both — neither was Smith's invention
  - The "sackcloth" for 1260 days = the medieval suppression of Scripture. During the 538-1798 period, the Bible was largely inaccessible to ordinary people: vernacular translation was forbidden; preaching outside ecclesiastical channels was persecuted; reading Scripture without the church's interpretation was a capital offense in several jurisdictions
  - Throughout the period, Scripture _prophesied_ — speaking through the Waldenses, Wycliffe, Huss, Tyndale, the Reformation martyrs. "Clothed in sackcloth" — witnessing in mourning, under suppression, but still witnessing

- **The beast kills the witnesses** (Rev 11:7-10)
  - "When they shall have finished their testimony, the beast that ascendeth out of the bottomless pit shall make war against them, and shall overcome them, and kill them"
  - "Their dead bodies shall lie in the street of the great city, which spiritually is called Sodom and Egypt, where also our Lord was crucified"
  - "They of the people and kindreds and tongues and nations shall see their dead bodies three days and an half, and shall not suffer their dead bodies to be put in graves"
  - "They that dwell upon the earth shall rejoice over them... because these two prophets tormented them that dwelt on the earth"
  - Historicist reading: this episode corresponds to the public repudiation of Scripture during the French Revolution's dechristianizing phase
    - During the Revolution's dechristianizing wave (especially late 1793 and into 1794), Parisian and provincial authorities closed churches, adopted the republican calendar (replacing the Lord's Day with a ten-day _décade_), and staged the Festival of Reason at Notre-Dame on November 10, 1793. The campaign was intense but uneven across regions and not a single formal national abolition by the Convention itself; Robespierre's decree of a Supreme Being (May 1794) and the Thermidorian reaction then shifted policy. Freedom of worship was reestablished by the Convention on February 21, 1795, and churches began reopening through 1795-1797. The 1801 Concordat formalized the restoration
    - France had been one of the "ten horns" of Dan 7's beast; the same nation long identified as the "eldest daughter of the church" publicly turned against Scripture during the height of the dechristianizing phase
    - Historicist writers (Keith, Smith, Froom) computed a 3.5-year period in this window and read it as the prophecy's fulfillment; the exact start/stop dates vary by reader (typical anchors: late 1793 / early 1794 to 1797 restorations), and this is the traditional historicist computation rather than a single clean mainstream chronology
    - "Sodom and Egypt, where also our Lord was crucified" — a spiritual designation for a place that publicly mocks Christ; France's revolutionary capital fit the pattern in the historicist reading

- **The resurrection** (Rev 11:11-13)
  - "After three days and an half the Spirit of life from God entered into them, and they stood upon their feet; and great fear fell upon them which saw them"
  - "They ascended up to heaven in a cloud; and their enemies beheld them"
  - Historicist reading: the revival of Scripture after the French Revolution — the Bible Society movement
    - British and Foreign Bible Society founded March 7, 1804
    - American Bible Society's founding convention opened May 8 and adopted its public address May 11, 1816
    - Over the 19th century, Bible translation and distribution went from a restricted trade to a global movement; by the century's end the Bible was being translated into hundreds of languages
    - The "ascent in a cloud" corresponds to the Bible's rise to unprecedented global visibility — the inverse of its medieval suppression

- [→] The witnesses were killed in public in 1793 and rose in public a few years later. The same Revolution that tried to bury Scripture became the turning point after which Scripture spread more widely than at any prior point in human history. "They ascended up to heaven in a cloud; and their enemies beheld them."

## The Seventh Trumpet

At last, after the interlude of Rev 10-11, the seventh trumpet sounds.

- **The announcement** (Rev 11:15-19)
  - "The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever"
  - "The twenty-four elders... fell upon their faces, and worshipped God, Saying, We give thee thanks, O Lord God Almighty, which art, and wast, and art to come; because thou hast taken to thee thy great power, and hast reigned"
  - "The nations were angry, and thy wrath is come, and the time of the dead, that they should be judged"
  - "The temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament: and there were lightnings, and voices, and thunderings, and an earthquake, and great hail"

- **"The time of the dead, that they should be judged"**
  - The seventh trumpet announces the judgment — exactly the work Dan 7:9-14 showed, Study 2 traced, Study 3 dated to 1844
  - `[⟲]` **Judgment thread:** Dan 7:9-14 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:15-19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 20:11-12. Rev 11:15-19 is the third link.

- **"The temple of God was opened in heaven... the ark of his testament"** (Rev 11:19)
  - The ark was in the _Most Holy Place_ of the earthly sanctuary (Heb 9:3-4) — behind the second veil, accessible only on the Day of Atonement, and only to the high priest
  - Rev 11:19 describes the Most Holy Place _opened_ — the same transition Dan 8:14's "cleansing of the sanctuary" prophesied, the same event that occurred at the end of the 2300 days in 1844
  - The ark contains the _testament_ — the Ten Commandments (Ex 25:16; Deut 10:5). The Most Holy Place's opening puts the law of God at the center of the final visible phase
  - `[⟲]` **Sanctuary thread:** Lev 16 → Dan 8:11-14 → Heb 8-9 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 15:5-8. Rev 11:19 is the sanctuary thread's fifth link — the Most Holy Place opened at the trumpets' climax

- [→] The seventh trumpet is not a description of the final end. It is the announcement of the transition to the final phase — the judgment begins, the Most Holy Place opens, the law of God is unveiled at the center of the ark. Rev 12-14 will tell us what happens next on earth: the dragon's war, the two beasts, the three angels' messages, the mark and the seal.
- [→] But before turning the page: the heart-test of Rev 10-11 is not the chronology. It is the bittersweet pattern. Sweet in the mouth, bitter in the belly. Most Christian lives have at least one episode of it: a hope God seemed to give, that broke in the way no one was ready for; a calling that tasted right, then turned sour in the carrying of it; a prayer answered in a way that left the answerer wounded. The Advent pioneers stood at the place where the easy answer would have been to renounce the prophecy and walk away. They did not. They went back to the text, found their error in the _event_ rather than the _date_, and were told from the same book that had broken them: "thou must prophesy again." Where in your own walk has the sweet turned bitter — and did you let bitterness end the story, or did you let the same Word that broke your expectation reshape it?

## Key Takeaways

- Rev 10's "little book open" (_biblaridion_) is the book Daniel was told to seal "till the time of the end" (Dan 12:4); by Rev 10, the time of the end has come
- The angel's oath "time no longer" (Rev 10:6) = no further prophetic time-period will follow the 2300 days; the prophetic clock has run out
- The seven thunders are the _only_ material in Revelation John is explicitly forbidden to write — a sealed pocket within the open book
- The bittersweet book (Rev 10:9-11) = the Millerite Advent expectation (sweet, 1831-1844) and the Great Disappointment (bitter, October 22, 1844); "thou must prophesy again" = the post-1844 continued mission
- Rev 11's measuring of the temple (11:1) corresponds to the investigative judgment beginning in 1844
- The two witnesses in sackcloth 1260 days = OT and NT Scripture under the 538-1798 suppression (SDA-dominant historicist reading); the registered 1260-day chain (Dan 7:25 → Dan 12:7 → Rev 11:2 → Rev 11:3 → Rev 12:6 → Rev 12:14 → Rev 13:5) traces seven textual references across six scene-units
- The killing of the witnesses in "the great city" (Rev 11:7-10) = the French Revolution's atheistic public rejection of Scripture (1793-1797)
- Their resurrection (Rev 11:11-12) = the Bible Society movement (British and Foreign Bible Society 1804, American Bible Society 1816) and the 19th-century global Bible distribution
- The seventh trumpet (Rev 11:15-19) announces the kingdom, the judgment of the dead, and the opening of the Most Holy Place and the ark
- The registered Judgment chain (Dan 7:9-14 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:15-19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 20:11-12) reaches its third link here. The registered Sanctuary chain (Lev 16 → Dan 8:11-14 → Heb 8-9 → Rev 4-5 → Rev 11:19 → Rev 14:6-7 → Rev 15:5-8) reaches its fifth link — the Most Holy Place opened at the trumpets' climax

With the Most Holy Place opened and the judgment announced, Rev turns back to tell us the deepest story: the cosmic war behind all the history we have traced. A woman clothed with the sun. A dragon with seven heads. A man child caught up to God's throne. A war in heaven. A flight into the wilderness for 1260 days. And a final declaration about who the dragon is pursuing at the end: "the remnant of her seed, which keep the commandments of God, and have the testimony of Jesus Christ." Study 12 is Revelation's panorama of the great controversy in a single chapter.
