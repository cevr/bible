# Daniel 7 — The Four Beasts and the Ancient of Days

> "I saw in the night visions, and, behold, one like the Son of man came with the clouds of heaven, and came to the Ancient of days, and they brought him near before him." — Dan 7:13

## Where this chapter sits

- **Dan 2** — the master timeline shown as an _image_: Babylon → Medo-Persia → Greece → Rome → divided Rome → kingdom of God.
- **Dan 7** — _the same span_ told as four _beasts_, with the _little horn_ added — and the heavenly _Ancient of Days courtroom_ opened. About fifty years after Dan 2.
- **Dan 8-9** — the sequence retold from Medo-Persia onward, with the _sanctuary_ and the _2300 days_ added.
- **Dan 11-12** — surgical political detail walking from Persia to the end.
- Dan 7 is the master prophetic chapter of the book and the foundation for Rev 13, Rev 14, and the investigative judgment.

> [⟲] **Recapitulation thread (link 2).** Dan 7 is Dan 2 _retold from a different angle_. Same kingdoms. Different vantage. The structural key of the entire book is being demonstrated here: the same span keeps getting retold at higher resolution.

> [⟲] **Judgment courtroom thread (link 3).** Dan 5 staged the judgment of one empire (numbered, weighed, divided). Dan 7 opens the _heavenly_ court that sits over _all_ empires — and Rev 4-5, Rev 14:6-7, Rev 20:11-12 will revisit this same scene from progressively closer angles.

---

## Two structural facts before verse 1

- **Out-of-order chronology.** Dan 7 is dated "in the first year of Belshazzar" (Dan 7:1, c. 553 BC) — _earlier_ than Dan 5, which describes Belshazzar's fall (539 BC), and Dan 6, which follows under Darius. The book deliberately breaks chronology to put Dan 7 last in its Aramaic block.
- **Two languages.** Dan 1 and Dan 8-12 are Hebrew. Dan 2-7 — the six chapters in the middle — are Aramaic, the diplomatic and court language of Babylon. The book switches at Dan 2:4 and switches back at Dan 8:1.

The shape that emerges is a chiasm:

```
Ch. 2          Ch. 3            Ch. 4-5            Ch. 6            Ch. 7
Prophecy       Persecution      Babylon's          Persecution      Prophecy
(image)        (furnace)        kings              (lions' den)     (beasts +
                                humbled +                            judgment)
                                fall
A              B                C                  B'               A'
```

> [KEY🔑] **The Aramaic chiasm.** The middle section of Daniel is a deliberate structural unit. Prophecy brackets persecution (A/A'). Persecution brackets Babylon's collapse (B/B'). At the center: Babylon's kings humbled (Dan 4) and Babylon's empire ended (Dan 5). Read along the centerline outward: Babylon collapses (C), the saints get tested (B/B'), and bracketing it all is _prophecy that lets them stand_ (A/A'). Prophecy is not given as decoration. It is given so the saints can stand through persecution while Babylon collapses on schedule.

> [DYK🔎] Babylon means _confusion_ (Gen 11:9 — "called Babel; because the LORD did there confound the language of all the earth"). The Aramaic block is, by language, _Babylon's section of Daniel_ — and what Babylon's own court tongue is forced to record is Babylon's certain fall. The book lets the empire write its own end in its own language.

## Dan 7:1 — The dream is given and written down

> "In the first year of Belshazzar king of Babylon Daniel had a dream and visions of his head upon his bed: then he wrote the dream, and told the sum of the matters" (Dan 7:1)

- Belshazzar's first year is c. 553 BC. Daniel — taken to Babylon as a teenager around 605 BC — is now an old man, about fifty years after the Dan 2 image.
- "He wrote the dream" — not memory, not oral tradition. The vision is documented; the writing is part of how it is preserved as Scripture.
- [→] God gives the same prophecy a second time. The _repetition_ is the method, not the redundancy.

---

## Dan 7:2-3 — Four winds, the great sea, four beasts

> "Daniel spake and said, I saw in my night vision, and, behold, the four winds of the heaven strove upon the great sea. And four great beasts came up from the sea, diverse one from another" (Dan 7:2-3)

- _Wind_ in Scripture often pictures strife and conflict (Jer 25:32-33; Jer 49:36 — "I will bring the four winds from the four quarters of heaven... and there shall be no nation whither the outcasts of Elam shall not come"). Four winds striving = political turmoil.
- _The great sea_ — Rev 17:15 will define waters: "The waters which thou sawest, where the whore sitteth, are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues." Bible interprets Bible. The four beasts arise out of populated, contested territory.
- [→] The whole vision is anchored in real history, not in private mythology. Empires rise out of conflict among the nations.

---

## Dan 7:4 — The lion with eagle's wings (Babylon)

> "The first was like a lion, and had eagle's wings: I beheld till the wings thereof were plucked, and it was lifted up from the earth, and made stand upon the feet as a man, and a man's heart was given to it" (Dan 7:4)

- A lion is the king of beasts; an eagle is the king of birds. Babylon was both at once. Babylon used lion imagery prominently; striding lions lined the Processional Way associated with the Ishtar Gate.
- The _wings plucked_ and the _man's heart given_ are a compressed biography of Nebuchadnezzar himself: pride (lion + wings), humiliation when his reason was taken and he was driven into beast-like existence (Dan 4:33), restoration when his understanding returned (Dan 4:34, "made to stand"). Daniel's readers had lived through that arc.
- [→] Babylon's lion-form anticipates Rev 13:2: "his mouth as the mouth of a lion." The end-time composite beast inherits Babylon's voice.

---

## Dan 7:5 — The bear with three ribs (Medo-Persia)

> "And behold another beast, a second, like to a bear, and it raised up itself on one side, and it had three ribs in the mouth of it between the teeth of it: and they said thus unto it, Arise, devour much flesh" (Dan 7:5)

- The dual nature of the empire — Media + Persia — is shown by the bear _raised on one side_: Persia outgrew Media. Dan 8:3-4 will confirm this with a ram whose two horns are unequal, the higher coming up last.
- _Three ribs_ = Medo-Persia's three principal conquests: Lydia (547 BC under Cyrus), Babylon (539 BC), Egypt (525 BC under Cambyses). "Devour much flesh" describes Persia's appetite.
- [→] The bear-feet of Rev 13:2 reach back here.

---

## Dan 7:6 — The leopard with four heads (Greece)

> "After this I beheld, and lo another, like a leopard, which had upon the back of it four wings of a fowl; the beast had also four heads; and dominion was given to it" (Dan 7:6)

- A leopard is fast; four wings double the speed. Alexander the Great's empire (334-323 BC) ran across the known world in roughly a decade — the fastest conquest in ancient history.
- _Four heads_ = the four-way split of his empire eventually divided among four successor kingdoms after his death: Cassander (Macedonia and Greece), Lysimachus (Thrace and most of Asia Minor), Seleucus (Syria and east), Ptolemy (Egypt). Dan 8:8 and Dan 8:21-22 will name this same four-way division as four horns replacing the great horn of the goat.
- [DYK🔎] Two of these four Hellenistic kingdoms reappear in Dan 11 as "the king of the north" (Seleucid) and "the king of the south" (Ptolemaic). The Bible keeps naming the same four kingdoms from different angles — beasts, horns, heads, kings — because recapitulation is how Daniel teaches.

---

## Dan 7:7 — The fourth beast (Rome)

> "After this I saw in the night visions, and behold a fourth beast, dreadful and terrible, and strong exceedingly; and it had great iron teeth: it devoured and brake in pieces, and stamped the residue with the feet of it: and it was diverse from all the beasts that were before it; and it had ten horns" (Dan 7:7)

- _Iron teeth_ tie back to Dan 2's iron legs. "Devoured and brake in pieces, and stamped the residue" — Rome's signature method.
- "Diverse from all the beasts that were before it." Rome was not a mere successor empire; it was a different _kind_ — legal, bureaucratic, continent-spanning, with citizenship, roads, and a senate. The first three beasts could each be likened to a known animal. Rome has no animal — there is nothing in the wild like it.
- The ten horns are introduced here; Dan 7:24 will identify them.

---

## Dan 7:8 — The little horn appears

> "I considered the horns, and, behold, there came up among them another little horn, before whom there were three of the first horns plucked up by the roots: and, behold, in this horn were eyes like the eyes of man, and a mouth speaking great things" (Dan 7:8)

- An eleventh horn rises _among_ the ten — so it arises from within the broken-up Roman territory.
- _Three of the first horns plucked up by the roots_ — three kingdoms removed to make room for it.
- _Eyes like the eyes of man_ + _a mouth speaking great things_ — this horn is _diverse_ (v. 24): not merely political. It has the features of a person — sight and speech, signs of a religious-prophetic power, not just a military one.

> [CTRF] **Pair: the little horn's eyes-and-mouth = counterfeit prophet on a political beast.** The other ten horns are political; this eleventh is _diverse_: it has _eyes_ ("a Prophet was beforetime called a Seer," 1 Sam 9:9) and a _mouth_ ("speaking great things," v. 8). A horn with eyes and a mouth is a religious power growing out of a political base. God's pattern pairs a sovereign and a prophet who speaks His word (Ex 4:16; Ex 7:1 — "I have made thee a god to Pharaoh: and Aaron thy brother shall be thy prophet"). The little horn parodies the pattern: a state-system fused to its own prophet-mouth, but the words proceed from beneath, not from above.

- [→] Iron-and-clay (Dan 2:43) said this in metal: pure-iron Rome eventually mixed with clay. Clay in Scripture is what the potter shapes (Isa 64:8) — _people_, often God's people. State + religious-people fused into one institution is precisely the shape that produces a horn with eyes and a mouth. Dan 2 told you the alloy; Dan 7 names the resulting beast.

## Dan 7:9-10 — The courtroom opens

> "I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit, whose garment was white as snow, and the hair of his head like the pure wool: his throne was like the fiery flame, and his wheels as burning fire. A fiery stream issued and came forth from before him: thousand thousands ministered unto him, and ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him: the judgment was set, and the books were opened" (Dan 7:9-10)

- The KJV's "cast down" is rendered "set in place" / "set up" in modern translations — the underlying Aramaic verb describes thrones being _placed_, not overturned. Plural _thrones_, then a singular figure sits: the Ancient of Days takes the throne; other thrones are arrayed for those who attend the court (cf. Rev 4:4's twenty-four elders on thrones).
- The Ancient of Days is God the Father. The throne/fire/glory imagery is paralleled in Eze 1:26-28; the white-hair language is echoed in Rev 1:14 and applied to the glorified Christ.
- _Books opened_ = records. The judgment proceeds by evidence, not by decree.
- A capital court: judge present, heavenly witnesses ("thousand thousands ministered; ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him"), records on the bench, the accused in view (the little horn's mouth is still speaking, v. 11).

> [⟲] **Judgment chain (canonical sequence).** Dan 7:9-14 (court convened) → Rev 4-5 (Lamb qualified to take the scroll) → Rev 11:15-19 (temple opened, ark seen) → Rev 14:6-7 ("the hour of his judgment is come") → Rev 20:11-12 (great white throne, books opened). The single most cross-referenced chain in Daniel and Revelation begins here.

## Dan 7:11-12 — The horn's voice triggers the verdict

> "I beheld then because of the voice of the great words which the horn spake: I beheld even till the beast was slain, and his body destroyed, and given to the burning flame. As concerning the rest of the beasts, they had their dominion taken away: yet their lives were prolonged for a season and time" (Dan 7:11-12)

- The court convenes _because_ the horn is speaking. The blasphemy that drew heaven's attention is what the court adjudicates.
- The fourth beast is _destroyed_. The first three beasts had their _dominion_ removed but their _lives prolonged_ (Babylon's culture lived on under Persia; Persia under Greece; Greece under Rome). The fourth beast does not enjoy that continuity — its destruction is total.
- [→] The verdict in heaven leads to the execution on earth. Dan 7:11 corresponds to Rev 19's destruction of the beast — but only _after_ Dan 7:9-10's court session and Dan 7:13-14's coronation are complete.

## Dan 7:13-14 — The Son of Man comes _to_ the Ancient of Days

> "I saw in the night visions, and, behold, one like the Son of man came with the clouds of heaven, and came to the Ancient of days, and they brought him near before him. And there was given him dominion, and glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages, should serve him: his dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom that which shall not be destroyed" (Dan 7:13-14)

> [KEY🔑] **Dan 7:13 is NOT the Second Coming.** "Came _to_ the Ancient of days" — the Son of Man comes _to the Father_. He does not come _to earth_. He comes _into_ the judgment, not _out_ of it. "They brought him near before him" — courtroom language, not descent-language. Contrast Rev 1:7: "Behold, he cometh with clouds; and _every eye shall see him_, and they also which pierced him." At the Second Coming, Christ comes _to earth_, _every eye_ sees Him, _earth's inhabitants_ respond (cf. Matt 24:30, "all the tribes of the earth mourn"). In Dan 7:13, Christ comes _to heaven's courtroom_, the _Ancient of Days_ receives Him, _angels_ attend. Different destination. Different audience. Different event. Dan 7:13 is Christ's _accession to the judgment_ — the moment the High Priest enters the Most Holy Place of the heavenly sanctuary to begin the final phase of His priestly work. Rev 22:12 confirms: "my reward is with me" — the verdicts are already rendered when Christ returns to earth. Nobody pleads at the Second Coming. The pleading belongs to the session in Dan 7.

- [→] If Dan 7:13 is conflated with the Second Coming, the investigative judgment collapses into nothing. The sanctuary service, the books, the "hour of his judgment" in Rev 14:7 — all of it requires this distinction to stand.

> [Q] Where else in Scripture does a Son-of-Man cloud-coming go _up_ rather than down? Acts 1:9-11 confirms Christ _ascended_ in clouds and will return the same way. The upward trip is to the Father (Heb 9:24 — "Christ is... entered into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us"). Matt 26:64 has Christ quoting Dan 7:13 to the high priest just before the cross — applying the verse to His own coming-up to the Father, not to His coming-down to earth. Every cloud-coming is not the same cloud-coming. Look where He is going.

## Dan 7:15-16 — Daniel asks

> "I Daniel was grieved in my spirit in the midst of my body, and the visions of my head troubled me. I came near unto one of them that stood by, and asked him the truth of all this. So he told me, and made me know the interpretation of the things" (Dan 7:15-16)

- Daniel's response is not curiosity — it is grief. The cost of what he has seen is real.
- He _asks_. Daniel does not invent meanings. He receives them.
- [→] When Scripture supplies a vision, Scripture supplies the interpretation. The angel's explanation in vv. 17-27 is part of the canon, not a footnote.

## Dan 7:17-18 — The four kingdoms named, the saints possess

> "These great beasts, which are four, are four kings, which shall arise out of the earth. But the saints of the most High shall take the kingdom, and possess the kingdom for ever, even for ever and ever" (Dan 7:17-18)

- "These great beasts, which are four, _are four kings_." The Dan 2:38-39 / Dan 7:17 / Dan 7:23 principle (king = kingdom) is reasserted. The whole symbol-system rests here.
- Verse 18 telegraphs the ending before the rest of the explanation: _the saints get the kingdom_. Whatever the rest of the chapter describes in terms of beasts and horns and 1260 years, the result is fixed.
- [→] Read the rest of the chapter knowing the destination. The trial is real, the persecution is real, but the verdict is settled.

## Dan 7:19-20 — Daniel asks again about the fourth beast

> "Then I would know the truth of the fourth beast... and of the ten horns that were in his head, and of the other which came up, and before whom three fell; even of that horn that had eyes, and a mouth that spake very great things, whose look was more stout than his fellows" (Dan 7:19-20)

- Daniel's interest concentrates on the fourth beast and the eleventh horn. So does the rest of the chapter — and the rest of Daniel and Revelation.
- "Whose look was more stout than his fellows" — the little horn appears _bigger_ than the ten horns it grew up among, even though it started "little." The optical impression matters: this power _appears_ to dominate the political horns, while in fact growing up among them from the same fourth-beast territory.

## Dan 7:21-22 — The horn wars; the court rules; the saints possess

> "I beheld, and the same horn made war with the saints, and prevailed against them; until the Ancient of days came, and judgment was given to the saints of the most High; and the time came that the saints possessed the kingdom" (Dan 7:21-22)

- Three consecutive milestones: (1) the Ancient of Days takes His seat (the v. 9-10 session); (2) judgment is given _for_ the saints (the verdict in their favor); (3) the saints _possess_ the kingdom.
- "Made war with the saints, and prevailed against them" — for the duration of the 1260 years, the horn wins on earth. The verdict in heaven reverses earth's record.
- [→] Two histories run on the same calendar. On earth: the horn prevails. In heaven: the verdict goes the other way. The whole burden of Dan 7 is to keep the saints' eyes on the second one.

---

## Dan 7:23 — The fourth kingdom

> "Thus he said, The fourth beast shall be the fourth kingdom upon earth, which shall be diverse from all kingdoms, and shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it in pieces" (Dan 7:23)

- King = kingdom restated for the third time in the chapter (vv. 17, 23 explicit; v. 24 again).
- Rome is described territorially ("devour the whole earth") and methodically ("tread it down... break it in pieces"). The empire that gave the world _pax Romana_ achieved it by stamping the residue.

---

## Dan 7:24 — The ten horns and the eleventh

> "And the ten horns out of this kingdom are ten kings that shall arise: and another shall rise after them; and he shall be diverse from the first, and he shall subdue three kings" (Dan 7:24)

- _Ten horns out of this kingdom_ = the successor kingdoms born from Rome's collapse. Historicist teachers have traditionally listed the ten as: Alamanni, Franks, Burgundians, Suevi, Vandals, Visigoths, Ostrogoths, Heruli, Lombards, Anglo-Saxons. The exact list varies by source, but the pattern is what matters: Rome fragmented into multiple successor kingdoms that formed the post-Roman map from which modern Europe developed.
- _Another shall rise after them; and he shall be diverse from the first_ — the eleventh horn is _later than_ the ten and _different in kind_.
- _Subdue three_ — three of the original ten are removed to make room for it. Reformation and post-Reformation historicists identified the three as the Heruli (493 AD), the Vandals (534 AD), and the Ostrogoths (538 AD) — three Arian kingdoms removed from the path of the rising papal power in the late fifth and sixth centuries.

## Dan 7:25 — The seven marks of the little horn (and the 1260 years)

> "And he shall speak great words against the most High, and shall wear out the saints of the most High, and think to change times and laws: and they shall be given into his hand until a time and times and the dividing of time" (Dan 7:25)

The seven identifying marks (combined from vv. 8, 20, 24, 25):

1. Rises _among_ the ten, _after_ the ten (vv. 8, 24) — within post-Rome Europe, not before
2. Plucks up three of the first horns by the roots (vv. 8, 24) — three kingdoms removed
3. Has _eyes_ and _a mouth speaking great things_ (vv. 8, 20) — a seeing, speaking, religious power
4. Is _diverse from_ the other horns (v. 24) — a different _kind_ of power than the surrounding nations
5. Speaks _great words against the most High_ (v. 25) — blasphemous claims
6. Wears out the saints of the most High (v. 25) — long-term persecution, not a single flare-up
7. Thinks to _change times and laws_ (v. 25) — attempts to alter the very law of God

> [Q] Seven marks feels excessive — why so many? Because the little horn is the most significant anti-God power in Daniel, and Scripture is careful not to let it be misidentified. With _all seven marks converging on the same power_, the fit becomes unmistakable. Many Reformation and post-Reformation historicists — Wycliffe, Tyndale, Luther, Knox, Isaac Newton — identified the same power: the papal system that arose from within fallen Rome, saw three Arian kingdoms removed from its path in the late fifth and sixth centuries, spoke as the "Vicar of Christ," persecuted dissenters for centuries, and claimed authority over the Sabbath commandment. The marks fit one system. They do not fit a future individual.

**"Think to change times and laws."**

- Two targets: sacred _time_ and divine _law_. In the Decalogue — the only body of law God Himself wrote (Ex 31:18) — the Sabbath is exactly where sacred time and divine law converge.
- God's law has one time-element: the Sabbath (Ex 20:8-11). The fourth commandment is the one commandment that is _about_ time — the seventh day, the weekly rhythm. Any claim to alter God's weekly worship cycle qualifies as "thinking to change times."
- _Think to change_ does not mean _succeeds_. The phrase suggests an attempt, a claim of authority. God's law is not actually altered; the power only claims the authority to alter it.
- [→] The little horn's signature move is not merely political conquest or military persecution. It is _legislative_ — it reaches for God's commandment and claims the right to rewrite it.

**The 1260 years.**

- _A time_ + _times_ (dual in Aramaic, so two) + _half a time_ = three and a half "times."
- In the time-prophecies of apocalyptic, a "time" = a year (this will be confirmed in Dan 8-9 by the 70-weeks math). Three and a half years = 1260 days, and by the year-day principle (Num 14:34; Eze 4:6) = 1260 years.
- The historicist application: the papal political dominance over post-Rome Europe runs from 538 AD (the removal of the Ostrogoths and the operative beginning of papal power under Justinian's decree) to 1798 AD (the French general Berthier's seizure of the pope, ending the papal political reach).

> [⟲] **1260-year chain (link 1).** Dan 7:25 → Dan 12:7 (under oath) → Rev 11:2-3 → Rev 12:6, 14 → Rev 13:5. Five canonical witnesses to the same prophetic period.

## Dan 7:26 — Judgment removes the horn's dominion

> "But the judgment shall sit, and they shall take away his dominion, to consume and to destroy it unto the end" (Dan 7:26)

- The judgment that opened in v. 9-10 is the same judgment that here _takes away_ the horn's dominion. The court session and the verdict are one process; v. 26 names the outcome.
- _To consume and to destroy unto the end_ — the dominion is not paused; it is terminated. Rev 19's lake of fire is the final scene of this verdict.

## Dan 7:27 — The kingdom given to the saints

> "And the kingdom and dominion, and the greatness of the kingdom under the whole heaven, shall be given to the people of the saints of the most High, whose kingdom is an everlasting kingdom, and all dominions shall serve and obey him" (Dan 7:27)

- Three times in this chapter the kingdom is given to the saints: v. 18, v. 22, v. 27. Three witnesses, in case the reader misses it.
- _The people of the saints of the most High_ — corporate, not just individual. A people inherits the kingdom.
- The stone from Dan 2 becomes a mountain that fills the earth. Dan 7:27 names who inhabits that mountain: _the saints_.
- [→] The ones who refused Babylon's table in Dan 1, stood through Babylon's furnace in Dan 3, and survived the little horn's 1260 years — they get the kingdom. Not the political empires. Not the religious horn. The faithful.

## Dan 7:28 — Daniel's silence

> "Hitherto is the end of the matter. As for me Daniel, my cogitations much troubled me, and my countenance changed in me: but I kept the matter in my heart" (Dan 7:28)

- Daniel kept it. He did not preach this on a Babylonian street corner. The vision was given for the church across the centuries, not for Babylon's news cycle.
- "My countenance changed in me" — what Daniel saw _showed on his face_. The seer is reshaped by the vision.

## The recapitulation principle — Dan 7 demonstrates it

> [KEY🔑] **The recapitulation principle.** Daniel does not tell his story once. Dan 2 covered four kingdoms (the political outside). Dan 7 covers _the same four kingdoms_ plus the courtroom (the religious-political inside, with judgment). Dan 8-9 will cover _the same sequence_ plus the sanctuary and the 2300-day timeline. Dan 11 will cover it again in maximum detail, walking from Persia to the end. Four passes. Same history. Different emphasis each time.

- This is not an inference — it is the structure of the book itself. Same empires, same endpoint, retold with more resolution.
- Revelation will do the same on its own scale: churches (Rev 1-3), seals (Rev 6-7), trumpets (Rev 8-11), great-controversy span (Rev 12-14), final-phase endgame (Rev 15-22). Multiple passes covering the ground from John's era to the end.
- [→] When you see a symbol that feels familiar, it probably is. You are not reading a new storyline; you are reading the same story from a different angle. Each pass adds what the last one left out.

## Key Takeaways

- Dan 7 is Dan 2 retold from a different angle, with the courtroom added — the recapitulation principle on the page
- The Aramaic chiasm (Dan 2-7) brackets Babylon's collapse with prophecy and persecution; structure is argument
- Four beasts = four kingdoms (Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome)
- The fourth beast's ten horns = the nations born from Rome's collapse; three are uprooted (Heruli, Vandals, Ostrogoths) for the eleventh horn
- The little horn has seven identifying marks; no single future individual fits all seven; the papal system historically does
- Counterfeit pair: the little horn's eyes-and-mouth = a counterfeit prophet on a political beast (a state-system fused to its own prophet-mouth)
- "Think to change times and laws" (Dan 7:25) targets God's Sabbath and His commandments specifically
- The 1260-year period: 538 AD to 1798 AD (papal political dominance over post-Rome Europe)
- **Dan 7:13 is NOT the Second Coming.** The Son of Man comes _to the Ancient of Days_ — up, into the judgment — not down, to earth. This is the structural key to the investigative judgment
- The judgment begins _before_ the kingdom is given; the verdict precedes the execution
- The kingdom ultimately belongs to the saints, not to the beast or the horn (Dan 7:18, 22, 27)

## Next: Daniel 8 — The Ram, the Goat, and the 2300 Days

Two years after Dan 7, Daniel sees a third vision. A ram with two horns (Medo-Persia, Dan 8:20). A goat with one notable horn (Greece, Dan 8:21). The goat's horn breaks; four horns rise; out of one of the four "came forth a little horn." The vision pivots toward "the sanctuary" and "the 2300 days." The chapter ends with Daniel fainting at the time-prophecy he could not yet understand. Dan 8 is the chapter that brings _the sanctuary_ into Daniel's prophetic frame, and Dan 9 will then unlock the 2300-day timeline by handing the reader the 70-week starting block. Dan 8-9 is the longest mathematical prophecy in Scripture.
