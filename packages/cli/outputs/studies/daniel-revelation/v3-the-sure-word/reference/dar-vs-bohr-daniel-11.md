## The crucial divergence: where each reading splits

DAR and Bohr agree on the chapter from v. 1 through v. 35 with one minor exception (v. 2 = Xerxes vs. Artaxerxes). **The splits are at v. 22-23, at v. 36, and at v. 40.**

### Split #1 — v. 22-23: where does pagan Rome end?

- **DAR**: Pagan Rome runs through v. 22 (the Cross under Tiberius). Verses 23-30 are still pagan Rome consolidating (Actium B.C. 31, Jerusalem A.D. 70, Constantinople A.D. 330, Vandals A.D. 455). Papal Rome begins at v. 30-31.
- **Bohr**: Roman Empire runs through v. 28; papal Rome begins at v. 29. He compresses the pagan-Rome detail and arrives at the papacy earlier.

**Significance**: Minor. Both reach papal Rome at v. 31 = 538. The internal chronology is identical even if the section labels differ.

### Split #2 — v. 36: who is the "king" who exalts himself above every god?

This is where the chapter's interpretation forks.

- **DAR** (paras 1376-1431): "_The king here introduced cannot denote the same power which was last noticed, namely, the papal power; for the specifications will not hold good if applied to that power. Take a declaration in the next verse: 'Nor regard any god.' This has never been true of the papacy._" Smith follows Mede, Wintle, Boothroyd in translating "_A certain king shall do according to his will_" — introducing a new actor. He identifies the new actor as **Revolutionary France 1793-1799** and walks every clause: atheism (1792 Convention), abolition of marriage (1792 civil-contract law), Goddess of Reason (Notre Dame 1793), Supreme Being cult (Robespierre 1794), land confiscation (£700M sterling per Alison Vol. IV).

- **Bohr** (pp. 74-90): Same king as v. 35 — the **papacy**, continuing description. Reads "exalts himself" as papal arrogance, "magnifies above every god" as papal claims over saints and Mary, and the "regards no god" language as papal idolatry rather than literal atheism. Does not name France.

**Significance**: Major. If Bohr is right, the 1260 closes at 1798 but the chapter's _next_ section already collapses into endless papacy and the historic vindication of Revolutionary France as a Daniel 11 actor disappears. If DAR is right, the chapter contains a precise, dateable, named fulfillment in 1793-1799 — atheism, abolition of marriage, Goddess of Reason, land confiscation — that fingerprints no other regime in history.

### Split #3 — v. 40: literal "time of the end" or symbolic end-time invasion?

This is the chapter's interpretive Rubicon.

- **DAR** (paras 1432-1475): "Time of the end" begins at 1798. The triangular war happens _in_ 1798 — France invades Egypt (May-July 1798), Turkey declares war on France (Sept 2, 1798), Napoleon's Syrian campaign and retreat from St. Jean d'Acre (1799). "_King of the north_" = the literal power on Seleucid territory = the Ottoman sultan. Every subsequent geographic name is literal.

- **Bohr** (pp. 17-21, 223+): "Time of the end" begins at 1798 _but_ v. 40 describes the **future repeat** of papal aggression. Following Louis Were explicitly (Bohr cites _The King of the North at Jerusalem_, p. 75), Bohr applies the "automatic transition from literal to spiritual" principle: before A.D. 34 (Stephen's stoning), prophecy uses literal geography; after, all geography is spiritualized. So vv. 40-45 must be spiritual:
  - "King of the north" = the papacy in its end-time global aggression
  - "King of the south" = symbolic resistance (often identified as atheism/secularism — Egypt symbolizing the world of Ex 5:2 "_I know not the LORD_")
  - "Euphrates" = symbolic Babylon (Rev 16:12, 17:1, 15)
  - "Glorious land" / "Jerusalem" = the worldwide church
  - "Edom/Moab/Ammon" = symbolic groups (Bohr argues they cannot be literal because the nations no longer exist)
  - "Holy mountain between the seas" = symbolic Mount Zion = the church's spiritual location

**Significance**: This is the entire polemic of TSW (and of v3 PRINCIPLES.md P1, P3, P4, P7). Spiritualizing v. 40 erases:

1. The 1798 triangular-war vindication (literal Egypt + literal Turkey + literal France in one year)
2. The chapter-stable meaning of "king of the north" (literal Seleucid territory from v. 5 onward)
3. Adam Clarke's 1825 written-in-advance forecast of Persia + Russia troubling Turkey, fulfilled in the Crimean War
4. The specific Ottoman tribute record on Edom/Moab/Ammon
5. The geographic topographic fingerprint of Jerusalem ("between the seas")

## Where they actually agree

Worth naming, because the disagreement is sharper for what is _shared_:

- Daniel 11 is a continuous prophecy from Persia to the second advent
- The historicist method (year-day, recapitulation, literal-first defaults in the early chapter)
- The 1260 years run **538 to 1798**, ending with Berthier's capture of Pius VI
- The papacy is the dominant actor through the medieval section
- The chapter ends with a future event that triggers the standing-up of Michael (Dan 12:1)
- Ellen White's interpretive authority is binding (though they cite her _differently_ — see "EGW handling" below)
- William Miller's framework was Adventism's starting point (Bohr emphasizes Miller's original "king of the north = papacy" reading as a "landmark"; DAR honors Miller's method but updates his identifications)
- The 2300-day prophecy reaches 1844 (both agree this is the chapter's secondary backbone)

### Daniel 11:36 — THE FIRST MAJOR SPLIT

> "_The king shall do according to his will; and he shall exalt himself, and magnify himself above every god, and shall speak marvelous things against the God of gods, and shall prosper till the indignation be accomplished._"

| DAR (paras 1376-1380)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             | Bohr (pp. 74-90)                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **A NEW actor introduced.** Smith follows Mede, Wintle, Boothroyd: "_A certain king shall do according to his will_" — the article is not definite-restrictive but introductory. "_The king here introduced cannot denote the same power which was last noticed, namely, the papal power; for the specifications will not hold good if applied to that power._" The new actor: **Revolutionary France 1793-1799** | **Same power as v. 35** — the papacy continues. The "king" continues description from the 1260-year actor. "Exalts himself" = papal claims; "magnify above every god" = papal supremacy over saints/Mary |

**Divergence**: Total. This is the chapter's interpretive Rubicon.

**DAR's reasons for a new actor:**

1. **Linguistic** — Hebraists (Mede, Wintle, Boothroyd) render the article as introductory
2. **Specification** — v. 37's "_neither shall he regard the God of his fathers, nor the desire of women, nor regard any god_" cannot fit the papacy, which has always _professed_ the God of its fathers and _always_ regarded its god
3. **Temporal** — v. 35 ends "_to the time of the end_" (1798); the papacy's 1260 closes here, so vv. 36-39 describe events _in_ the time of the end, not before
4. **Historical fit** — Revolutionary France (1792-1799) fits _every_ clause exactly; the papacy does not

**Bohr's reasons for continuity:**

1. The grammar's definite article suggests continuity ("_the_ king")
2. The whole section (vv. 31-39) is a single block about the king of the north / little horn / papacy
3. EGW (Letter 103, 1904) quoted vv. 30-36 together as one description of "scenes similar to" what would recur in the end-time
4. The "magnify above every god" language parallels Dan 7 and 2 Thess 2 — already established papal markers

The **"desire of women"** divergence is especially sharp:

### Daniel 11:40 — THE SECOND MAJOR SPLIT

> "_And at the time of the end shall the king of the south push at him: and the king of the north shall come against him like a whirlwind, with chariots, and with horsemen, and with many ships; and he shall enter into the countries, and shall overflow and pass over._"

| DAR (paras 1432-1454)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | Bohr (pp. 17-21, 223+)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **1798 triangular war.** "Time of the end" = 1798. France (v. 36) is the "him." Egypt pushes (Napoleon's invasion May-July 1798); Turkey whirlwinds (Sept 2, 1798 declaration of war, joined by Britain + Russia); Napoleon's Syrian campaign repulsed at St. Jean d'Acre 1799. **King of the north = literal Ottoman sultan** on Seleucid territory. The verb subject of "_overflow and pass over_" shifts to the king of the north (DAR 1452 cites Hebrew construction) — vv. 41-45 track Ottoman history | **Future / symbolic.** "Time of the end" = 1798 (deadly wound). King of the north = **papacy** (recovered from wound). End-time invasion: papal aggression moves from Euphrates (symbolic Babylon) → west → south → conquers spiritual countries. King of the south = some symbolic resistance (Bohr does not pin to one identity; sometimes atheism, sometimes secular powers); the river/whirlwind imagery from Isa 8:7-8 and Rev 16:12 controls his reading |

**Divergence**: Total. The 1798 anchor is the same; the _referent_ is opposite.

**DAR's key reasoning:**

1. The chapter has used "king of the north" geographically since v. 5 (literal Seleucid territory)
2. v. 40 says the action happens "_at_ the time of the end" — 1798 itself — and a _triangular_ war actually occurred in 1798 (France + Egypt + Turkey)
3. Every specification (chariots, horsemen, many ships, the geographic sequence) maps to the documented events
4. [RULE V] Scripture is its own expositor — the chapter's own usage at vv. 5-13 fixes "king of the north" as the literal northern power, and by 1798 that was Turkey

**Bohr's key reasoning** (citing Louis Were, _The King of the North at Jerusalem_, p. 75):

1. "_When passing over into the Christian era there is an automatic transition from literal to spiritual Babylon; from literal to spiritual Jerusalem; from the literal lands of Israel and Babylon to their spiritual antitypes._"
2. Stephen's stoning (A.D. 34) ended the Jewish theocracy; after that, all prophetic geography becomes spiritual
3. Edom/Moab/Ammon no longer exist as nations, so they must be symbolic; if symbolic, then everything else in the section must be symbolic
4. Therefore the whole of vv. 40-45 is the end-time papal aggression against the worldwide church

**DAR's counter to Bohr's "automatic transition"**:

- The "principle" appears nowhere in Scripture; it is asserted, not proven
- The chapter itself does not switch vocabulary at any point — same Persia, same Greece, same Egypt, same Syria from v. 2 to v. 45
- Edom/Moab/Ammon do exist _geographically_ (the deserts of Jordan), and the Ottoman tribute records on those tribes are historical
- If "king of the north" can mean papacy at v. 40, then it must also mean papacy at vv. 5-13 — but no one reads it that way there
- [RULE IV] No Scripture must be left to contradict another. Bohr's reading at v. 40 contradicts the chapter's own usage at vv. 5-13

## EGW handling — both sides claim her, differently

This deserves its own section because both readings appeal to Ellen White, and Bohr makes the EGW argument central to his case.

**Bohr's EGW evidence:**

1. **Word to the Little Flock (1847)** — James and Ellen White: "_This last power that treads down the saints is brought to view in Revelation 13:11-18. His number is 666._" Bohr treats this as identifying the king of the north as the papacy
2. **Letter 103 (1904)** — EGW quotes Dan 11:30-36 and says "_scenes similar to those described in these words will take place._" Bohr reads this as EGW applying vv. 30-36 (all the way through "_the king shall do according to his will_") to the papacy as one continuous power
3. **The Great Controversy (1888/1911)** — chapters on the time of trouble, God's people delivered, etc., describe a final crisis in language Bohr maps onto Dan 11:40-45 even though EGW never quotes those verses

**DAR-stream response (TSW position):**

1. The 1847 _Little Flock_ statement was made _before_ Smith's mature work (DAR published from 1875+) and reflects an early Millerite holdover. It identifies the _last persecuting power_ as the papacy — which DAR also affirms (the papacy is the actor of vv. 31-35 and the final wound-healed beast of Rev 13). It does not by itself decide vv. 36-45
2. The Letter 103 quote stops at v. 36 — EGW says scenes _similar to_ vv. 30-36 will recur, but does not equate the v. 36 king with the v. 35 actor. The "similar" language is _typological recurrence_, not a hermeneutical instruction to read the verses as one power
3. EGW's silence on vv. 40-45 is silence — Bohr's argument that her _thematic_ discussion of the final crisis maps onto Dan 11:40-45 is an inference layered on her words, not a direct quotation
4. Bohr's own admission (p. 8): "_It might surprise some to know that Ellen White never actually quoted any verse from Daniel 11:40-45._" This is decisive. The interpretive weight Bohr places on EGW for vv. 40-45 rests on inference from _parallel themes_ in GC and EW, not on her exposition of those verses

**The methodological issue**: Bohr makes EGW's _thematic emphasis on the final crisis_ into a _hermeneutic key_ for Daniel 11. DAR keeps EGW as a confirming witness on the topics she addresses (the papacy, the Reformation, the time of trouble) but treats Daniel 11 itself as Scripture-interprets-Scripture, with EGW silent where the text is most contested.

## What this comparison reveals

1. **The frameworks share an enormous amount.** Both are historicist, both date 538-1798, both treat Daniel 11 as a continuous prophecy from Persia to the end, both believe v. 45 is future, both believe Michael stands up at the end. The disagreement is sharper _because_ the agreement is so wide.

2. **The split is method, not data.** DAR walks the chapter on Miller's Rules (literal-first, Scripture-its-own-expositor, history vindicates). Bohr layers Were's "automatic transition" principle on top, which forces the chapter to switch hermeneutic register mid-prophecy. The data set is the same; the rules of interpretation differ.

3. **DAR's strongest move** is asking what _changed in the chapter_ to justify a hermeneutic switch. From v. 2 to v. 45 the chapter uses the same actors (king of north, king of south), the same geography (Egypt, Syria, glorious land), the same method (named persons, dated events). No internal signal marks a method-change.

4. **Bohr's strongest move** is the apparent EGW thematic alignment between her final-crisis chapters and an end-time papal aggressor. If the entire vision is funneling toward Rev 13/17's papal-Babylon climax, why should Daniel 11 — Daniel's longest single prophecy — end on the Ottoman empire rather than on the papacy?

5. **DAR's reply** is that the papacy _is_ in Daniel 11 — it is the actor of vv. 23-35 — and the chapter has already given it 13 verses (more than any other actor). What follows in vv. 36-45 is the chapter's continuation into other powers (France) and other regions (the Ottoman east), not because the papacy fades but because the prophecy is tracking the _whole_ end-time landscape, of which the papacy is one (central) part. The papacy's final role belongs to Rev 13 and Rev 17, not to Dan 11:45.

6. **For the v3 series**, the takeaway is that this comparison _is_ the polemic. Every Sure Word study will, silently, be making the DAR case against the Bohr/Were reading. The reader who is drifting toward the Bohr framework will recognize, in TSW's literal walk of the chapter, the path that does not require the "automatic transition" principle. The principle is the load-bearing assumption; remove it, and the spiritualizing collapses.
