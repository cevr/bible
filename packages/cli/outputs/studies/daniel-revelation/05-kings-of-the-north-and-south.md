# Study 5: Kings of the North and South — Daniel 10-11

**Daniel's final vision is the most detailed predictive prophecy in the Bible. Three weeks of fasting. A Man clothed in linen. A twenty-one-day angelic war behind political decisions. Then a prophecy that walks from Persia through Greece through Rome all the way to "the time of the end" — naming kings' policies, marriages, wars, tax collectors, invasions, and betrayals so precisely that skeptics have insisted it must have been written _after_ the events. That skepticism is actually the strongest testimony to how specific the prophecy is. We will walk Dan 11 from verse 2 to verse 45 — confident where the text and history converge, and honest about where the end-time section is debated.**

## Prep Reading

- Daniel 10 (entire chapter)
- Daniel 11 (entire chapter)
- Jude 1:9
- Revelation 12:7-9
- 1 Thessalonians 4:16

## The Three-Week Fast

Dan 10 opens in "the third year of Cyrus king of Persia" — about 536/535 BC, two years after Cyrus' decree that let the Jews return home (Ezra 1, typically dated 538/537 BC). Daniel is now in his mid-eighties. The first group of returnees has gone back to Jerusalem (Ezra 2). But the rebuilding has stalled under local opposition (Ezra 4:1-5). Daniel is not with them — he stayed in Babylon/Persia, perhaps too old to travel. And he is mourning.

- **The fast** (Dan 10:2-3)
  - "In those days I Daniel was mourning three full weeks"
  - "I ate no pleasant bread, neither came flesh nor wine in my mouth, neither did I anoint myself at all"
  - Twenty-one days of partial fasting — pleading with God over something unspecified, likely the stalled rebuilding and the fragile returned community

- **The appearance** (Dan 10:4-9)
  - On the 24th day of the first month, by the Tigris (Hiddekel) river
  - "A certain man clothed in linen, whose loins were girded with fine gold of Uphaz: his body also was like the beryl, and his face as the appearance of lightning, and his eyes as lamps of fire, and his arms and his feet like in colour to polished brass, and the voice of his words like the voice of a multitude"
  - Daniel's men do not see the figure but sense His presence and flee. Daniel falls to the ground as if dead.

- **Who is this Man?**
  - Clothed in linen = the Day of Atonement high-priestly garments (Lev 16:4) — we established this pattern in Study 4
  - Description matches Rev 1:13-16 almost feature for feature: feet of burnished brass, eyes of fire, voice of many waters, face shining like lightning/the sun. Compare them line by line and you are looking at the same Person
  - [→] Daniel 10 and Rev 1 show the same Christ — our High Priest — at two different moments. Dan 10 shows Him entering the priestly ministry; Rev 1 shows Him conducting it among the candlesticks. Study 7 will come back to this.

[Q] **"Why Dan 10:13's 'twenty-one days' delay? Isn't Christ omnipotent?"**
→ The text does not say Christ was prevented. It says the angelic messenger was withstood by "the prince of the kingdom of Persia" for twenty-one days, until "Michael, one of the chief princes, came to help me" (Dan 10:13). The "prince of Persia" is not a human king — no human delays an angel. It is an angelic power aligned against God, working through the Persian court to shape policy against the Jews (cf. Eph 6:12 — "we wrestle... against principalities... against spiritual wickedness in high places"). God's answer came the first day Daniel prayed (Dan 10:12); the _delivery_ took three weeks because the contest was over whose counsel would prevail in the Persian court. Prayer moves heaven; heaven's answer sometimes has to move earth. The twenty-one days of Dan 10 and Daniel's three weeks of fasting match by design.

[DYK🔎] Michael appears three times by name in Daniel (Dan 10:13; 10:21; 12:1) and twice in the NT (Jude 1:9; Rev 12:7) — five named occurrences, every one of them in combat with Satan or as the defender of God's people. Jude 1:9 calls Him "the archangel." 1 Thess 4:16 says the Lord Himself descends "with the voice of the archangel." Historicist interpreters, reading these texts together with the common "archangel" language connecting them, identify Michael (Heb. _Mi-ka-El_ = "who is like God?") as the pre-incarnate Christ acting as heaven's captain. This is an interpretive inference from the chain of texts, not a direct verse equating Michael with Christ. The name itself poses the Christ-question: _who_ is like God? Answer: only the One who is God.

`[⟲]` **Michael thread:** Dan 10:13 (this link) → Dan 10:21 (same chapter, second appearance) → Dan 12:1 (Study 6) → Jude 9 → Rev 12:7 (Study 12). Five named appearances trace the Warrior-Prince's combat for God's people through both Testaments.

## Persia → Greece → Alexander's Four

After restoring Daniel, the Man tells him "that which is noted in the scripture of truth" (Dan 10:21). Dan 11 then walks forward from Daniel's own moment.

- **Four more Persian kings** (Dan 11:2)
  - "There shall stand up yet three kings in Persia; and the fourth shall be far richer than they all: and by his strength through his riches he shall stir up all against the realm of Grecia"
  - After Cyrus (already reigning): three more kings + one "far richer" who stirs up against Greece. Historically: Cambyses (530-522 BC), Pseudo-Smerdis (522 BC), Darius I (522-486 BC), and Xerxes I (486-465 BC — the famously wealthy king who invaded Greece in 480 BC, whose defeat at Salamis planted the grudge that Alexander would later settle)
  - Dan 11:2 predicts the exact sequence and the fourth's character, 150+ years before Xerxes crossed the Hellespont

- **A mighty king with a broken kingdom** (Dan 11:3-4)
  - "A mighty king shall stand up, that shall rule with great dominion, and do according to his will"
  - "And when he shall stand up, his kingdom shall be broken, and shall be divided toward the four winds of heaven; and not to his posterity"
  - Alexander the Great (336-323 BC) — empire at its peak, then sudden death at 32, empire divided _not_ to his infant son or half-brother but to four of his generals (Cassander, Lysimachus, Seleucus, Ptolemy)
  - [→] Dan 11:4 names two specifics 200+ years early: the empire would divide four ways, and none of Alexander's family would inherit. Both happened.

## The King of the South vs. the King of the North

Dan 11:5-20 narrows the focus to two of Alexander's four successor kingdoms:

- **King of the south** = Ptolemaic Egypt (south of Israel)
- **King of the north** = Seleucid Syria (north of Israel)
- Israel sits in between — the land Dan 11:16 calls "the glorious land" — so every time north and south collide, Israel is the battlefield

The identities in Dan 11 shift as history advances. In the early verses, "north" and "south" are specific kingdoms. Later, as those kingdoms are absorbed, the titles attach to the powers that inherit the same _role_ (whoever is pressuring Israel from the north, whoever from the south). That is how the prophecy keeps its meaning as the political map changes.

### The verified section — Dan 11:5-20

The Ptolemies vs. Seleucids stretches from ~323 BC down to Rome's takeover of the region. Dan 11 matches the documented history step by step.

This is why critical scholars, unwilling to accept prophecy, concluded Daniel must have been written _after_ the events (the "Maccabean thesis" — late 2nd century BC). The precision is precisely the problem for them. For anyone who accepts God's claim to "declare the end from the beginning" (Isa 46:10), Dan 11:5-20 is exhibit A.

### The transition — Dan 11:21-35: Rome enters the frame

Around verse 21, the "king of the north" shifts. The Seleucid line is collapsing. Rome is the power on the rise. The text moves with the history.

- **v. 21-22 — "a vile person, to whom they shall not give the honour of the kingdom: but he shall come in peaceably, and obtain the kingdom by flatteries"**
  - The verse language echoes Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175-164 BC), who took the Seleucid throne deceptively after Seleucus IV's assassination — and many commentators hold the immediate referent is Antiochus IV with Rome as the longer-range application
  - Dan 11:22 ends: "and shall be broken; yea, also the prince of the covenant" — "the prince of the covenant" shares conceptual ground with Dan 9:25-26's "Messiah the Prince" (also tied to the covenant). Non-historicist readings often identify this "prince" as Onias III, the legitimate high priest deposed and murdered during Antiochus IV's rise. Historicists argue Onias is insufficient because Daniel's covenant/prince language reaches its fullest fulfillment in Christ, whose death ratified the new covenant — the "prince of the covenant" par excellence
  - Historicist reading (we will adopt): Antiochus IV is the near-type in vv. 21-22, but the verses telescope forward to Rome — whose rise broke the Hellenistic world and whose execution of Christ "broke" the Prince of the Covenant (AD 31, Study 3). After this pivot, the "king of the north" role transfers from Seleucid hands to Rome in vv. 23-30, and then to papal Rome in vv. 31ff.
  - [→] This is a telescoping transition rather than an abrupt break. Dan 11 traces a single continuous line of northern anti-covenant power whose identity shifts as history advances: Seleucid → pagan Rome → papal Rome → (for vv. 36-45, see below).

- **v. 23-28 — Rome's rise and treachery**
  - "Work deceitfully: for he shall come up, and shall become strong with a small people" (v. 23) — Rome began as a small city-state
  - Treaties, flattery, alliances — the Roman method
  - "His heart shall be against the holy covenant" (v. 28) — Rome's eventual pressure on the Jewish people, culminating in AD 70

- **v. 29-30 — "the ships of Chittim"**
  - "The ships of Chittim shall come against him: therefore he shall be grieved, and return, and have indignation against the holy covenant"
  - "Chittim" = Cyprus/the western Mediterranean. Traditionally identified with Rome's intervention that turned back Antiochus IV from Egypt in 168 BC — the "Day of Eleusis," when Roman envoy Popillius Laenas delivered the Senate's ultimatum and drew the famous "line in the sand" around Antiochus, forcing his withdrawal. Historicists read this as a broader indicator of Rome itself — the western power that redirects "the king of the north" against Israel
  - [→] v. 30's "have intelligence with them that forsake the holy covenant" — collaboration with apostate Jews, which unfolded through the Herodian and Sadducean compromises and ultimately through the handover of Christ to Rome

- **v. 31 — "pollute the sanctuary of strength, and shall take away the daily, and they shall place the abomination that maketh desolate"**
  - Three targets: (1) pollute the sanctuary, (2) take away the daily, (3) set up the abomination that desolates
  - Historicist reading: as the role of "the king of the north" transfers from pagan Rome to papal Rome, this verse marks the transition of religious power. Rome under its new Christian guise "took away the daily" — the perception of Christ's heavenly ministry — and substituted an earthly priesthood claiming to mediate. The "abomination of desolation" language ties to Matt 24:15 and to Dan 9:27's AD 70 destruction; it reaches forward as the same principle keeps operating
  - Christ Himself took Dan's "abomination of desolation" language and pointed it at the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70 (Matt 24:15 || Luke 21:20). Historicists read Dan 11:31 as the broader principle — desolating power, repeated in new forms across the centuries

- **v. 32-35 — the long persecution and the refining**
  - "Such as do wickedly against the covenant shall he corrupt by flatteries: but the people that do know their God shall be strong, and do exploits" (v. 32) — the two responses to religious pressure: compromise, or stand
  - "They shall fall by the sword, and by flame, by captivity, and by spoil, many days" (v. 33) — the long persecution of the faithful; parallel to Rev 12:13-17 and Rev 13:5-7
  - "Some of them of understanding shall fall, to try them, and to purge, and to make them white, even to the time of the end" (v. 35) — persecution has a refining purpose; "time of the end" signals the text is reaching toward the end of its long arc

## The Willful King (Dan 11:36-45)

This is the most debated section of Daniel. The "king" of v. 36 has several features that historicists have mapped in different ways.

- **The features** (Dan 11:36-39)
  - "The king shall do according to his will" — absolute self-determination
  - "He shall exalt himself, and magnify himself above every god, and shall speak marvellous things against the God of gods" (v. 36) — blasphemous self-elevation
  - "Shall prosper till the indignation be accomplished" — time-limited success
  - "Neither shall he regard the God of his fathers, nor the desire of women, nor regard any god" (v. 37) — rejects traditional religion
  - "But in his estate shall he honour the God of forces [fortresses, or power itself]: and a god whom his fathers knew not shall he honour" (v. 38)

- **The final campaign** (Dan 11:40-45)
  - "At the time of the end shall the king of the south push at him" — a final southern challenge
  - "The king of the north shall come against him like a whirlwind" — a massive northern response
  - "Shall enter also into the glorious land" (v. 41) — Israel (physical or spiritual) drawn in
  - "Tidings out of the east and out of the north shall trouble him" (v. 44)
  - "He shall plant the tabernacles of his palace between the seas in the glorious holy mountain; yet he shall come to his end, and none shall help him" (v. 45) — the final fall

### Three main historicist readings of the willful king

Honest historicism: the main identification has been discussed for centuries, with careful interpreters landing in different places. We lay out three views and note the trade-offs.

- **View A: Papal Rome's final phase**
  - Held by a long stream of Protestant historicists (Keith, Barnes, and many later Adventist writers)
  - The "king" = the same little horn of Dan 7, now in its final manifestation; Dan 11 continues to trace papal Rome from v. 31 through v. 45
  - Strengths: preserves the single-subject continuity (one "king of the north" tracked from v. 21 to the end); Dan 11's "speak marvellous things against the God of gods" echoes Dan 7:25's "speak great words against the most High"
  - Challenges: v. 37's "neither shall he regard... the desire of women" is read as celibacy (papal), but the full phrase — "neither shall he regard the God of his fathers, nor the desire of women, nor regard any god" — reads most naturally as a power that rejects religion altogether
  - v. 45's death scene ("plant the tabernacles of his palace between the seas in the glorious holy mountain; yet he shall come to his end, and none shall help him") is read as the end of the papal system in the final crisis

- **View B: A secular/atheistic power in final conflict**
  - Uriah Smith's reading, which identified v. 36's king as revolutionary/atheistic France and its successors; held by a stream of Adventist writers and some modern historicist interpreters who take v. 37's "regard no god" at face value
  - The king = an end-time secular/atheistic/materialist power that "magnifies himself above every god" by displacing all religion
  - Strengths: v. 37's literal reading (rejects all gods, not one); the God of fortresses (v. 38) reads as worship of military/technological power; the French Revolution correlation with Rev 11's "beast from the bottomless pit" gives historicist continuity
  - Challenges: breaks the single-subject continuity from earlier in the chapter; requires explaining why the earlier "king of the north" has given way to a new character without a textual flag

- **View C: A composite end-time coalition**
  - The king = a final alliance of forces — religious-political (papal Rome's revived influence) united with secular power — pressuring God's people in the final crisis
  - Strengths: accommodates the mixed features (irreligion _and_ reverence of a "strange god"); fits Rev 13's dragon + sea beast + earth beast alliance and Rev 16:13's "three unclean spirits"
  - Challenges: the most interpretively ambitious; needs to explain how Dan 11:36's singular "king" becomes a coalition

- **What the text requires vs. what it leaves open**
  - **Required**: a power that (a) arises late in the Dan 11 sequence, (b) blasphemes against God, (c) drives a final northern attack that draws Israel in, (d) is destroyed between the seas at "the glorious holy mountain," (e) has "none to help him"
  - **Open**: whether that power is the continuous king-of-north already identified (View A), a new secular actor (View B), or a composite (View C)
  - [→] Honest historicism holds what the text demands and admits what it leaves open. We do not need certainty on every detail of Dan 11:40-45 to be confident that the chapter ends with the final fall of the power that has persecuted God's people across history, at "the glorious holy mountain" — which opens straight into Dan 12's resurrection.

[DYK🔎] "Between the seas, in the glorious holy mountain" (Dan 11:45) — the dual-sea geography has been read as the Mediterranean + Dead Sea (Israel's physical geography), with "the glorious holy mountain" as Mount Zion. Dan 8's chiasm (Study 3) paired "he-goat stamped the ram" (Dan 8:7) with this ending — so the end of Dan 11 mirrors and answers the beginning of Dan 8. Whatever else is disputed about vv. 40-45, the geographical and structural signals point toward a final confrontation at/near Jerusalem/Zion — not a vague generic "end-time battle." The location matters because the next chapter (Dan 12:1) says "at that time shall Michael stand up" — Christ acts, and the resurrection begins. Dan 11:45's fall and Dan 12:1-2's rising are back-to-back.

## Why This Chapter Matters for the Reader

Dan 11 is not a collection of ancient trivia. It is a test — and a consolation.

- **The test:** can God predict history accurately? Dan 11:2-20 is the answer. Verses this specific, written 350+ years in advance (Daniel's 536/535 BC setting to Seleucus IV's death in 175 BC), strain accidental-prediction explanations. Either Daniel is divinely given, or critical scholars must insist on a late (2nd-century BC) authorship — which still does not explain the post-Maccabean material (vv. 22-45), because no one in 165 BC could see Rome's subsequent centuries, the cross, or the papal rise.
- **The consolation:** God is not surprised. Empires rise, wars roll over Israel, alliances shift, persecution stretches "many days" (v. 33) — and every move is tracked in advance. "My counsel shall stand, and I will do all my pleasure" (Isa 46:10). The faithful are "purged" and "made white" (v. 35), not abandoned. The willful king of v. 45 "shall come to his end, and none shall help him" — nothing pursuing the saints has the last word.

- [→] If you are tempted to panic about current events, Dan 11 is the prescription. The details change. The pattern does not. The end is written.

## Key Takeaways

- Dan 10 opens with Daniel fasting 21 days — and a 21-day angelic contest behind Persian policy; prayer and heavenly politics are linked
- The Man clothed in linen (Dan 10:5-6) matches Christ in Rev 1:13-16 almost feature for feature — same Person, different moments in His priestly work
- Michael = the pre-incarnate Christ (historicist reading) — Dan 10:13; 12:1; Jude 1:9; Rev 12:7; 1 Thess 4:16 form a single chain
- Dan 11:2-4 predicts four Persian kings, then Alexander, then his empire divided four ways — all verified by Hellenistic history
- Dan 11:5-20 tracks Ptolemaic/Seleucid wars verse by verse, with the precision that has forced skeptics to a late-dating defense
- Around Dan 11:21-22 the "king of the north" role shifts from Seleucid to Roman power — "the prince of the covenant" broken = Christ crucified (cf. Dan 9:26)
- Dan 11:31's "take away the daily" parallels Dan 8:11 — the papal phase of Rome obscures Christ's continual heavenly ministry (Study 4)
- Dan 11:32-35 covers the long persecution that parallels Rev 12 and Rev 13 — a refining, not an abandonment
- The willful king (Dan 11:36-45) has three main historicist readings: papal Rome final phase, a secular/atheistic power, or a composite; required features are clear, the specific identification is honestly debated
- Dan 11:45 ends at "the glorious holy mountain" — a final confrontation at/near Zion; Dan 12:1 opens with Michael standing up

## Next: Study 6 — Sealed Till the Time of the End (Daniel 12)

Dan 12 is a four-verse coda that holds some of the Bible's most important words. "Michael shall stand up." A time of trouble such as never was. A partial resurrection. Two numbers given to no one else in Scripture — 1290 and 1335 days. A sealed book that will be opened at "the time of the end." Daniel's last question ("how long... until these things?") is answered three times, each time more definitely. Then the angel tells him: "Go thy way, Daniel: for the words are closed up and sealed." The book will reopen in Rev 10. We are about to cross that bridge.
