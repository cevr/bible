FRIDAY'S PRESENTATION

You showed a lot of material. You have done a lot of research, and it is all interesting. But the presentation felt like a firehose of information. There was no clear thread connecting the slides, and it was hard to keep up with the pace. I found myself trying to take in every detail, but I couldn't see how they fit together. There was no single thread connecting the slides. Each one was a standalone observation, and they moved quickly from one to the next.

When you said at the end, "pick something you find interesting and we'll talk more about it," the audience was being asked to find their own thread. That is a hard ask for people who do not yet have the framework you have.

I also want to mention something about the rhetorical questions. You use them frequently: "Did you know that Daniel says this?" or "Did you know that three is a symbol of the Sabbath?" And then there is a pause, but no answer follows. The question hangs there, and the audience is left to figure out the implication on their own. I think the intent is to spark curiosity. But what it can feel like from the other side is: "I know something you don't, and I'm waiting for you to catch up." I do not think that is what you mean at all, but it is how it can land. The fix is simple: state the point directly. Instead of "Did you know that the ram in Daniel 8 is also a sacrifice animal?", say: "The ram in Daniel 8 is a sacrifice animal. Here is why that matters." Make the statement, then build on it. Let the audience receive the insight instead of having to guess at it.

WHAT I THINK IS MISSING

You have asked why people do not engage with the studies. I think the answer is straightforward: people engage when a study answers a question they already have. Your studies answer questions that only someone with your framework would think to ask.

"What is the chiastic structure of Daniel 8?" is a question researchers ask. "What is Christ doing right now, and what does it have to do with me?" is a question everyone asks. Both questions lead to the same material. The difference is the starting point.

Your research is the destination. What is missing is the path from where people are to where the research takes them.

A DRAFT SERIES

Instead of just describing what I think could work, I went ahead and drafted something. It is a 20-study series covering all of Daniel and Revelation — start to finish, one continuous narrative. I am attaching the full draft separately so you can read it at your own pace.

Here is the shape of it:

Part I: The Foundation (Studies 1-4)
What is Daniel about, and how do we read it?

1. A Tale of Two Cities — Daniel 1. All prophecy is Jerusalem vs. Babylon.
2. The Zoom Lens — How Daniel is built. The Aramaic chiasm. The Hebrew section. Repeat and enlarge.
3. The Four Kingdoms — Daniel 2 and 7 together. Where are we in history?
4. The Three-Step Pattern — Prophecy, persecution, deliverance. The cycle encoded in Daniel 2-7 and repeated in every generation.

Part II: The Sanctuary (Studies 5-9)
What is Christ doing right now, and what did it cost Him?

5. The Sanctuary — The blueprint for salvation. Every piece of furniture is a prophecy about Christ.
6. Daniel 8: The Day of Atonement in Prophecy — The ram and goat as sanctuary animals. Christ vs. Satan. Your findings on the compass directions, the typology, the Day of Atonement connection.
7. Daniel 9: The Cross at the Center — The 70 weeks. The karath word study. The 777 encoding. The covenant cut in blood.
8. 1844 and the Judgment — The 2300 days. The judgment scene. Jesus' parables as investigative judgment in miniature.
9. Daniel 10-12: The Final Conflict — The Man in Linen. North vs. South. The two institutions under attack: Sabbath and marriage.

Part III: Revelation (Studies 10-16)
What has the church experienced, and what is coming?

10. Revelation's Architecture — How the book is built. Repeat and enlarge, just like Daniel.
11. Christ Among the Lampstands — Revelation 1. Every physical detail is a sanctuary detail. The Judge is your Advocate.
12. The Seven Churches — History and heart condition. Laodicea as the SDA diagnosis.
13. Seals and Trumpets — What the church suffers. What God does about it. The ark revealed in the seventh trumpet.
14. The Woman and the Dragon — Revelation 12. The panoramic view of the great controversy.
15. The Final Test — Revelation 13-14. The beasts, the mark, the three angels' messages, the 144,000.
16. How This Ends — The plagues, Babylon's fall, the Second Coming, the new earth. The two cities resolve.

Part IV: The Life (Studies 17-20)
What does this demand of you?

17. The Sabbath — The sign from creation to the new earth. Every doctrinal conflict converges here.
18. The State of the Dead — The firewall against the final deception. The serpent's first lie.
19. The 144,000 — What God is waiting for. Character, not knowledge. The 1888 lesson.
20. The Way Home — 2 Peter 1:19. The Day Star in your heart. The point of all prophecy.

Each study follows the same structure:

- A thesis that matters to someone today
- The biblical story told plainly
- Symbols explained as they appear (never assumed)
- Your structural findings as confirmation
- Pioneer and Reformer witnesses
- A practical call at the end

The thread that holds it together: Daniel is a zoom lens. Each chapter covers the same conflict at increasing magnification. Daniel 2 is the wide shot. Daniel 7 zooms into character. Daniel 8 into the sanctuary. Daniel 9 into the cross. Daniel 10-12 into the endgame. Revelation replays it from the church's perspective. Every study answers one question about the hearer's life right now.

Your research appears throughout. The Daniel 2-7 chiasm in Study 2. The four-in-prophecy pattern in Study 3. The ram/goat typology and compass directions in Study 6. The karath word study and 777 encoding in Study 7. The mega-chiasm and Man in Linen in Studies 8 and 9. The NT structure in Study 10. The sabbatismos finding in Study 17. It is all there. But it arrives as confirmation of what the biblical narrative already teaches, rather than as the starting point.

WHAT YOU COULD DO WITH THIS

This draft is just a starting point. You know the material far better than I do, and I am sure there are connections I have missed or things I have framed incorrectly. Here are some ways you might use it:

- Take it as is and develop it. Fill in the structural evidence, add the Hebrew connections, expand where it is thin.
- Use it as a template. Keep the structure (thesis, story, symbols, confirmation, call) but replace my content with your own.
- Ignore the series entirely and just take the format. Pick any one of your existing studies, and rebuild it with: a thesis, a story, symbol explanations, structural confirmation, and a closing call. See how people respond.
- Use it as a reference for what an audience needs. Even if you never teach from this outline, reading through it might clarify where your current presentations could add context, transitions, or practical application.

I have no attachment to this particular outline. It is a tool for you to use, modify, or set aside as you see fit.

The research you have done is real and it matters. The structural findings are genuine. What I keep coming back to is this: the 144,000 are not described by their knowledge. They are described by their character. "In their mouth was found no guile: for they are without fault before the throne of God" (Revelation 14:5). The structures are the lamp. The character is the destination. A study series that walks people from where they are to who God is calling them to become — using your findings as the light along the way — I think that could reach people in a way the raw research has not yet been able to.
