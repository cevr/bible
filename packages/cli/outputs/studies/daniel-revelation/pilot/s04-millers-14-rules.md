# Miller's 14 Rules of Interpretation

**The Reformation gave the church an open Bible. Miller gave the Adventist movement a method for reading it. The 14 Rules are not Miller's invention — they are his fourteen-fold summary of how Scripture itself asks to be read. Every later session in this series stands on these Rules. Strip them out and the chain dissolves into private opinion. Keep them and the chain holds.**

---

## 1. The moment

It is September 1816, in a low farmhouse in Low Hampton, New York. William Miller (1782–1849) — recently a Deist, recently a militia captain, newly broken at the Baptist pulpit — has shut the door of his study and opened his Bible. Beside him sit Cruden's _Concordance_ and a stack of blank paper. The commentaries — Scott, Henry, Clarke — he has deliberately laid aside.

He has decided to do something almost no one was doing: he is going to read straight through the Bible, Genesis to Revelation, allowing every passage that bears on a difficult subject to gather around that subject before he draws any conclusion. If the Book is from God, he reasons, it must be consistent with itself; if it was given to men, it must be intelligible to them. He has begun his study not as a confident believer but as a man who needs to know whether the Book that broke his Deism is in fact what it claims to be. He will not finish for two years. He will not preach what he has found for sixteen.

> "I laid by all commentaries, former views and prepossessions, and determined to read and try to understand for myself. I then began the reading of the Bible in a methodical manner; and by comparing scripture with scripture, and taking notice of the manner of prophesying, and how it was fulfilled… I found, on a close and careful examination of the Scriptures, that God had explained all the figures and metaphors in the Bible, or had given us rules for their explanation."
> — _William Miller, Works vol. 1, p. 12_

He did not yet have the Rules in fourteen articles. He had the _practice_ of them. The articles came later — "by special request," he says — when readers wanted to know how he had reached the conclusions of his _Lectures_. What he eventually wrote down was not a new method but a confession of an old one: the Reformation's method, made systematic, made teachable, made portable to a farmer with a concordance.

A century-and-a-half before, the Reformers had recovered three Latin slogans — _sola Scriptura, tota Scriptura, analogia Scripturae_ (Scripture alone, all of Scripture, Scripture interprets Scripture). Miller had never read the Reformers in Latin. He didn't need to. He had read the Book they had read, by the method they had used, and he had reached the conclusions they would have reached had they lived to walk Daniel 8 with him. **[CHAIN]** What Wycliffe and Luther opened, Miller systematized.

The burden that drove him to write the Rules down was simple. The doctrine he had found — that Christ would return at the end of the 2300 days of Daniel 8:14 — was being attacked not on the Bible but on his _manner of reading_ it. His critics were not (mostly) saying his Hebrew was wrong. They were saying his **method** was wrong: too literal, too systematic, too willing to let one passage settle another. So Miller answered not with another exposition but with the interpretive constitution underneath all his expositions. **The Rules are the bedrock. The lectures are the building.**

## 2. The Scripture opened

### The two anchor texts

Two passages stand under the Rules as a whole. Miller cites them under Rule IV, but they govern the entire fourteen.

> "We have also a more sure word of prophecy; whereunto ye do well that ye take heed, as unto a light that shineth in a dark place, until the day dawn, and the day star arise in your hearts: knowing this first, that no prophecy of the scripture is of any private interpretation. For the prophecy came not in old time by the will of man: but holy men of God spake as they were moved by the Holy Ghost."
> — (2 Peter 1:19–21)

> "Whom shall he teach knowledge? and whom shall he make to understand doctrine? them that are weaned from the milk, and drawn from the breasts. For precept must be upon precept, precept upon precept; line upon line, line upon line; here a little, and there a little."
> — (Isaiah 28:9–10)

**`[KEY🔑]`** "No prophecy of the scripture is of any **private** interpretation." Peter is not forbidding _personal_ reading; he is forbidding _isolated_ reading. A prophecy is not understood by being clipped from the Bible and explained by itself. It is understood by being set among all the other prophecies the same Spirit gave through other holy men. Scripture is one document with one Author; therefore Scripture interprets Scripture.

Isaiah supplies the _how_: precept upon precept, line upon line, here a little and there a little. The method is additive, not deductive. You do not begin with a theory and conscript verses to defend it; you begin with verses and let the theory assemble itself.

These two texts are the seed. The 14 Rules are the tree that grew up out of them.

### The Rules, walked one by one

Miller's own preface, set down in 1840:

> "In studying the Bible, I have found the following rules to be of great service to myself, and now give them to the public by special request. Every rule should be well studied, in connexion with the scripture references, if the Bible student would be at all benefited by them."
> — _MWV1 p. 20_

**Rule I.** "Every word must have its proper bearing on the subject presented in the Bible." **Proof: Matthew 5:18.**

> "For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled."
> — (Matt 5:18)

No word is decorative. No word is filler. Each word in Scripture is doing work in the sentence, the paragraph, the chapter, the book, and the whole Bible. The interpreter's first question is: _what does this word add to the subject?_ — not: _can I get away with skipping this word?_ `[→]` This is why Adventist prophetic study counts kings, days, weeks, years, beasts, horns, heads, and toes. Every detail bears.

**Rule II.** "All scripture is necessary, and may be understood by a diligent application and study." **Proof: 2 Timothy 3:15–17.**

> "All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness: that the man of God may be perfect, throughly furnished unto all good works."
> — (2 Tim 3:16–17)

No book of the Bible is dispensable. No chapter is optional. The man of God is _thoroughly_ furnished only by _all_ of Scripture. Miller is striking at a habit — old then, older now — of declaring some books obscure and quietly setting them aside. Revelation, Daniel, Ezekiel, Leviticus: each is profitable, each may be understood, each is required.

**Rule III.** "Nothing revealed in the scripture can or will be hid from those who ask in faith, not wavering."

> "The secret things belong unto the Lord our God: but those things which are revealed belong unto us and to our children for ever, that we may do all the words of this law."
> — (Deut 29:29)

> "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God, that giveth to all men liberally, and upbraideth not; and it shall be given him. But let him ask in faith, nothing wavering."
> — (James 1:5–6)

The Holy Spirit gave the Book. The Holy Spirit makes the Book plain. Miller's confidence was not in his own intellect; it was in the promise that what God has revealed He intends His children to see. **`[Q]`** When you find a passage opaque, the first question is not _what scholar can decode this for me?_ but _am I asking in faith, nothing wavering?_

**Rule IV.** "To understand doctrine, bring all the scriptures together on the subject you wish to know; then let every word have its proper influence, and if you can form your theory without a contradiction, you cannot be in an error." **Proofs: Isaiah 28:7–29; Luke 24:27, 44–45; Romans 16:26; 2 Peter 1:19–20.**

> "And beginning at Moses and all the prophets, he expounded unto them in all the scriptures the things concerning himself."
> — (Luke 24:27)

This is the rule that built the whole 2300-day argument. It is the rule that built the sanctuary doctrine. It is the rule that will build the Sabbath doctrine and the state-of-the-dead doctrine and the three-angels doctrine in the sessions to come. **`[KEY🔑]`** _A doctrine that cannot be assembled from all the relevant texts without contradiction is not a Bible doctrine._ The test is not authority; the test is harmony. If you have a theory that requires you to explain away three passages to make sense of two, your theory is wrong; the passages are right.

**Rule V.** "Scripture must be its own expositor, since it is a rule of itself."

> "The law of the Lord is perfect, converting the soul: the testimony of the Lord is sure, making wise the simple."
> — (Ps 19:7)

> "But one is your Master, even Christ; and all ye are brethren."
> — (Matt 23:8)

Miller's blunt warning on this one is worth quoting whole:

> "If I depend on a teacher to expound it to me, and he should guess at its meaning, or desire to have it so on account of his sectarian creed, or to be thought wise, then his guessing, desire, creed or wisdom is my rule, not the Bible."
> — _MWV1 p. 21_

`[DYK🔎]` Three centuries before Miller, Luther had said the same thing in different words: _Scriptura sui ipsius interpres_ — Scripture is its own interpreter. Miller never quoted Luther. He didn't need to. He had reached the same Reformation principle by walking the same Reformation Bible.

**Rule VI.** "God has revealed things to come, by visions, in figures and parables, and in this way the same things are oftentime revealed again and again, by different visions, or in different figures, and parables. If you wish to understand them, you must combine them all in one." **Proofs: Hosea 12:10; Habakkuk 2:2; Hebrews 9:9, 24; Genesis 41:1–32; Daniel 2:7–8.**

> "I have also spoken by the prophets, and I have multiplied visions, and used similitudes, by the ministry of the prophets."
> — (Hos 12:10)

`[⟲]` _Recapitulation_ — though Miller does not use that word — is here named as a Bible principle. God says the same thing in multiple symbolic systems on purpose, so that the witnesses corroborate. The image of Daniel 2, the four beasts of Daniel 7, the ram-and-goat of Daniel 8, the heavenly scenes of Revelation — these are not four contradicting prophecies; they are four parallel views of the same arc, each clarifying what the others leave shadowed. Pharaoh's two dreams in Genesis 41 model this: God said the same thing twice, in different figures, **"because the thing is established by God, and God will shortly bring it to pass"** (Gen 41:32). The Adventist instinct for recapitulation comes straight from this rule.

**Rule VII.** "Figures always have a figurative meaning, and are used much in prophecy, to represent future things, times and events; such as mountains, meaning governments; beasts, meaning kingdoms." **Proofs: Daniel 2:35, 44; 7:8, 17; Revelation 17:1, 15; Psalm 119:105; Ezekiel 4:6.**

> "The waters which thou sawest… are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues."
> — (Rev 17:15)

> "I have appointed thee each day for a year."
> — (Ezek 4:6)

`[KEY🔑]` Symbols are not free-floating. They have meanings, and those meanings are fixed by the Bible itself. _Beasts_ means _kingdoms_ (Dan 7:17); _waters_ means _peoples_ (Rev 17:15); _a day_ in prophetic time means _a year_ (Ezek 4:6; Num 14:34); _the lamp_ means _the word of God_ (Ps 119:105). Where Scripture has appointed a symbol, the interpreter does not get to reassign it.

**Rule VIII.** "Parables are used as comparisons to illustrate subjects, and must be explained in the same way as figures by the subject and Bible." Reference: Mark 4:13.

> "Know ye not this parable? and how then will ye know all parables?"
> — (Mark 4:13)

The parable of the sower is the master parable — Christ's hint that the method which unlocks it unlocks the rest. Set the subject; let the Bible supply the meanings of the figures within it; do not invent meanings the Bible does not supply.

**Rule IX.** "Figures sometimes have two or more different significations, as day is used in a figurative sense to represent three different periods of time."

> "In the day of prosperity be joyful, but in the day of adversity consider."
> — (Eccl 7:14)

This rule is the safety latch on Rule VII. _Day_ is a fixed symbol, but it can mean three different fixed things: an indefinite period; one literal year (the year-day principle, Num 14:34); a thousand years (2 Pet 3:8). Context decides which. The interpreter's job is not to pick the meaning he likes; it is to read enough of the surrounding text to know which of the three the Spirit means here.

> **(Rule X — continuation of Rule IX):** _1. Indefinite. 2. Definite, a day for a year. 3. Day for a thousand years._ — _MWV1 p. 22_

(In Miller's own typeset, Rules IX and X are co-numbered around the threefold meaning of _day_. Smith later collapses them; we keep Miller's numbering.)

**Rule XI.** "How to know when a word is used figuratively. If it makes good sense as it stands, and does no violence to the simple laws of nature, then it must be understood literally, if not, figuratively." **Proofs: Revelation 12:1–2; 17:3–7.**

> "There appeared a great wonder in heaven; a woman clothed with the sun, and the moon under her feet…"
> — (Rev 12:1)

A literal woman cannot be clothed with the sun. Therefore the woman is a figure. _Literal first; figurative only when literal fails._ This rule cuts in both directions — it prevents the Adventist from spiritualizing the Second Coming (which makes literal sense) and prevents the futurist from literalizing the locusts of Revelation 9 (which does not).

**Rule XII.** "To learn the true meaning of figures, trace your figurative word through your Bible, and where you find it explained, put it on your figure, and if it makes good sense you need look no further, if not, look again."

The simplest practice of the whole list. Got a figure? Trace it. Where the Bible interprets it, take the Bible's interpretation. Where the Bible doesn't interpret it, slow down. **`[→]`** This is what a concordance is _for_. Miller built the 2300-day argument with Cruden's _Concordance_ in his lap.

**Rule XIII.** "To know whether we have the true historical event for the fulfilment of a prophecy. If you find every word of the prophecy (after the figures are understood) is literally fulfilled, then you may know that your history is the true event. But if one word lacks a fulfilment, then you must look for another event, or wait its future development. For God takes care that history and prophecy doth agree, so that the true believing children of God may never be ashamed." **Proofs: Isaiah 45:17–19; 1 Peter 2:6; Revelation 17:17; Acts 3:18.**

> "He hath put in their hearts to fulfil his will… until the words of God shall be fulfilled."
> — (Rev 17:17)

`[KEY🔑]` This is the rule that authorizes appeal to history at all. Prophecy is not vindicated by being prophesied; it is vindicated by being _fulfilled_. And the test of fulfilment is not approximate — it is exact. _Every word._ If a candidate event leaves a single specified detail unaccounted for, the candidate is wrong and the interpreter must keep looking. This is why Adventist prophetic study is willing to spend a century identifying the right pope-on-the-right-date, the right Sultan, the right decree. **The God who wrote the prophecy is the God who governs the history; the two cannot disagree.**

**Rule XIV.** "The most important rule of all is, that you must have faith."

This rule is the longest. It deserves to be quoted whole:

> "It must be a faith that requires a sacrifice, and, if tried, would give up the dearest object on earth, the world and all its desires, character, living, occupation, friends, home, comforts, and worldly honors. If any of these should hinder our believing any part of God's word, it would show our faith to be vain. Nor can we ever believe so long as one of these motives lies lurking in our hearts. We must believe that God will never forfeit his word. And we can have confidence that he that takes notice of the sparrow, and numbers the hairs of our head, will guard the translation of his own word, and throw a barrier around it, and prevent those who sincerely trust in God, and put implicit confidence in his word, from erring far from the truth, though they may not understand Hebrew or Greek."
> — _MWV1 p. 23_

`[IMG]` Notice what Miller does in his final rule. He removes the gatekeepers. The interpretive constitution of the Adventist movement ends not with a credential but with a condition of the heart. _Faith_, defined as the willingness to lose everything rather than disbelieve God's plain word. The farmer with a concordance and that kind of faith will be guarded by the Holy Spirit "from erring far from the truth, though they may not understand Hebrew or Greek." The seminary professor without that faith will not be. **The 14th Rule sits over all thirteen others as a posture, not a technique.**

### EGW on Miller's method

Forty years later, Ellen White summarized the same Rules in a single sentence:

> "Following his rule of making Scripture its own interpreter, Miller learned that a day in symbolic prophecy represents a year (Numbers 14:34; Ezekiel 4:6); he saw that the period of 2300 prophetic days, or literal years, would extend far beyond the close of the Jewish dispensation, hence it could not refer to the sanctuary of that dispensation."
> — _The Great Controversy, ch. 18_ (132.1481)

"His rule of making Scripture its own interpreter" — Rule V, by name, identified as the engine that produced the 2300-day conclusion. EGW is not crediting Miller with cleverness; she is crediting him with method. The same method anyone can use. The same method she expects the reader to use. The same method this series will use from here on out.

## 3. The application

You are not in 1816. You have access to commentaries Miller never imagined, software searches Miller would have wept for, and translations Miller never saw. None of it will save you from a private interpretation. What saved Miller — what the Reformers had recovered, what Smith inherited, what the SDA pioneers built every doctrinal pillar upon — was not access to tools but obedience to a method. **The 14 Rules are that method.**

`[IMG]` The image of God in you is restored as your _mind_ is brought into harmony with His mind, and His mind is recorded in His Word. Sloppy reading produces a sloppy doctrine of God, and a sloppy doctrine of God produces a sloppy image of God in His worshipper. Miller's Rules are not academic. They are pastoral. The man who reads his Bible by Rule IV — letting every relevant passage settle every doctrinal question without contradiction — will end up worshipping the God who wrote the Bible. The man who clips verses for his pre-existing convictions will end up worshipping the convictions.

So adopt the Rules. Not as Miller's. As _Scripture's_. They are not a confession; they are a confession of how the Book asks to be read. Every session that follows in this series — Daniel 8 and the 2300 days, Daniel 7 and the judgment, Revelation 10 and the Disappointment, Leviticus 16 and the sanctuary, the fourth commandment and the Sabbath, Revelation 14 and the three angels — every one of them stands or falls on these fourteen. Drop a Rule and the doctrine drops with it.

`[Q]` Which Rule do you most often _break_? Rule II (treating some books as dispensable)? Rule IV (defending a position by ignoring the passages that contradict it)? Rule V (outsourcing your conclusions to a teacher)? Rule XI (literalizing what Scripture treats as figure, or figuring what Scripture treats as literal)? Rule XIV (refusing to follow a plain text because of what it would cost)? Name it. The session that fixes the chain in you is the session that fixes the broken Rule.
