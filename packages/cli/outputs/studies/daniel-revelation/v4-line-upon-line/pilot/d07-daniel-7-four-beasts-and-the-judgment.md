# Daniel 7 — Four Beasts and the Judgment

**The same future Daniel 2 drew in metal is now drawn in beasts — and once again the
Bible hands us the dictionary. Beast = kingdom. We do not guess; we read the label off
the page, and watch Revelation copy the very same key.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Daniel 2 taught us
> the key (metal = kingdom). Daniel 7 hands us the next entry (beast = kingdom) — line
> upon line.

---

## The setup — a second sketch of the same future

- Years after the king's dream, **Daniel himself** now dreams (Dan 7:1)
- four winds strive on a **great sea**; four beasts come up (Dan 7:2-3)
- [→] this is **Daniel 2 told over again**, deeper. Same span of history, new picture.
  The Bible does not change its mind — it adds detail. That repeat-with-more-detail is
  how these books teach: recapitulation.

> "To the student of prophecy, the seventh chapter of Daniel is a most important record.
> By a continuous chain of events, the prophet gives the history from the days of Babylon
> to the great investigative judgment, which is the central theme of the chapter."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 88_

[→] mark Haskell's words: the chapter runs **from Babylon to the judgment**. The judgment
is not an afterthought — it is the destination.

---

## [KEY🔑] First entry: the great SEA = peoples

Before the beasts mean anything, the water must. Daniel does not define "sea" here — so we
reach to where the Bible does:

> "The waters which thou sawest... are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and
> tongues." (Revelation 17:15)

- **sea / waters = peoples, nations, tongues** — defined outright by Scripture
- [KEY🔑] so beasts rising "out of the sea" = kingdoms rising **out of the crowded,
  populous, warring world**. The four winds striving on the sea = strife and commotion
  among the nations.

> "The Bible definition of sea, or waters, when used as a symbol, is, peoples, and
> nations, and tongues. In proof of this, see Revelation 17:15..."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 114_

[DYK🔎] This is the same discipline as Dan 2: take the plain sense unless the text itself
makes it figurative.

> "All Scripture language is to be taken literally, unless there exists some good reason
> for supposing it to be figurative; and all that is figurative is to be interpreted..."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 113_

---

## [KEY🔑] Second entry: BEAST = kingdom

We do not have to imagine what the four beasts mean. As in Daniel 2, the interpretation is
given **inside the vision**:

> "These great beasts, which are four, are four kings, which shall arise out of the
> earth." (Daniel 7:17)

And again, plainer, for the fourth:

> "The fourth beast shall be the fourth kingdom upon earth, which shall be diverse from
> all kingdoms, and shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it
> in pieces." (Daniel 7:23)

[KEY🔑] **Beast = kingdom.** The angel says "kings... kingdom" — Scripture handing us the
dictionary, exactly as Dan 2:38-40 said "kingdom" four times. We are not interpreting an
animal; we are reading a label.

> "The definition of the symbol of the four beasts is given to Daniel ere the close of
> the vision. Verse 17: 'These great beasts, which are four, are four kings...'"
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 114_

[→] Same key, second cut. Metal in chapter 2, beast in chapter 7 — **both defined by the
text as kingdom.** Learn it once, it opens every vision ahead.

---

## The four beasts named in history

Scripture gives the meaning (four successive kingdoms); the four beasts simply re-draw the
four metals of Daniel 2. Haskell ties the two visions together with the first beast:

> "Four great beasts came up from the sea; that is, they arose into prominence from the
> midst of the multitudes of earth. Babylon, the first of these kingdoms, was represented
> to Nebuchadnezzar as the golden head of the great image."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 90_

- **Lion (with eagle's wings) = Babylon** — the gold head, now winged
  - [⟲] the gold head of Dan 2 is the lion of Dan 7 — _same kingdom, two pictures_
    > "At first the lion had eagle's wings, denoting the rapidity with which Babylon
    > extended its conquests under Nebuchadnezzar..."
    > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 115_
- **Bear = Medo-Persia** — the silver breast, now a bear, raised on one side, three ribs
  in its mouth
  > "The Medo-Persian kingdom was bloodthirsty and cruel in its nature, and is
  > represented by a bear."
  > — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 91_
- **Leopard (four wings, four heads) = Greece** — the brass belly, now a swift four-winged
  leopard
  > "The Grecian kingdom, which followed the Medo-Persian, is compared to the
  > sprightliness of a leopard in its natural state."
  > — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 92_
- **Fourth beast (dreadful, no name) = Rome** — the iron legs, now a beast so terrible no
  animal fit it
  > "there was no animal with a character to represent its terrible nature; so a beast
  > without name." [the fourth beast = Rome]
  > — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 92_

[DYK🔎] Same four kingdoms as Dan 2 — Babylon, Medo-Persia, Greece, Rome — confirmed by
Dan 5:28 (Medes and Persians) and Dan 8:21 (Grecia named outright). The Bible labels its
own symbols; history only supplies the dates.

## [KEY🔑] The TEN HORNS = ten kings

The fourth beast has ten horns. Again — defined in the text:

> "And the ten horns out of this kingdom are ten kings that shall arise..." (Daniel 7:24)

- **ten horns = ten kings** — Rome breaking into the divided kingdoms of Europe
- [⟲] this is the **iron mixed with clay** of Dan 2:41-43 — the one empire that fragments
  instead of being conquered. Same prophecy, sharper detail.

## The little horn — defined by its marks, one verse at a time

Among the ten, a new power rises:

> "...there came up among them another little horn, before whom there were three of the
> first horns plucked up by the roots..." (Daniel 7:8)

Daniel asks who it is. The angel answers with a string of identifying marks — and **each
mark is a Scripture, not a guess.** We do not name this power; the verses fence it in until
only one power on earth fits.

> "And another shall rise after them; and he shall be diverse from the first, and he shall
> subdue three kings." (Daniel 7:24)

- **Mark 1 — among the ten, after the ten** — it rises out of divided Rome, after the
  barbarian kingdoms (Dan 7:8, 24)
- **Mark 2 — plucks up three horns** — three of the ten fall before it (Dan 7:8, 24)
  > "the three horns plucked up were... the Heruli in A.D. 493, the Vandals in 534, and
  > the Ostrogoths in 538."
  > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 136_
- **Mark 3 — "eyes like the eyes of a man, and a mouth speaking great things"** (Dan 7:8)
  — an intelligent, religious, speaking power
  > "...the eyes, a fit emblem of the shrewdness, penetration, cunning, and foresight of
  > the papal hierarchy..." [little horn = the papacy]
  > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 119_
- **Mark 4 — "speak great words against the most High"** — blasphemy (Dan 7:25)
- **Mark 5 — "wear out the saints of the most High"** — a persecuting power (Dan 7:25)
- **Mark 6 — "think to change times and laws"** — claims authority over God's law (Dan
  7:25)
- **Mark 7 — rules "a time and times and the dividing of time"** — a fixed prophetic span
  (Dan 7:25)

> "And he shall speak great words against the most High, and shall wear out the saints of
> the most High, and think to change times and laws: and they shall be given into his hand
> until a time and times and the dividing of time." (Daniel 7:25)

[→] Seven marks; one power fits all seven — a religious-political power, rising out of
fallen Rome, after the ten, uprooting three, speaking against God, wearing out the saints,
for a set time. Haskell flags exactly what kind of power this is:

> "And, behold, there came up among them another little horn, before which there were
> three of the first horns plucked up by the roots. A new power, a power outside the
> empire is here represented."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 94_

[→] "A power outside the empire" — not another political kingdom like the first four, but a
new kind of power rising among them. Mark 5 alone settles its character: it makes war on
the saints.

> "Daniel beheld this horn making war upon the saints. Has such a war been waged by the
> papacy? Fifty million martyrs... answer, Yes. Witness the cruel persecutions of the
> Waldenses, the Albigenses, and Protestants in general."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 125_

And the prophetic eye confirms the same identity — the power that thought to change God's
times and laws, established in the sixth century:

> "Prophecy had declared that the papacy was to 'think to change times and laws.'
> Daniel 7:25."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 51_

> "In the sixth century the papacy had become firmly established... Paganism had given
> place to the papacy."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 54_

## [KEY🔑] The 1260 — "a time and times and the dividing of time"

Mark 7 is a time prophecy. Decode it where the text raises it:

> "...and they shall be given into his hand until a time and times and the dividing of
> time." (Daniel 7:25)

- **a time = 1 year; times = 2 years; dividing of time = ½ year** → **3½ prophetic years**
- a prophetic year = 360 days → 3½ × 360 = **1260 days**
- the year-day principle (Num 14:34; Eze 4:6) → **1260 days = 1260 literal years**

The dates, read off history:

- **538 — supremacy begins.** The third horn (Ostrogoths) is pulled, leaving the bishop of
  Rome in undisputed possession
  > "...the Goths, retiring, left him in undisputed possession of Rome in 538."
  > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 135_
- **538 + 1260 = 1798 — supremacy ends.** Berthier's army takes the pope captive; the
  wound is given.

[→] **538 → 1798.** A 1260-year span the prophecy named in advance, opened and closed on
schedule. The arithmetic is plain; the history filled it in.

## [⟲] The weave — the Bible wrote the cross-reference

Now the move this whole series is built on. **Revelation deliberately re-draws Daniel 7.**
We are not importing a parallel — John copied Daniel's key on purpose.

**[⟲] Rev 13:2 — the three beasts re-assembled into one:**

> "And the beast which I saw was like unto a leopard, and his feet were as the feet of a
> bear, and his mouth as the mouth of a lion: and the dragon gave him his power, and his
> seat, and great authority." (Revelation 13:2)

- leopard + bear + lion — **Daniel's beasts, named in reverse, fused into Revelation's one
  beast.** John is pointing straight back at Daniel 7. The Bible wrote the weave.

**[⟲] Rev 13:5 — the same 1260, restated as 42 months:**

> "...a mouth speaking great things and blasphemies; and power was given unto him to
> continue forty and two months." (Revelation 13:5)

- "mouth speaking great things" = Dan 7:8, 25, word for word
- **42 months × 30 days = 1260 days** = Dan 7:25's "time, times, and dividing of time."
  Same span, second wording.

**[⟲] Rev 13:7 — the same war on the saints:**

> "...to make war with the saints, and to overcome them..." (Revelation 13:7)

- this is Dan 7:21, 25 — _"the same horn made war with the saints, and prevailed against
  them"_ (Dan 7:21). Daniel's little horn and Revelation's beast do the **same deed**.

**[⟲] Rev 12:6 + Rev 12:14 — the SAME 1260, stated a third way:**

> "And the woman fled into the wilderness... a thousand two hundred and threescore days."
> (Revelation 12:6)

> "...for a time, and times, and half a time..." (Revelation 12:14)

- Rev 12:6 says **1260 days**; Rev 12:14 says **time, times, half a time** — the very
  phrase of Dan 7:25.

[→] Count them: the **same 1260** appears as "time, times, dividing of time" (Dan 7:25),
"42 months" (Rev 13:5), "1260 days" (Rev 12:6), and "time, times, half a time" (Rev
12:14) — **four statements, one span.** And the prophetic eye says the same:

> "The periods here mentioned—'forty and two months,' and 'a thousand two hundred and
> threescore days'—are the same, alike representing the time in which the church of Christ
> was to suffer oppression from Rome. The 1260 years of papal supremacy..."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 266_

[→] The Bible cross-references itself; EGW only confirms what the four texts already say.
The weave is not ours; it is theirs.

## The judgment — the destination of the chapter

The little horn does not get the last word. The scene shifts to heaven:

> "I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit..." (Daniel
> 7:9)

> "I saw in the night visions, and, behold, one like the Son of man came with the clouds
> of heaven, and came to the Ancient of days... And there was given him dominion, and
> glory, and a kingdom, that all people, nations, and languages, should serve him..."
> (Daniel 7:13-14)

- **Ancient of days = God the Father**, taking the seat of judgment
  > "The 'Ancient of days,' God the Father, takes the throne of judgment..."
  > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 120_
- thrones set, the books opened (Dan 7:10) — **a court convenes in heaven**
  > "The work of the investigative judgment is introduced in the prophecy..."
  > — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 122_
- [→] **one like the Son of man** comes to the Ancient of days and receives "dominion, and
  glory, and a kingdom... an everlasting dominion" (Dan 7:13-14) — this is the stone of
  **Dan 2:44** again, the kingdom that shall never be destroyed, here shown being
  **awarded in the courtroom** rather than smashing the image.

And the verdict goes to the persecuted:

> "...the same horn made war with the saints, and prevailed against them; Until the
> Ancient of days came, and judgment was given to the saints of the Most High; and the
> time came that the saints possessed the kingdom." (Daniel 7:21-22)

> "But the judgment shall sit, and they shall take away his dominion, to consume and to
> destroy it unto the end." (Daniel 7:26)

- [→] the horn made war and "prevailed" — **for a time.** Then the court sits, the dominion
  is taken away, and the kingdom passes to the saints. This is the stone of Dan 2:44
  again — God's everlasting kingdom — now shown being **awarded in a courtroom**.
- [⟲] this judgment is the chapter's heart — Haskell called it "the central theme" (SDP
  88). The 2300-day cleansing of Dan 8:14 will date it; we reach that at D08-D09.

## Appeal

- The God who drew the rise and fall of Babylon, Persia, Greece, and Rome twice — once in
  metal, once in beasts — and named in advance a 1260-year span that opened in 538 and
  closed in 1798, is the God who **sits in the judgment now.**
- No earthly power, however great its words, escapes that court. _"The judgment shall sit,
  and they shall take away his dominion"_ (Dan 7:26). The little horn's clock ran out;
  God's never does.
- And the verdict is for the saints: _"judgment was given to the saints of the Most
  High"_ (Dan 7:22). The persecuted inherit the kingdom. **Take your stand with the One
  who wins the case.**
