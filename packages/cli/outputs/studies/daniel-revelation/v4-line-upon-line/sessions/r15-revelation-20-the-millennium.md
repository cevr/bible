# Revelation 20 — The Millennium and the Two Resurrections

**One chapter spans a thousand years and two resurrections — and once again the Bible
refuses to leave its own symbols undefined. The dragon names himself, the first
resurrection names itself, the second death names itself. We do not guess at the
millennium; we read the labels off the page and let Daniel's judgment-court and the
sanctuary scapegoat snap the picture into place.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Watch Revelation 20
> hand us three keys in its own words — and let the guides supply the sense the verse only
> names — line upon line.

---

## The setup — the chapter that buries a thousand years between two awakenings

- Rev 20 opens straight off Rev 19's destruction of the beast and false prophet (R14) —
  the **next scene in one unbroken vision**
- an angel comes down with **the key of the bottomless pit and a great chain** (Rev 20:1)
- [→] frame the whole chapter as **ONE span** bracketed by **TWO resurrections** (Rev 20:5)
  and closed by **ONE fire** — keep oriented before any symbol is decoded
- the chapter's destination is **the desolate earth**, not a golden reign on earth:

> "The revelator foretells the banishment of Satan and the condition of chaos and desolation
> to which the earth is to be reduced, and he declares that this condition will exist for a
> thousand years."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 658_

[→] mark EGW's word: **banishment** and **chaos and desolation**. That is where the
chapter is headed — hold it before we decode a single symbol.

## [KEY🔑] The dragon names himself — Satan, in his own four-fold label

The first actor steps on stage already labeled. The verse defines its own symbol **four
ways**, no overlay needed:

> "And he laid hold on the **dragon**, that **old serpent**, which is the **Devil**, and
> **Satan**, and bound him a thousand years." (Revelation 20:2)

[KEY🔑] **dragon = old serpent = the Devil = Satan** — Scripture handing us the dictionary
inside the verse. We are not interpreting a beast; we are reading four labels stacked on
one person.

- [⟲] this is the **SAME** entity, defined the **same four ways**, as Rev 12:9:

> "And the great dragon was cast out, that old serpent, called the Devil, and Satan, which
> deceiveth the whole world." (Revelation 12:9)

- the identical four-fold self-definition — the reader met this exact key back at **R09**.
  No ambiguity of identity across chapters: **same person**, bound here that he _"should
  deceive the nations no more"_ (Rev 20:3).
- [→] this is the genuine [KEY🔑] — the **verse's own words**. Everything else in the
  chapter that gets called a "key" elsewhere is in fact an overlay; this one is not.

## [→] The bottomless pit = the desolate, chaotic earth

> "And cast him into the **bottomless pit**, and shut him up, and set a seal upon him, that
> he should deceive the nations no more, till the thousand years should be fulfilled."
> (Revelation 20:3)

[→] this is an **OVERLAY** reading, established by the guide — **not** a verse
self-definition, so it carries the quote, never a bare [KEY🔑]. The verse says "bottomless
pit" (Gk. _abussos_); the guide supplies what it means:

> "The original word signifies an abyss, bottomless, deep, profound... the passage which
> specially throws light upon the meaning of the word here is Genesis 1:2, where we read
> that 'darkness was upon the face of the deep.'... it is applied to this earth in its
> chaotic state. Precisely this it must mean in this third verse of Revelation 20."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 690_

And Haskell paints the same desolation — the broken earth as the abyss itself:

> "The earth, broken and torn by the reelings to and fro in the seventh plague, is dark and
> dismal. It is without form, and void, and darkness is upon the face of the deep... It is
> chaos, the bottomless pit, or the abyss of Rotherham's translation... alone on the earth,
> from which all life is gone."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 327_

[DYK🔎] Hebrew _tehom_ (Gen 1:2) = Greek _abussos_ — auxiliary confirmation only. The
English "deep / bottomless" already yields it; no point hinges on the lexicon.

- [⟲] **DIFFERENT** application from the "bottomless pit" of Rev 9:1-2 (the historicist
  overlay applied to the Arabian desert / Saracen armies, R06) and the beast _"out of the
  bottomless pit"_ (Rev 11:7; Rev 17:8). **Same word, different referent** — Rev 20's use
  describes the millennium-state of the **whole earth**, not a historical actor. Never blur
  the same word across different applications.

## [→] The binding of Satan = no subjects left to deceive

> "And bound him a thousand years... that he should deceive the nations no more, till the
> thousand years should be fulfilled." (Revelation 20:2-3)

[→] **OVERLAY** reading — the chain is the chain of **circumstance**, established by the
guides; carries the quote. The verse says "bound"; the guides supply the sense:

> "Satan, in order to work, must have subjects upon whom to work. Without these, he can do
> nothing... all the saints are in heaven, beyond the power of his temptations; and all the
> wicked are in their graves, beyond his power to deceive... condemned throughout this
> period to a state of hopeless inactivity."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 691_

> "It is in this sense that he is bound: there are none remaining, upon whom he can exercise
> his power. He is wholly cut off from the work of deception and ruin."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 659_

- saints **in heaven**, wicked **in their graves** — nobody left on earth to deceive. The
  chain is not a literal fetter; it is a kingdom emptied of subjects.
- [→] read off the verse, sense supplied by the guides: he is "bound" because there is no
  one left to deceive. The [KEY🔑] stays reserved for the verse's own words; here the verse
  says "bound," and DAR + EGW supply what that binding _is_.

## [→] The angel who binds him is not Christ — the scapegoat "fit man"

> "And I saw **an angel come down from heaven**, having the key of the bottomless pit and a
> great chain in his hand." (Revelation 20:1)

Who is this angel? [→] **OVERLAY** drawn from the sanctuary type (Lev 16); carries the
guide quote, never a bare key:

> "Is this angel Christ, as some suppose? — Evidently not... On the day of atonement,
> anciently, two goats were taken... one for the Lord, and the other for the scape-goat...
> Satan is shown to be the antitypical scape-goat."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 687_

- [⟲] reach to the **SANCTUARY** thread the reader carries from Dan 8:14 / Lev 16 (D09):
  Christ is the High Priest who **finishes** the atonement; the scapegoat (Azazel) is led
  away by a _"fit man"_ (Lev 16:21-22) into a **land not inhabited** — the antitype is
  Satan banished to the desolate earth.

> "The scapegoat, bearing the sins of Israel, was sent away 'unto a land not inhabited'
> (Leviticus 16:22); so Satan, bearing the guilt of all the sins which he has caused God's
> people to commit, will be for a thousand years confined to the earth, which will then be
> desolate, without inhabitant, and he will at last suffer the full penalty of sin in the
> fires that shall destroy all the wicked."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 485_

[→] the angel binds; Satan is the one bound and banished. The Day-of-Atonement type
running since Dan 8:14 lands here: high priest finished, scapegoat led away, wilderness
that holds no life.

## [KEY🔑] The first resurrection — the verse names it

The dead now divide into two awakenings a thousand years apart — and the verse labels the
first one outright:

> "And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and judgment was given unto them... and they
> lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years. But the rest of the dead lived not again
> until the thousand years were finished. **This is the first resurrection**." (Revelation
> 20:4-5)

[KEY🔑] **"This is the first resurrection"** — the verse defines its own term (Rev 20:5).
Scripture handing us the dictionary again; we are not naming the event, we are reading its
label.

> "...lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years... This is the first resurrection, when
> the righteous dead will come forth at the sound of the voice of Christ, and with the
> living righteous, meet the Lord in the air."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 328_

- [⟲] **DIFFERENT** from the second resurrection — explicitly **two events** separated by a
  thousand years; Rev 20:5 itself defines the gap:

> "...no language could more plainly prove two resurrections; the first, a resurrection of
> the righteous at the commencement of the thousand years; and the second, that of the
> wicked at the end of that period."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 693_

- [⟲] Daniel foretold this two-fold awakening: _"many of them that sleep in the dust of the
  earth shall awake, some to everlasting life, and some to shame and everlasting contempt"_
  (Dan 12:2). Two destinies in Daniel; Rev 20:5 now separates them by a thousand years.
  - [→] note: this Dan 12:2 weave is **defensible canon** (two plain destinies) but is
    subordinate here — the two-resurrection verdict rests first on **Rev 20:5's own
    self-definition** plus **DAR 693.4**, which carry the receipts.

## [→] The saints reign and judge in heaven during the thousand years

> "And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and **judgment was given unto them**: and I
> saw the souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus... and they lived and
> reigned with Christ a thousand years." (Revelation 20:4)

[→] **OVERLAY**: the saints are in **heaven** (the New Jerusalem), not on a renewed earth —
carries the guide quote:

> "the saints are with Christ in heaven during the thousand years while the earth lies
> desolate... the saints and the city come down, and the wicked dead are raised and come up
> against it; that the latter there receive their judgment."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 696_

> "During the thousand years, the saints live in the New Jerusalem... and as priests of God
> and of Christ, they sit in judgment on the cases of the wicked. Do ye not know... that the
> saints shall judge the world?"
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 329_

And the prophetic eye shows the books opened in that millennial court:

> "Jesus and the saints sit in judgment. The books are opened — the book of life and the
> book of death... The saints, in unison with Jesus, pass their judgment upon the wicked
> dead."
> — _EGW, Early Writings, p. 52_

- [⟲] **SAME** judgment scene as Dan 7:22 — EGW links Rev 20:4 directly to Daniel's court:

> "Daniel declares that when the Ancient of Days came, 'judgment was given to the saints of
> the Most High.' Daniel 7:22. At this time the righteous reign as kings and priests unto
> God. John in the Revelation says: 'I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and judgment was
> given unto them.'"
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 660_

- the Dan 7 judgment-by-the-saints **IS** the Rev 20 millennial judgment work — two visions
  of **one assize**. The key held since D07 pays off here.
- [→] so the great-white-throne of Rev 20:11-15 is the **execution** of a sentence the
  saints already adjudicated during the thousand years — not a fresh verdict. The "books"
  (Rev 20:12) are the heavenly records reviewed across the millennium (SSP 329.1; EW 52.2),
  read here as overlay, not as a self-defining symbol.

## [→] Satan as scapegoat through the thousand years

The bound dragon, alone on the desolate earth, contemplates _"the record of the past six
thousand years of rebellion"_ (SSP 327.1). [→] **OVERLAY**: the Day-of-Atonement transfer
is now complete — carries the guide quote:

> "When Christ finishes His work in the temple, the sins of Israel will all be laid upon
> Satan: and during the thousand years on the earth, alone and desolate, the sins which he
> tempted the redeemed to commit, will rest heavily on his heart... During the thousand
> years the righteous reign with Christ, and with Him, go through the Book of Death,
> awarding punishment."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 333_

- [⟲] this **closes the SANCTUARY arc** the reader has carried since Dan 8:14 / Lev 16: the
  high priest's finished atonement, the confession over the scapegoat's head, the banishment
  to the wilderness — antitype fulfilled in the bound Satan bearing the guilt he caused
  (ties back to GC 485.3 from the angel-section).

## [→] The second resurrection — the wicked raised, Gog and Magog mustered

> "But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished... And
> when the thousand years are expired, Satan shall be loosed out of his prison, and shall go
> out to deceive the nations... **Gog and Magog**, to gather them together to battle...
> and compassed the camp of the saints about." (Revelation 20:5,7-9)

[→] **OVERLAY** for the descent + muster; carries the guide quote:

> "At the end of the one thousand years, the holy city, the New Jerusalem... comes down, and
> is located upon the earth, and becomes the camp of the saints, around which the resurrected
> wicked come up, numberless as the sand of the sea. The Devil deceives them, and thus brings
> them up to this battle."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 694_

> "At the voice of God, the earth gave up the dead... They rise to see the holy city come
> down from God out of heaven. The Mount of Olives cleaves asunder and the city with all its
> inhabitants rests there... Then Satan marshals the hosts of the wicked."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 334_

> "As the wicked dead are raised and he sees the vast multitudes upon his side, his hopes
> revive... He proposes to lead them against the camp of the saints and to take possession
> of the City of God."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 663_

> "At the close of the thousand years the second resurrection will take place. Then the
> wicked will be raised from the dead and appear before God for the execution of 'the
> judgment written.'"
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 661_

- [⟲] **Gog and Magog** (Rev 20:8) — **DIFFERENT** from Ezekiel 38-39's Gog and Magog (the
  historicist northern-power application). Here the name is applied **typologically** to the
  entire resurrected wicked host:

> "The individuals of whom those organizations were then composed, now come up in the second
> resurrection... under the names, Gog and Magog."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 696_

- a name for the **final confederacy**, not a literal nation.
- [⟲] the **beast and false prophet** (Rev 20:10) — the **organizations** were the **SAME**
  ones destroyed at the millennium's start (Rev 19:20, R14); their individual **members** now
  rise in the second resurrection as the army. Same powers, now their members resurrected —
  never blur.

## [KEY🔑] The lake of fire = the second death — the verse names it

The chapter's final symbol labels itself, exactly as the first did:

> "And death and hell were cast into the lake of fire. **This is the second death**. And
> whosoever was not found written in the book of life was cast into the lake of fire."
> (Revelation 20:14-15)

[KEY🔑] **"the lake of fire... This is the second death"** — the verse defines its own
symbol (Rev 20:14). Scripture handing us the dictionary one last time; we read the label,
not a guess.

- fire comes down from God and devours them (Rev 20:9) — **the same fire that destroys also
  purifies the earth**:

> "Fire comes down from God out of heaven, and mingles with the fire which comes from the
> interior of the earth; and it devours them... And death and hell were cast into the lake
> of fire... This is the second death."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 336_

> "Fire will come down from God out of heaven and devour them — burn them up root and
> branch. Satan is the root, and his children are the branches. The same fire that will
> devour the wicked will purify the earth."
> — _EGW, Early Writings, p. 51_

- [→] **OVERLAY** (carries quote): the lake of fire is the **agency** of destruction; the
  second death is **annihilation**, not eternal conscious torment:

> "...may it not be called a symbol of the agencies which God employs to close up his
> controversy with the living wicked at the beginning of the thousand years, and with all
> the hosts of the ungodly at the end of that period? Literal fire will of course be largely
> employed in this work."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 697_

> "This is the final epitaph of all the forces that have risen up from first to last, to
> oppose the will and work of the Lord Almighty... The fire is to them everlasting because it
> allows of no escape... they never find release from its dread embrace."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 701_

- [⟲] **SAME** lake / **same** event across Rev 19:20 (beast + false prophet), Rev 20:10
  (the devil), Rev 20:15 (all not in the book of life) — **one final fire**, the close of
  the controversy. And out of it comes the new world:

> "From the purifying fires which destroy them come forth the new heavens and the new
> earth."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 696_

## Appeal

- The God who **named** two resurrections a thousand years apart, who lets the bound dragon
  sit alone with **six thousand years of rebellion**, and who writes the final **epitaph in
  fire** — is the God whose judgment-court (Dan 7:22) has already given the verdict **TO the
  saints**.
- Two books, two resurrections, two destinies: the **book of life** or the **book of
  death**; the first resurrection _"blessed and holy"_ (Rev 20:6), or the second death from
  which there is _"no escape"_ (DAR 701.2).
- The fire that ends the rebellion **births the new earth** — and the redeemed live in it:
  > "The feet of the wicked will never desecrate the earth made new."
  > — _EGW, Early Writings, p. 51_
- [→] the only safe side is **inside the city**, in the **first resurrection** — your name
  in the **book of life**. Take your stand now, while the High Priest still bears the sins
  away.
