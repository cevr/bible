# Revelation 8-9 — The Seven Trumpets

**The Bible already drew this empire-sweep twice — in Daniel's metals and Daniel's
beasts. So when the seven trumpets sound, we do not guess. The verse names its own
symbols — Abaddon the destroyer, the locusts, the three woes — and the year-day clock the
prophecy carried opened and closed on the calendar: 1299 → 1449 → August 11, 1840. The
trumpets are God's warning judgments on the empire, and the dates are nails in a sure
place.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Where a verse labels
> its own symbol, that is a [KEY🔑]. Where DAR and Haskell lay history over the symbol,
> that is a [→] connector carrying the guide's own words. Watch which is which.

---

## The setup — a third sweep of the same era, now political

- years into Revelation's recapitulation, John watches **seven angels given seven
  trumpets** (Rev 8:2) — a **new and distinct** series, not a continuation of the seals
- [→] the seals gave us **church** history (the inner life of the church); the trumpets
  give us the **principal political and warlike events** of the same gospel dispensation:

> "This verse introduces a new and distinct series... in the seals we have had the history
> of the church... in the seven trumpets... the principal political and warlike events."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 453_

- [→] Haskell frames the whole book in three passes — same era, three angles:

> "The messages to the seven churches are ecclesiastical history... The seven seals reveal
> the inner workings of the church,-the individual experience... Another phase of history,
> not wholly national, but having to do with nations, is revealed in the sounding of the
> trumpets... the seventh trumpet carrying history into eternity, like the seventh church
> and the seventh seal."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 145_

- **NOT a [KEY🔑]:** no verse says "the trumpets ARE judgments on the empire." That is the
  DAR/Haskell historicist **overlay** — carried here as a [→] teaching connector with the
  guide quote attached, never as the verse's own key.
- [→] this frames the session: **same era as the churches (R02) and the seals (R04-05),
  third pass** — this time tracking the nations.

## [KEY🔑] The seven trumpets = the seven angels' summons (Rev 8:2)

> "And I saw the seven angels which stood before God; and to them were given seven
> trumpets." (Revelation 8:2)

- [KEY🔑] the verse names the **seven trumpets** in-text as the instruments given to the
  seven angels. The only thing the verse itself defines is the **symbol-object** (seven
  angels, seven trumpets) — **not** their historical meaning.
- the historical **meaning** (political judgments) is the overlay carried in the prior
  section's [→]. Here we register only what the text plainly shows.
- [→] the trumpets are the **complement** to Daniel — they pick up exactly where the
  metals and beasts left off, at Rome's fragmentation:

> "Their sounding comes in as a complement to the prophecy of Daniel 2 and 7, commencing
> with the breaking up of the old Roman empire into its ten divisions, of which, in the
> first four trumpets, we have a description."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 454_

- [→] so the **first four trumpets** describe Rome's breakup — the very ground Daniel 2's
  feet and Daniel 7's ten horns already covered.

## The censer cast down — the prelude before the trumpets sound (Rev 8:3-5)

Before any trumpet sounds, the scene pauses at the golden altar:

> "And another angel came and stood at the altar, having a golden censer; and there was
> given unto him much incense, that he should offer it with the prayers of all saints upon
> the golden altar which was before the throne... And the angel took the censer, and filled
> it with fire of the altar, and cast it into the earth: and there were voices, and
> thunderings, and lightnings, and an earthquake." (Revelation 8:3-5)

- [→] this angel is **NOT** one of the seven trumpet angels. It is the work of
  **mediation** — Christ's whole ministry of intercession in the heavenly sanctuary during
  the gospel dispensation:

> "Doubtless the entire work of mediation for the people of God during the gospel
> dispensation is here presented... the angel offers his incense with the prayers of all
> saints."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 453_

- [→] the **casting-down** of the censer = the **END** of that intercession — and only
  then do the voices, thunderings, lightnings, and earthquake follow:

> "his work is then done; no more prayers are to be offered up mingled with incense; and
> this symbolic act can have its application only at the time when the ministration of
> Christ in the sanctuary in behalf of mankind has forever ceased."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 453_

[⟲] **the SAME event, confirmed by the prophetic eye.** What DAR reads off Rev 8 is the
very scene EGW was shown — Jesus, ministering before the ark, throwing down the censer:

> "Then I saw Jesus, who had been ministering before the ark containing the ten
> commandments, throw down the censer. He raised His hands, and with a loud voice said, 'It
> is done.' ... 'He that is unjust, let him be unjust still.'"
> — _EGW, Early Writings, p. 279_

- [⟲] close of Christ's intercession, **end of probation.** This is the **SAME single
  eschatological event** later shown at **Rev 11:19** (the temple opened, the ark seen) and
  **Rev 16:17-18** ("It is done"). It is **NOT** a recapitulated or different instance —
  one close of probation, drawn three times.
- [DYK🔎] the censer scene stands at the **head** of the trumpet vision: before the
  judgments of the nations are sounded, the prophecy shows that Christ's mediation is the
  mercy holding it all back — until the day it is cast down.

## The first four trumpets — Rome broken into ten (Rev 8:7-12)

Trumpets 1-4 fall on the **Western Roman Empire** — the nations God used to break it up:

- **First trumpet — the Goths under Alaric**
- **Second trumpet — the Vandals under Genseric**
- **Third trumpet — the Huns under Attila**
- **Fourth trumpet — the extinction of Western Rome**

[→] Haskell reads the theological lesson under the history: the first four trumpets are
God's **judgment-lesson** to Rome. A nation that rejects the righteousness of Christ for
self-righteousness is given over to subjugation — exactly as Nebuchadnezzar's boast _"Is
not this great Babylon, that I have built"_ was answered by his fall:

> "God, in the voice of the first four trumpets, taught this lesson to the Roman Empire."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 148_

- [→] the fourth trumpet's outcome: Rome **shattered into the ten divisions** — the
  iron-and-miry-clay of Daniel 2 that will never re-weld; and the first of the ten,
  the **Heruli** under Odoacer (an Arian), is the **first horn plucked up** by the little
  horn (Dan 7:8):

> "Rome was now broken into fragments, and the ten divisions presented to the prophet
> Daniel were each given power. As iron and miry clay refuse to unite, so the fragments of
> the Western Roman Empire will remain separate until the end of time."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 159_

### [⟲] The ten divisions = Daniel 2's feet = Daniel 7's ten horns

[⟲] the ten divisions here are the **SAME instance** as Dan 2's **feet** and Dan 7's **ten
horns** — DAR ties the trumpets straight to "Daniel 2 and 7... the ten divisions" (DAR
454), and Haskell equates them with Dan 2:41-43 (SSP 159). These are Rome's **4th-6th
century fragmentation.**

- [⟲] this is **NOT** the end-time ten of **Rev 17:12** — kings who _"have received no
  kingdom as yet... receive power as kings one hour with the beast"_ — which is a
  **DIFFERENT, future** ten. **Never blur the two:** the trumpets' ten are behind us
  (medieval Rome); Rev 17's ten are the closing scene.

## [KEY🔑] The three woe-trumpets announced (Rev 8:13)

> "And I beheld, and heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven, saying with a loud
> voice, Woe, woe, woe, to the inhabiters of the earth by reason of the other voices of the
> trumpet of the three angels, which are yet to sound!" (Revelation 8:13)

- [KEY🔑] the verse itself marks the **three remaining trumpets** (5th, 6th, 7th) as **WOE**
  trumpets — the text supplies the label outright: **"woe, woe, woe."**
- [→] DAR confirms the count — and that this announcing angel is not one of the seven:

> "This angel is not one of the series of the seven trumpet angels, but simply one who
> announces that the three remaining trumpets are woe trumpets... Thus the next, or fifth
> trumpet, is the first woe; the sixth trumpet, the second woe; and the seventh, the last
> one in this series of seven trumpets, is the third woe."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 468_

- [→] this sets up the two woes the rest of the session walks. The **seventh woe** (the
  third) sounds later — carried forward into R07/R11.

## The fifth trumpet / first woe — the Saracens (Rev 9:1-11)

> "And the fifth angel sounded, and I saw a star fall from heaven unto the earth: and to
> him was given the key of the bottomless pit. And he opened the bottomless pit; and there
> arose a smoke out of the pit... and there came out of the smoke locusts upon the earth."
> (Revelation 9:1-3)

- [→] **NOT a [KEY🔑] for "star" or "bottomless pit"** — no verse defines them in-text. DAR
  decodes the bottomless pit by the Greek _abussos_ (a waste, desolate place) applied to the
  Arabian desert, and the fall of Chosroes as the opening that loosed the Saracens. This is
  **auxiliary lexicon + historicist overlay**, carried as a [→] connector:

> "It may appropriately refer to the unknown wastes of the Arabian desert, from the borders
> of which issued the hordes of Saracens, like swarms of locusts."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 472_

- [→] Haskell reads the same picture: the locusts **are** the Saracens, the smoke = the
  dense Mohammedan doctrine issuing from a furnace, after the typology of the eighth
  plague on Egypt:

> "The Saracens themselves are called locusts by the prophet John, and the doctrine which
> impelled their actions was as a dense smoke, issuing out of a furnace. The work of these
> locust-like warriors is described in the eighth plague, sent upon the land of Egypt."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 165_

Now the verse itself draws a line — and **this** is a key:

> "And it was commanded them that they should not hurt the grass of the earth, neither any
> green thing, neither any tree; but only those men which have not the seal of God in their
> foreheads." (Revelation 9:4)

- [KEY🔑] the locusts' **commission is defined IN-TEXT** by the **seal of God.** The verse
  itself marks who is struck and who is spared — the line is the seal.
- [⟲] **the SAME seal as Rev 7:3** — the 144,000 sealed in R05; the seal of God = the
  Sabbath of the fourth commandment. The Saracens were loosed against the **unsealed**
  (apostate Christendom), not against the sealed Sabbath-keepers. **SAME seal concept** —
  not a recapitulation, the same seal carried forward one chapter.

And the verse names the locusts' king outright:

> "And they had a king over them, which is the angel of the bottomless pit, whose name in
> the Hebrew tongue is Abaddon, but in the Greek tongue hath his name Apollyon."
> (Revelation 9:11)

- [KEY🔑] the verse **defines its own symbol:** the king is **Abaddon / Apollyon — the
  destroyer.** No overlay needed; the text supplies the name and the meaning.
- [⟲] careful with the **bottomless pit:** here in Rev 9 it is the Arabian desert / source
  of Islam (the Greek _abussos_). The **same word** is **RECAPITULATED in Rev 20:1-3** —
  but there it is **Satan's binding** in the millennium. **SAME word, DIFFERENT referents.**
  Flag it so the reader never blurs Rev 9 with Rev 20.

## [KEY🔑] / [→] The five months = 150 years — the year-day clock starts (Rev 9:5,10)

> "And to them it was given that they should not kill them, but that they should be
> tormented five months... And they had tails like unto scorpions... and their power was to
> hurt men five months." (Revelation 9:5,10)

- the locusts' power was **to hurt** five months — to torment by depredation, **not**
  politically to kill.
- [→] **five months × 30 days = 150 prophetic days = 150 literal years** by the year-day
  principle (Num 14:34; Eze 4:6) — and DAR fixes the exact start-date:

> "'Five months,' thirty days to a month, give us one hundred and fifty days; and these
> days, being symbolic, signify one hundred and fifty years. Commencing July 27, 1299, the
> one hundred and fifty years reach to 1449."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 479_

- [→] Haskell calls these exact dates **"nails in a sure place"** — the day given lets the
  student reckon the close to the day, July 27, 1449:

> "Since the exact day for the beginning of this power is given, the expiration of the five
> months may be reckoned to the day. It closed July 27, 1449... These dates are 'nails in a
> sure place' for both the first and the second woe."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 172_

- [⟲] the **year-day principle re-met** — the **SAME interpretive rule** the reader carried
  from **Dan 7:25** (1260) and **Dan 8:14** (2300). Here it is applied to a **short** span
  with an **exact start-date**, proving the rule's precision.

## The sixth trumpet / second woe — the Ottoman Turks (Rev 9:13-21)

> "And the sixth angel sounded... saying to the sixth angel which had the trumpet, Loose
> the four angels which are bound in the great river Euphrates. And the four angels were
> loosed..." (Revelation 9:14-15)

- the four angels bound at the **great river Euphrates**, loosed = the **four Ottoman
  sultanies** for the sixth-trumpet period.
- [⟲] the Euphrates **here** = the four Ottoman sultanies bordering the Euphrates region.
  This is a **DIFFERENT instance** from **Rev 16:12** — _"the great river Euphrates... dried
  up, that the way of the kings of the east might be prepared."_ Two different uses
  (sixth-trumpet vs end-time). **Do NOT blur them.**
- [→] the weapons in the text decoded:

> "And the heads of the horses were as the heads of lions; and out of their mouths issued
> fire and smoke and brimstone... For their power is in their mouth, and in their tails."
> (Revelation 9:17,19)

- [→] **fire, smoke, brimstone out of the horses' mouths** = Ottoman **gunpowder /
  firearms / cannon** (above all the 1453 siege of Constantinople); the horse's **tail** =
  the Turkish standard/ensign — DAR's historicist overlay on Rev 9:17-19.
- [→] and the prophetic clock starts again:

> "A prophetic year is three hundred and sixty prophetic days, or three hundred and sixty
> literal years; a prophetic month, thirty prophetic days, is thirty literal years; one
> prophetic day is one literal year; and an hour, or the twenty-fourth part of a prophetic
> day, would be a twenty-fourth part of a literal year, or fifteen days; the whole
> amounting to three hundred and ninety-one years and fifteen days."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 481_

- [→] Haskell confirms the same span from the same start:

> "The power which came on the stage of action July 27, 1449, was to bear sway for an hour
> and a day and a month and a year,-three hundred ninety-one years and fifteen days,
> literally speaking."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 176_

## [→] The Eastern Question — August 11, 1840, and the year-day vindicated

The two spans now **chain end-to-end:** the first woe's 150 years closed July 27, 1449 —
and the second woe's 391 years 15 days **commence from that very date:**

> "Commencing when the one hundred and fifty years ended, July 27, 1449, the period would
> end Aug. 11, 1840."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 485_

- [→] and the **manner** of the fulfillment was foretold too: as Ottoman supremacy **began**
  by the Greek emperor's voluntary acknowledgment of the sultan, so it would **end** by the
  sultan voluntarily surrendering his independence into the hands of the Christian powers
  (DAR 485).
- [→] Haskell records that this was **predicted in advance** — Litch and Miller named the
  date two years before:

> "In 1838 Josiah Litch and William Miller, after a careful study of the prophecies, came
> to the conclusion that on this last date nations might expect to see the Turkish sultan
> surrender his power."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 177_

- [→] and on that day the treaty/ultimatum to Mehemet Ali went officially into force —
  Turkey became "the Sick Man of the East," tied to Dan 11:45:

> "This treaty was signed, and the ultimatum was officially put in the power of Mehemet Ali
> on August 11, 1840. Since that time Turkey has been known everywhere as the 'Sick Man of
> the East.'"
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of the Seer of Patmos, p. 178_

[⟲] and the prophetic eye **confirms the historicist method by this very fulfillment** —
EGW records Litch's 1838 exposition and its exact-to-the-day vindication:

> "In the year 1840 another remarkable fulfillment of prophecy excited widespread
> interest. Two years before, Josiah Litch... published an exposition of Revelation 9,
> predicting the fall of the Ottoman Empire... 'in A.D. 1840, sometime in the month of
> August.'"
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 334_

> "At the very time specified, Turkey, through her ambassadors, accepted the protection of
> the allied powers of Europe, and thus placed herself under the control of Christian
> nations. The event exactly fulfilled the prediction... a wonderful impetus was given to
> the advent movement."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 335_

- [DYK🔎] EGW underlines what made it unassailable — it rested on nothing but the text:

> "...which was purely a matter of calculation on the prophetic periods of Scripture..."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy (1888), p. 334_

- [→] this is the year-day's **public vindication** — a prophecy that named not only the
  **year** but the **day**, fulfilled **to the day**, which launched the Advent movement of
  1840-1844.

## The lesson of the woes — a scourge on apostate Christendom

[→] DAR draws the moral of the two woes together — the Saracens and the Turks were God's
rod, but the rod taught nothing:

> "The hordes of Saracens and Turks were let loose as a scourge and punishment upon
> apostate Christendom. Men suffered the punishment, but learned therefrom no lesson."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 487_

- [→] this ties trumpets 5-6 back to the session's thesis: the trumpets are **warning
  judgments** on the empire / apostate church — exactly as the first four were God's lesson
  to Rome (SSP 148).
- [→] and it sets up the **seventh trumpet** (the third woe), carried forward — it sounds in
  Rev 11, and (like the seventh church and seventh seal) **carries the history into
  eternity** (SSP 145).
