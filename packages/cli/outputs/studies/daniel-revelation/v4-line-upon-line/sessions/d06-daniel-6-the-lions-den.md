# Daniel 6 — The Lions' Den

**This is the last chapter of sacred history before prophecy opens — and Heaven sealed it
as the permanent charter for God's servant under a corrupt state: a faith that will not
omit a known duty, sealed in a den that prefigures the sealed tomb of Christ.**

> The rule of the road: this chapter is **narrative, not symbol**. No verse here defines
> its own symbol — so there is **no [KEY🔑] anywhere in D06**. Every interpretive move is
> a [→] historicist overlay carrying its establishing guide quote, or a [⟲] weave that
> resolves same-vs-different. We let the guides carry the meaning, line upon line.

---

## The excellent spirit that left only one charge to bring

Daniel is now an old man, a captive who has outlived the empire that took him. Under a new
crown, he rises again to the top — and his enemies cannot find a single flaw to use.

> "It pleased Darius to set over the kingdom an hundred and twenty princes... and over
> these three presidents; of whom Daniel was first... Then this Daniel was preferred above
> the presidents and princes, because an excellent spirit was in him; and the king thought
> to set him over the whole realm. Then the presidents and princes sought to find occasion
> against Daniel concerning the kingdom; but they could find none occasion nor fault...
> Then said these men, We shall not find any occasion against this Daniel, except we find
> it against him concerning the law of his God." (Daniel 6:1-5)

- **one charge, and only one** — the conspirators comb his whole public record and come up
  empty. The only "fault" they can manufacture is his loyalty to God.
- [→] OVERLAY — this is **the model record for the man of God in a corrupt government.**
  The chapter is left on file for every age:

> "The history of England, the continental countries, and the United States today repeat
> the same story... The sixth chapter of Daniel is left on record to show how a man of God,
> when elevated to such a position, can remain uncontaminated... it shows what treatment a
> man who is true to principle must expect to receive from the hands of those who are
> corrupt."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 81_

[→] Haskell's overlay sets the frame: Daniel 6 is a **guide for every age** — Babylon's
story repeated under each later power. DAR reads the same record off the text:

> "Preference was given to Daniel because of his excellent spirit... Daniel's conduct was
> perfect so far as related to the kingdom. He was faithful and true... they could find no
> occasion to accuse him, except as concerning the law of his God. So let it be with us. A
> person can have no better recommendation."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 107_

And the prophetic eye holds Daniel up as the pattern for the converted man of business:

> "Daniel, the prime minister of the greatest of earthly kingdoms, was at the same time a
> prophet of God... His business transactions, when subjected to the closest scrutiny of
> his enemies, were found to be without one flaw. He was an example of what every
> businessman may become when his heart is converted and consecrated, and when his motives
> are right in the sight of God."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 546_

[DYK🔎] Daniel was **prime minister and prophet at once** — proof that high office and a
clean conscience are not enemies. The example, EGW says, of what every converted
businessman may become.

## The flattering decree and the unalterable law

The plot is set. The conspirators cannot trap Daniel in the kingdom — so they will trap him
in his religion, using the one law no king could later repeal.

> "Then these presidents and princes assembled together to the king, and said thus unto
> him, King Darius, live for ever. All the presidents of the kingdom... have consulted
> together to establish a royal statute... that whosoever shall ask a petition of any God
> or man for thirty days, save of thee, O king, he shall be cast into the den of lions...
> sign the writing, that it be not changed, according to the law of the Medes and Persians,
> which altereth not." (Daniel 6:6-9)

- they come **"together"** — tumultuously, in a rush — and claim **all** are agreed. A lie:
  Daniel, first of the three presidents, was never consulted.
- the bait is **flattery** — for thirty days the king alone grants every petition. The trap
  is sprung on Daniel's known habit of prayer.

> "The decree they fixed upon was one which would flatter the king's vanity... the king,
> not fathoming their evil designs, signed the decree, and it took its place on the
> statute-book as one of the unalterable laws of the Medes and Persians."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 108_

### [⟲] The weave — the law of the Medes and Persians

When a thing recurs, the weave must say plainly: **same instance, or different?**

- [⟲] WEAVE — the "unalterable law of the Medes and Persians" here is the **SAME legal
  institution** seen when Babylon fell (Dan 5:28) and twice in Esther (the irrevocable
  decree of Esther 1, the counter-decree of Esther 8). Within the canon it is **one
  institution throughout** — **NO recapitulation, NO eschatological re-application.**
- [→] do **not** import Rev 13's mark or Dan 7:25's "change times and laws" here. The
  sources do not authorize it — this is a single Medo-Persian statute, not a type of the
  end-time law-crisis.

[DYK🔎] What the guides **do** support: a law engineered by flattery to trap the faithful.
The menace is the malice of men **weaponizing an irrevocable statute** — not, per these
sources, a prophetic type. (The end-time "change times and laws" theme is real — but it
belongs to Daniel 7, where the verse itself names it. We meet it next session.)

## Windows open toward Jerusalem — the duty he would not omit

Daniel knows the decree is signed. He does exactly what he has always done — no more, no
less. The trap depends on his prayer; he prays anyway.

> "Now when Daniel knew that the writing was signed, he went into his house; and his windows
> being open in his chamber toward Jerusalem, he kneeled upon his knees three times a day,
> and prayed, and gave thanks before his God, as he did aforetime. Then these men assembled,
> and found Daniel praying and making supplication before his God." (Daniel 6:10-11)

- **"as he did aforetime"** — no change. No concealment, no flight, no secret devotions.
- DAR marks the discipline exactly:

> "Daniel... foresaw the conspiracy... but took no means to thwart it. He simply committed
> himself to God... he did not leave the empire on pretended business, or perform his
> devotions with more than ordinary secrecy."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 108_

The prophetic eye gives the reason for the open window:

> "Why should he cease to pray now, when he most needed to pray?... He did not try to
> conceal his act... neither the king nor his decree could make him swerve from allegiance
> to the King of kings."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 540_

And Haskell strikes the same note — a man at peace with all lawful authority, immovable on
the one thing:

> "kings and decrees could not make him swerve from his allegiance to the King of kings."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 83_

### [→] OVERLAY — the two great lines of duty

Here is the chapter's central charge, and DAR states it as a divine seal stamped on duty
itself:

> "In this case, and in the case of the three Hebrews in the fiery furnace, the seal of God
> is set in favor of two great lines of duty: (1) As in the case of the three in the fiery
> furnace, not to yield to any known sin; and (2) As in the present case, not to omit any
> known duty. And from these instances, the people of God in all ages are to derive
> encouragement."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 111_

- **(1) not to yield to any known sin** — the three Hebrews, the fiery furnace (Dan 3)
- **(2) not to omit any known duty** — Daniel, the den (Dan 6)
- [⟲] WEAVE — the furnace and the den are **PARALLEL but DISTINCT** instances. The **same
  divine seal** is stamped on the **complementary halves** of duty — not one event told
  twice. Do not collapse them: Dan 3 answers "shall I do wrong?"; Dan 6 answers "shall I
  omit right?"

[Q] The decree never told Daniel to sin — only to stop a good. Notice the trap: the easiest
faith to surrender is the one that asks you merely to **leave off** a duty for thirty days.

## The trapped king and the den of lions

The accusers spring the trap. The king — who loves Daniel — discovers he has signed away
his own friend, and cannot undo it.

> "Then the king, when he heard these words, was sore displeased with himself, and set his
> heart on Daniel to deliver him: and he laboured till the going down of the sun to deliver
> him... Then the king commanded, and they brought Daniel, and cast him into the den of
> lions... And a stone was brought, and laid upon the mouth of the den; and the king sealed
> it with his own signet, and with the signet of his lords... Then the king went to his
> palace, and passed the night fasting... and his sleep went from him." (Daniel 6:14-18)

- the king is **bound by his own law** — the very thing the conspirators counted on. The
  irrevocable statute now traps the king, not just the prophet.
- mark the detail: a **stone laid** on the mouth of the den, **sealed with the signet** of
  king and lords. Hold that picture for the section ahead.

EGW gives the reason God allowed the den at all:

> "God did not prevent Daniel's enemies from casting him into the lions' den; He permitted
> evil angels and wicked men thus far to accomplish their purpose; but it was that He might
> make the deliverance of His servant more marked... Through the courage of this one man...
> Satan was to be defeated."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 543_

[→] God did not spare the trial; He used it. The deeper the den, the more **marked** the
deliverance — the very strategy by which Satan is defeated.

> "Daniel in the lions' den was the same Daniel who stood before the king as chief among
> the ministers of state... A man whose heart is stayed upon God will be the same in the
> hour of his greatest trial as he is in prosperity. Faith reaches to the unseen, and
> grasps eternal realities."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 545_

[DYK🔎] **Same Daniel in the den as in the palace.** The man does not change with his
circumstances, because his anchor was never his circumstances.

## The sealed den and the sealed tomb

At dawn the king runs to the den. Daniel answers, alive — and Haskell reads the whole scene
as a **type of the sealed sepulcher of Christ.**

> "Then the king arose very early in the morning, and went in haste unto the den of
> lions... Then said Daniel unto the king, O king, live for ever. My God hath sent his
> angel, and hath shut the lions' mouths, that they have not hurt me: forasmuch as before
> him innocency was found in me... So Daniel was taken up out of the den, and no manner of
> hurt was found upon him, because he believed in his God." (Daniel 6:19-23)

> "Satan exulted as he did years later when he saw the Son of God in the sepulcher, with a
> stone before the door, and the stone sealed with the Roman seal. But there was no more
> power to hold Daniel in the den of lions than to keep Christ in the grave. The angel came,
> not to the stone, but into the den."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 85_

> "'He is not here, but risen.'... So no manner of hurt was found upon Daniel, the
> representative of Christ, because he believed in his God; because innocency was found in
> him."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 86_

### [⟲] The weave — type, not identity

- [⟲] WEAVE — this is **type-antitype, NOT identity.** The **SAME structural pattern**
  (stone laid → sealed with a king's signet → angel → emergence), in **DIFFERENT historical
  instances.** **Daniel is the type; Christ the antitype.**
- mark Haskell's hinge: the angel came **"not to the stone, but into the den"** — that maps
  to the **resurrection**, not merely a rolled-away stone. The deliverance is from within.
- [→] do not collapse the two into one event. Two sealed stones, two angels, two
  emergences — one pattern, the lesser pointing to the greater.

The prophetic eye confirms the **representative** reading — Daniel stands for Christ's
suffering people:

> "Heaven is very near those who suffer for righteousness' sake. Christ identifies His
> interests with the interests of His faithful people; He suffers in the person of His
> saints."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 545_

## Doubly vindicated — faith did it

The deliverance might be written off as luck — perhaps the lions simply were not hungry.
The king destroys that excuse with a test.

> "And the king commanded, and they brought those men which had accused Daniel, and they
> cast them into the den of lions, them, their children, and their wives; and the lions had
> the mastery of them, and brake all their bones in pieces or ever they came at the bottom
> of the den." (Daniel 6:24)

> "Here, then, stood Daniel, preserved by a power higher than any power of earth... No hurt
> was found on him, because he believed in his God. Faith did it... the lions were hungry
> enough when they could get hold of the guilty... Thus was Daniel doubly vindicated."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 110_

- the accusers' theory was **chance** — hungerless lions. The test exposes it: the lions
  "had the mastery" of the guilty before they hit the bottom.
- [→] so the deliverance was **not luck — it was miracle.** Daniel is **doubly vindicated**:
  saved alive, and the chance-theory destroyed. It fulfills the proverb —
  > "The righteous is delivered out of trouble, and the wicked cometh in his stead."
  > (Proverbs 11:8)

## The heathen king's proclamation of the living God

The trap meant to silence Daniel's prayer instead publishes Daniel's God to the whole
empire. Darius writes a decree of his own.

> "Then king Darius wrote unto all people, nations, and languages, that dwell in all the
> earth... I make a decree, That in every dominion of my kingdom men tremble and fear before
> the God of Daniel: for he is the living God, and stedfast for ever, and his kingdom that
> which shall not be destroyed, and his dominion shall be even unto the end. He delivereth
> and rescueth, and he worketh signs and wonders in heaven and in earth, who hath delivered
> Daniel from the power of the lions." (Daniel 6:25-27)

DAR unpacks the nine-fold portrait of the true God hidden in the heathen king's decree:

> "How excellent an eulogium is this on the great God and his faithful servant!"
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 111_

- **(1)** the living God — **(2)** stedfast forever — **(3)** has a kingdom — **(4)** a
  kingdom that shall not be destroyed — **(5)** dominion without end — **(6)** delivereth the
  bound — **(7)** rescueth His servants — **(8)** worketh wonders in heaven and signs on
  earth — **(9)** hath delivered Daniel.

The prophetic eye marks the pattern — yet another pagan throne forced to confess the God of
Israel:

> "Once more a proclamation was issued by a heathen ruler, exalting the God of Daniel as the
> true God."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 544_

And Haskell draws the fruit: one man's integrity became a worldwide pulpit.

> "his integrity was the means of publishing the truth throughout the world. From this time
> Daniel prospered-during the reign of Darius, and in the reign of Cyrus, who issued the
> wonderful decree for the deliverance of the Jews."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 87_

### [⟲] The weave — the recurring royal confession

- [⟲] WEAVE — Nebuchadnezzar's proclamation after the fiery furnace (Dan 3:29), his
  proclamation after his restoration (Dan 4:1-3), and Darius's here (Dan 6:25-27) are
  **DISTINCT historical instances** sharing **one structural form** — a pagan monarch,
  forced by a miracle, confessing the God of Israel **to all peoples, nations, and
  languages.**
- [→] **RECAPITULATED pattern, not one event.** The recurring phrase exposes the form: God
  turns each crisis into a worldwide testimony. DAR 111 pairs the den and the furnace as
  parallel seals of divine faithfulness.

## The hinge of history — the den, Cyrus, and the close of the story

One verse carries the whole book over a threshold. Daniel outlasts another throne — and the
miracle of the den becomes the providential hinge to the return from captivity.

> "So this Daniel prospered in the reign of Darius, and in the reign of Cyrus the Persian."
> (Daniel 6:28)

> "The deliverance of Daniel from the den of lions had been used of God to create a
> favorable impression upon the mind of Cyrus the Great... He moved upon Cyrus as His
> agent... to grant the Jewish people their liberty."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 557_

[→] OVERLAY — the den is the **hinge** that connects captivity to restoration. The same
miracle that vindicated one man moved the monarch who would free a nation.

And here the **book itself turns.** DAR marks the structural pivot:

> "Thus closes the historical part of the book of Daniel. We now come to the prophetic
> portion, which, like a shining beacon light, has thrown its rays over all the course of
> time... lighting up the pathway of the church onward to the eternal kingdom."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 112_

[DYK🔎] D06 is the **last chapter of sacred history before prophecy opens.** Chapters 1–6
were narrative; from chapter 7 forward, Daniel turns prophet. The den is the doorway.

Haskell makes Daniel the representative of every persecuted saint to the end:

> "what was done for him will be done for all whom Heaven favors to-day. Though death claim
> the body, the promise of God is a speedy resurrection."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 87_

## Appeal

- **Be the same person in trial as in favor.** Daniel in the den was the same Daniel who
  stood before the king (PK 545). The call is simple: open your window, kneel, and **omit no
  known duty** though the law be signed against you.
- The decree never asked Daniel to do wrong — only to leave off a good for thirty days.
  Watch for that trap. _"Why should he cease to pray now, when he most needed to pray?"_
- **Heaven is very near those who suffer for righteousness' sake** (PK 545). Whoever touches
  His chosen ones touches Him — _"He suffers in the person of His saints."_
- For the oppressed: Daniel represents **all** who suffer for the Word. Whether in death,
  prison, or the den, Satan has no power over Christ — _"I am come that they might have
  life."_ Christ never forgets His own:
  > "Will the Lord forget His people in this trying hour?... Did He forget the three worthies
  > in the fiery furnace? or Daniel in the den of lions?"
  > — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 626_

[CHAIN] — **Link forged: the two seals of duty — not to yield to a known sin (the furnace),
not to omit a known duty (the den).** These twin seals, stamped across the four narrative
trials, are the **character credentials** of the people who will stand when prophecy
unrolls. This session closes the **historical half** of Daniel (DAR 112). Hold the
credentials; the prophecy will test them.
