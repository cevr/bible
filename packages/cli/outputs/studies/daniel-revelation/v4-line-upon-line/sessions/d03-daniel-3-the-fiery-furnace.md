# Daniel 3 — The Fiery Furnace

**Daniel 2 said Babylon would fall. Daniel 3 is the king's answer: an all-gold image,
a worship-or-die decree, and a furnace for any who refuse. But three Hebrews say "but
if not" — and a fourth figure walks the fire with them. Dura is the first
dress-rehearsal of the last great test.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Daniel 3 names its
> own key — the fourth in the fire — and hands the worship-or-death pattern forward to
> Revelation 13, line upon line.

---

## The all-gold image on the plain of Dura (Dan 3:1)

> "Nebuchadnezzar the king made an image of gold, whose height was threescore cubits,
> and the breadth thereof six cubits: he set it up in the plain of Dura, in the province
> of Babylon." (Daniel 3:1)

- gold image, **sixty cubits high, six broad** — note the disproportion. **60×6** is a
  man-made counterfeit; God's revelation comes in symmetry, this comes lopsided
- [→] this image is a **deliberate answer to the Daniel 2 statue.** There the head was
  gold and the rest silver, brass, iron — Babylon would give way. Here the king makes it
  **all** gold:

> "...instead of having simply the head of his image of gold, he made it all of gold...
> that his kingdom should not give way to another kingdom, but be perpetual."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 78_

- [→] this is Nebuchadnezzar **repudiating the prophecy.** Daniel 2 promised Babylon
  would fall to a successor; the all-gold image is the king's "no."

[DYK🔎] The wise men handed him the idea — they took God's own words and twisted them
to flatter the throne:

> "...set it up where all might behold the head of gold, which had been interpreted as
> representing his kingdom."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 504_

- [→] the first symbol of Daniel 3 is **corrupted prophecy** — truth misapplied. Watch
  how the prophetic eye names the danger:

> "...truth unmixed with error is a power mighty to save; but... when used to exalt
> self... it becomes a power for evil."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 505_

[→] Teaching point: the weave to the final crisis begins right here. **God's own symbol
turned into an instrument of forced worship is the pattern, not the exception.** Hold
that — Revelation will use the same move.

## Worship or the fiery furnace — counterfeit worship enforced (Dan 3:4-6)

> "Then an herald cried aloud, To you it is commanded, O people, nations, and languages,
> That at what time ye hear the sound of the cornet, flute, harp, sackbut, psaltery,
> dulcimer, and all kinds of musick, ye fall down and worship the golden image... And
> whoso falleth not down and worshippeth shall the same hour be cast into the midst of a
> burning fiery furnace." (Daniel 3:4-6)

- every coercive system runs on **two levers** — a carrot and a club:

> "...pleasure on the one hand, pain on the other."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 79_

- music to **seduce**, the furnace to **terrify**. Both aimed at one thing: bend the
  knee.
- [→] now Haskell names the principle. Babylon is not just one ancient city — it is a
  **pattern of government** that recurs:

> "All tyranny is a repetition of the Babylonian principles. We sometimes call it papal;
> it is likewise Babylonian."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 41_

- [→] and the doctrinal spine — **what makes enforced worship idolatry, no matter how
  "true" the object:**

> "When the civil power enforces worship of any sort, be that worship true or false in
> itself, to obey is idolatry... The command must be backed by some form of punishment,
> — a fiery furnace."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 41_

[DYK🔎] Babylon is bigger than Babylon:

> "...a symbol of earthly governments of all time."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 40_

- [→] underneath the politics runs the **great controversy** — the oldest rebellion,
  replayed:

> "...in the person of the Babylonian king, Satan is challenging the government of God...
> The motive power in the heavenly government is love; human power when exercised becomes
> tyranny."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 41_

- Lucifer would not bow to God; the king will not let men refuse to bow to him. Same
  spirit, new throne.
- [→] and the answer of Heaven — the doctrine of **religious liberty**, stated against
  Dura:

> "He had no more right, either civil or moral, to threaten men with death for not
> worshiping God... God never compels the obedience of man. He leaves all free to choose
> whom they will serve."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 510_

[→] God could have forced every knee from the start — and never has. The furnace is the
counterfeit of worship precisely because **real worship is free.**

## "But if not" — the limits of civil obedience (Dan 3:13-18)

Daniel is absent from the scene; the three Hebrews are accused. The king offers one
more chance — bow, or burn. Their answer draws the line of conscience exactly:

> "...we are not careful to answer thee in this matter. If it be so, our God whom we
> serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace, and he will deliver us out
> of thine hand, O king. But if not, be it known unto thee, O king, that we will not
> serve thy gods, nor worship the golden image which thou hast set up." (Daniel 3:16-18)

- [→] watch the **precise** line they drew — they obeyed the state right up to the edge,
  and not one inch past it:

> "With this requirement they could comply, and they did... This their religion forbade,
> and this they therefore refused to do."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 80_

- they **came** to the plain (the command to be present, they kept). They would **not
  worship** (the command to bow, they refused). The faithful obey civil authority up to,
  and never past, the point of compromised worship.
- [→] and the answer was **unconditional** — deliverance or no deliverance, the verdict
  was the same:

> "Our God can deliver if he will; but if not, it is just the same... to that we shall
> render unconditional obedience. Their answer was both honest and decisive."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 81_

[DYK🔎] Where did three young men get that nerve? From **history** — from remembering
how God deals with His people:

> "From the history of their fathers they had learned that disobedience to God results
> in dishonor, disaster, and death."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 507_

- they stood "with triumphant assurance born of implicit trust in God." Their courage
  was **historicist memory** — the same backward-looking faith this whole series is
  built on.
- [→] and the stakes were cosmic — the whole universe was the courtroom:

> "The whole universe was watching... The controversy was not between man and Satan, but
> between Satan and Christ, and eternal principles were at stake."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 42_

[Q] Haskell's question still cuts: **"What could a religion be worth which admitted of
compromise?"** A faith that bows when the music plays was never faith at all.

## [KEY🔑] The furnace and the fourth figure (Dan 3:19-25, 28)

The king heats the furnace **sevenfold**; the men who carry the Hebrews up die in the
flame. Then the king looks in:

> "...Lo, I see four men loose, walking in the midst of the fire, and they have no hurt;
> and the form of the fourth is like the Son of God." (Daniel 3:25)

Who is the fourth? **The Bible does not leave us to guess — the verse defines its own
symbol.** The king's own confession names him:

> "Blessed be the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego, who hath sent his **angel**,
> and delivered his servants that trusted in him..." (Daniel 3:28)

- [KEY🔑] **the verse's word is "angel."** Daniel 3:28 is the single verse-defined key of
  this session — Scripture handing us the dictionary, exactly as Dan 2:38 said "head of
  gold" and Dan 7:17 said "beasts... are... kings." We read the label off the page.
- [→] the king overreached, and his cruelty only **magnified** the miracle:

> "...the more intense the heat, the greater and more impressive the miracle of being
> delivered from it."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 82_

- [→] note: they did not **spring out** of the fire. With the fourth beside them, the
  furnace was as good as the palace:

> "...the form of the fourth was with them, and in his presence they could be content
> and joyful, as well in the furnace of fire as in the delights... of the palace. Let us
> in all our trials... but have the form of the fourth with us, and it is enough."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 82_

[DYK🔎] Keep the verse's claim and the king's pagan formula **distinct** — DAR is
careful here, so we cite it verbatim:

> "This language is by some supposed to refer to Christ; but it is not likely that the
> king had any idea of the Saviour. A better rendering... would be like a son of the
> gods... Nebuchadnezzar subsequently called him an angel."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 83_

- [→] the king, a pagan, said "like a son of the gods" out of his own framework — but
  when he settled into worship he called him **an angel** (v.28). That is the verse's own
  word, and that is the [KEY🔑].

[→] **Confirming-witness overlay (NOT a second KEY):** the prophetic eye and Haskell
identify that angel **as Christ in person** — in full harmony with Scripture, but as the
established teaching overlay, not a bare verse-key:

> "...the Saviour revealed Himself to them in person, and together they walked in the
> midst of the fire. In the presence of the Lord of heat and cold, the flames lost their
> power to consume."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 508_

> "...the king had heard from their lips descriptions of the glorious Being whom they
> served."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 44_

[→] Flag it clearly: **the verse defines "angel"; the Christ-identification is the
EGW/Haskell overlay.** One is the text's own word ([KEY🔑]); the other is the harmonized
teaching that fills it in ([→]). We never collapse the two.

## Vindication, and the gospel to the ends of the earth (Dan 3:26-30)

> "Then Nebuchadnezzar... spake, and said, Blessed be the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and
> Abednego... Therefore I make a decree, That every people, nation, and language, which
> speak any thing amiss against the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego, shall be cut
> in pieces... Then the king promoted Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego, in the province
> of Babylon." (Daniel 3:28-30)

- the same power that decreed the furnace now **blesses the God of the Hebrews** —
  Heaven turned the table:

> "A higher power than any on earth had vindicated those who stood firm against
> idolatry... None of the gods of the heathen ever had wrought such deliverance as
> that."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 83_

- [→] their **steadfastness** is what exalted God's name. Compromise would have buried
  it:

> "Had these Jews been time-servers, the name of the true God had not thus been exalted
> in Babylon. What honor does the Lord put upon them that are steadfast toward him!"
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 84_

[DYK🔎] How did a **pagan king** read the miracle and recognize the Son of God? Because
the Hebrews had been faithful **witnesses long before the crisis came:**

> "The king had heard from their lips descriptions of the glorious Being whom they
> served."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 44_

> "They had told of Christ, the Redeemer to come; and in the form of the fourth in the
> midst of the fire the king recognized the Son of God."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 509_

- [→] peacetime faithfulness equipped the king to read the furnace. **Witness now is
  what makes the crisis legible later.**
- [→] and the deliverance went **global** — what a whole nation failed to do, God did
  through three men:

> "What the Jewish nation as a nation had failed to do... God accomplished... with only
> three men. The story of the miraculous deliverance was told to the ends of the earth...
> The Sabbath was proclaimed."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 45_

> "...the principles of God's government — true Protestantism, as it would be called
> to-day, — were proclaimed before the nations of the earth."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 46_

[DYK🔎] Haskell notes the long reach of Dura: these "forces... paved the way for the
return of the Jews a few years later" (SDP 45) — one stand in one furnace bent the course
of nations.

## [⟲] Dura as a miniature of the image-of-the-beast crisis (Rev 13)

Now the weave — and the weave is where the interpretive risk concentrates. **Resolve
same-vs-different before we draw a single line.**

- [⟲] the **golden image of Dan 3** and the **"image to the beast" of Rev 13:14-15** are
  **DIFFERENT instances**, separated by millennia. Dura is a **literal historical idol**
  enforcing submission to Babylon; the Rev 13 image is an **end-time civil-religious
  power** (the two-horned beast, the United States) enforcing worship in honor of the
  papacy. They are **not the same image.** What links them is a **typological pattern,
  not a symbol equation** — so this is the overlay connector [→], **never a [KEY🔑].**

> "And he had power to give life unto the image of the beast, that the image of the beast
> should both speak, and cause that as many as would not worship the image of the beast
> should be killed." (Revelation 13:15)

- [→] same **structure**, different entity — civil power + forced worship + death penalty
  - economic exclusion:

> "The principles are the same in both. All the world was called to worship the image set
> up in the province of Babylon; refusing, they would suffer death. In Revelation there
> is brought to view an image to the beast... no one will be allowed to buy or sell who
> has not the mark or the name of the beast."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 48_

- [→] Dura is the rehearsal; Rev 13 is the performance:

> "This will be the final test brought upon the servants of God... From what homes will
> come the Daniels and the Meshachs?"
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 49_

- [⟲] and the **Babylon** of Dan 3 vs the **Babylon** of Rev 17-18 — **RECAPITULATED
  archetype, not identity.** Literal Babylon is the **first** instance of the
  civil-religious coercion pattern (SDP 40, "a symbol of earthly governments of all
  time"); Rev 17-18's "Babylon" replays the **same pattern** in the papal-political
  system of the last days. **Same pattern, different historical entity** — never blur the
  two. (Same discipline as Dan 2: the feet's ten horns are a _different_ ten from the
  toes' Rev 17 ten.)

[DYK🔎] Three verdicts, kept straight:

- Dan 3 image vs Rev 13 image = **DIFFERENT** (typological pattern, not equation)
- the fourth figure (Dan 3:25/28) = **SAME** (the verse self-defines: "angel")
- Babylon (Dan 3 vs Rev 17-18) = **RECAPITULATED** (same archetype, new entity)

[→] and the antitype names its target — the prophetic eye says the final decree falls
especially on Sabbath-keepers:

> "...a universal decree will denounce these as deserving of death..."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 512_

- the decree of Rev 13 is aimed at "those who hallow the Sabbath of the fourth
  commandment." Dura's question — _whom will you worship?_ — comes back as the **mark of
  the beast.**
- and so does the promise: the **fourth figure who walked the furnace will walk it
  again:**

> "He who walked with the Hebrew worthies in the fiery furnace will be with His followers
> wherever they are... Jehovah will reveal Himself as a God of gods."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 513_

[Q] If the same Christ who made the furnace bearable stands ready for the final test —
the only question left is whether you will already be **decided** when the music plays.

## Appeal

- A man-made image, sixty by six, demanded worship on pain of death — and three young
  men answered, _"but if not."_ They had settled the question of allegiance **before**
  the music sounded. The furnace found them **already decided.**
- The God who walked the fire then is the same who walks it now — _"the form of the
  fourth is like the Son of God"_ (Dan 3:25). He does not always spare the furnace; He
  promises to be **in** it. _"When thou walkest through the fire, thou shalt not be
  burned"_ (Isa 43:2).
- The final test is the same test: _whom will you worship?_ Decide it now, in the calm,
  while the choice is still quiet — so that when the decree comes, the answer is already
  yours. _"But if not... we will not serve thy gods"_ (Dan 3:18).

[CHAIN] — **Link forged: civil power + forced worship + death penalty = the Dura
pattern, and the fourth in the fire is Christ Himself.** Dura is the miniature; Revelation
13's image and mark are the full-scale repeat — same principle, different entity. The
Christ who delivered at Dura is the Christ who delivers the remnant. Hold it; the
Revelation arc will need it.
