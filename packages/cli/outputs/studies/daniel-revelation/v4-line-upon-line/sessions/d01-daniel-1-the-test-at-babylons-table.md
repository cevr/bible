# Daniel 1 — The Test at Babylon's Table

**Before Daniel ever reads a single prophecy, God writes the first one ON him — a captive
boy at a pagan king's table proves that loyalty to God in the smallest thing is the key
that unlocks everything ahead. Daniel 1 is not a symbol to decode; it is the method to
learn: faithfulness first, and God gives the rest.**

> The rule of the road: this chapter holds no symbol to define — it sets the posture every
> key ahead will require. Watch a captive boy settle his loyalty before the test comes, and
> watch God reward it. Line upon line, faithfulness comes first.

---

## The setup — Jerusalem falls and the vessels go to Babylon's temple

The book opens not in vision but in plain history — testable fact first, prophecy from
chapter 7:

> "WITH a directness characteristic of the sacred writers, Daniel enters at once upon his
> subject. He commences in the simple, historical style... till we reach the seventh
> chapter, when the prophetical portion, more properly so called, commences."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 24_

> "In the third year of the reign of Jehoiakim king of Judah came Nebuchadnezzar king of
> Babylon unto Jerusalem, and besieged it. And the Lord gave Jehoiakim king of Judah into
> his hand, with part of the vessels of the house of God: which he carried into the land of
> Shinar to the house of his god." (Daniel 1:1-2)

- the frame, filled in: **607 B.C.**, Jehoiakim left on his throne, the temple vessels and
  the royal hostages carried off to Babylon as tribute
  > "Daniel begins the book with the simple statement that in the third year of the reign of
  > Jehoiakim, king of Judah, 607 B. C., Nebuchadnezzar, the king of Babylon, came unto
  > Jerusalem and besieged it; that in the siege, Jehoiakim was given by the Lord into the
  > hands of Nebuchadnezzar, but allowed to remain on the throne in Jerusalem, yet
  > Nebuchadnezzar carried with him to Babylon, as tribute, a part of the vessels of the
  > house of God, and, as hostages, some of the members of the royal household."
  > — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 14_

[→] **WHY the captivity happened** — not Babylon's strength, but Judah's apostasy. The
temple emptied itself before any army arrived:

> "Such was God's passing testimony against sin... Had the Israelites been faithful to God,
> and kept his Sabbath, Jerusalem would have stood forever."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 25_

[→] the vessels in the heathen temple are the **stage**, not just the wound. God lets His
own house be stripped — and turns the defeat into a witness:

> "...was boastfully cited by the victors as evidence that their religion and customs were
> superior to the religion and customs of the Hebrews. Under such circumstances, through the
> very humiliations that Israel's departure from His commandments had invited, God gave to
> Babylon evidence of His supremacy, of the holiness of His requirements, and of the sure
> result of obedience. And this testimony He gave, as alone it could be given, through those
> who still held fast their loyalty."
> — _EGW, Education, p. 54_

[Q] God lets His own house be stripped to make a point about obedience — what does that
say about how seriously He takes the small daily faithfulness Daniel is about to show?

## The names — re-baptized into the gods of Babylon

> "Now among these were of the children of Judah, Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah:
> unto whom the prince of the eunuchs gave names: for he gave unto Daniel the name of
> Belteshazzar; and to Hananiah, of Shadrach; and to Mishael, of Meshach; and to Azariah,
> of Abednego." (Daniel 1:6-7)

[DYK🔎] each **Hebrew** name carried the true God — and each was deliberately swapped for a
name tied to a god of Babylon:

> "This change of names was probably made on account of the signification of the words.
> Thus, Daniel signified, in the Hebrew, God is my judge; Hananiah, gift of the Lord;
> Mishael, he that is a strong God; and Azariah, help of the Lord. These names each having
> some reference to the true God, and signifying some connection with his worship, were
> changed to names the definition of which bore a like relation to the heathen divinities and
> worship of the Chaldeans. Thus Belteshazzar, the name given to Daniel, signified keeper of
> the hid treasures of Bel; Shadrach, inspiration of the sun (which the Chaldeans worshiped);
> Meshach, of the goddess Shaca (under which name Venus was worshiped); and Abed-nego, servant
> of the shining fire (which they also worshiped)."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 28_

- (the Hebrew is **auxiliary** — it confirms the pressure on their identity; it does not
  carry a doctrine)
- [→] the assault is on **identity** before it ever reaches the dinner table — name,
  learning, and diet, Babylon means to absorb it all. The food test, next, is the one line
  they will not let Babylon cross. The stakes for the whole chapter are set here.

---

## [→] The test at the table — "Daniel purposed in his heart"

This is the chapter's hinge. The king sets his own provision before the captives — and one
boy draws a line:

> "And the king appointed them a daily provision of the king's meat, and of the wine which
> he drank..." (Daniel 1:5)

> "But Daniel purposed in his heart that he would not defile himself with the portion of
> the king's meat, nor with the wine which he drank..." (Daniel 1:8)

**WHY it was a defilement — two grounds:**

> "But it was frequently the case that the meat used by the kings and princes of heathen
> nations, who were often the high priests of their religion, was first offered in sacrifice
> to idols, and the wine they used, poured out as a libation before them; and again, some of
> the meat of which they made use, was pronounced unclean by the Jewish law; and on either of
> these grounds Daniel could not, consistently with his religion, partake of these articles..."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 29_

[→] EGW sharpens it from a diet rule to a **faith-confession** — to eat would be to bow:

> "But a portion having been offered to idols, the food from the king's table was
> consecrated to idolatry; and one partaking of it would be regarded as offering homage to
> the gods of Babylon. In such homage, loyalty to Jehovah forbade Daniel and his companions
> to join. Even a mere pretense of eating the food or drinking the wine would be a denial of
> their faith."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 481_

[→] the heart of the man — loyalty weighed against a king's favor, and even against life:

> "But Daniel did not hesitate. The approval of God was dearer to him than the favor of the
> most powerful earthly potentate—dearer than life itself."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 483_

[→] and the decision was **prayed through, not improvised** — settled by principle, on the
knees, before the pressure ever arrived:

> "Daniel and his companions, after considering their dangerous and difficult position,
> took this matter to the Lord in prayer, and decided to be true to principle... Even at the
> cost of appearing singular, they decided not to sit at the table of the king."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 21_

[→] there is the method in miniature: idolatry, ruined character, corrupt associations, the
safeguard of temperance removed — every one of those stakes weighed and answered **before**
the test, by principle, in prayer. Settle loyalty first.

## [→] Appetite — the oldest battlefield (Eden → Christ → the last days)

This is no minor diet rule. Haskell reads Daniel's food test as the **same battlefield** as
Eden's tree and Christ's wilderness:

> "Daniel and his companions gained the victory on the point of appetite. This was the
> avenue, and the only one, through which Satan was permitted to tempt Adam."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 23_

And the antitype — where Christ entered the same fight, and where it lands on the last
generation:

> "As Christ entered upon the work of his ministry, he began where Adam fell. The first
> temptation in the wilderness was on the point of appetite. Here the Saviour bridged the
> gulf which sin had made... In the last days God will prove his people as he proved Daniel.
> A voluntary self-control of appetite lies at the foundation of every reform."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 24_

[⟲] **same conflict, recapitulated — not one literal event.** Eden's tree (Gen 3) — Christ's
wilderness (Matt 4) — Daniel's table (Dan 1) — the last-day test are the **same** controversy
over appetite and loyalty, fought again at four distinct moments in history. Read it as
recapitulation **by principle** — do not flatten four real moments into one. The Bible itself
forges this cross-reference: it names Daniel as the type of the end-time loyal.

> "Though these three men, Noah, Daniel, and Job, were in it, they should deliver but their
> own souls by their righteousness, saith the Lord GOD." (Ezekiel 14:14)

[→] the **type / object-lesson** reading — Daniel standing loyal in a heathen court is drawn
as a picture of God's last-day people under apostate world-power:

> "The character of Daniel is referred to by Ezekiel, who was a contemporaneous prophet, as
> representing those who will live just before the second coming of Christ."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 23_

- (this is the guide's **overlay**, carried by Haskell's words — not a bare key. The verse
  names Daniel; Haskell draws the type.)

[Q] If the very first thing recorded of Daniel is a victory over appetite — the gateway sin
of Eden — what does God want settled in us before He trusts us with light?

## The ten days — a trial of faith, not an experiment

> "Prove thy servants, I beseech thee, ten days; and let them give us pulse to eat, and
> water to drink. Then let our countenances be looked upon before thee, and the countenance
> of the children that eat of the portion of the king's meat: and as thou seest, deal with
> thy servants." (Daniel 1:12-13)

[→] it was not a science fair — they already knew how it would end:

> "With true courage and Christian courtesy, Daniel said... 'Prove thy servants, I beseech
> thee, ten days.'... It was no experiment with them; for they foresaw the result."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 22_

> "And at the end of ten days their countenances appeared fairer and fatter in flesh than
> all the children which did eat the portion of the king's meat. Thus Melzar took away the
> portion of their meat, and the wine that they should drink; and gave them pulse."
> (Daniel 1:15-16)

[→] the result was **God, not granola** — ten days could not do that on diet alone:

> "Their increase in flesh and improvement in countenance which took place during these ten
> days, can hardly be attributed to the natural result of the diet; for it would hardly
> produce such marked effects in so short a time. Is it not much more natural to conclude
> that this result was produced by a special interposition of the Lord, as a token of his
> approbation of the course on which they had entered...?"
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 30_

[⟲] **ten days — different.** The "ten days" of Dan 1:12-14 are **literal chronological
days** — no prophetic marker stands anywhere in the text, so the year-day principle (Num
14:34; Eze 4:6) does **not** apply. They are categorically unlike the prophetic "ten days"
of Smyrna — _"ye shall have tribulation ten days"_ (Rev 2:10) — which is symbolic. Flag it
now so the literal number is never later mis-imported into a symbolic one.

## Daniel 1:17 — God gives the gift, and one thread is planted for the road ahead

> "As for these four children, God gave them knowledge and skill in all learning and
> wisdom: and Daniel had understanding in all visions and dreams." (Daniel 1:17)

[→] where the wisdom came from — the method again: faithfulness first, then God gives:

> "Their learning did not come by chance. They obtained their knowledge by the faithful use
> of their powers, under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. They placed themselves in
> connection with the Source of all wisdom... In faith they prayed for wisdom, and they
> lived their prayers."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 486_

[⟲] **the planted thread, recapitulated.** _"Daniel had understanding in all visions and
dreams"_ is the **gift** every later vision will draw on — Dan 2, 4, 7, 8, 9, 10-12 — the
same gift, extended and deepened in each chapter, unbroken from here. DAR marks that the
historical portion runs through chapter 6 and "the prophetical portion, more properly so
called, commences" at chapter 7 — but the **gift** itself is continuous from chapter 1.

[→] **NOTE FOR THE READER (method, not a key):** this is **not** a [KEY🔑]. Dan 1:17 says
Daniel _had_ the gift; it does **not** define a symbol's meaning the way Dan 2:38 _("thou art
this head of gold")_ or Rev 12:9 _("the dragon... is the Devil")_ will. D01 is the intro that
**earns** the right to the keys — not a key itself. The first true [KEY🔑] arrives next
session, in Daniel 2.

## The final exam — ten times better, and the captivity outlasted

> "Now at the end of the days that the king had said he should bring them in, then the
> prince of the eunuchs brought them in before Nebuchadnezzar... And in all matters of
> wisdom and understanding, that the king enquired of them, he found them ten times better
> than all the magicians and astrologers that were in all his realm." (Daniel 1:18-20)

[→] the king tested them **himself**, and judged on merit alone:

> "...recognizing merit wherever he saw it, without respect to religion or nationality, he
> acknowledged them to be ten times superior to any in his own land."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 31_

Haskell and the prophetic eye confirm it in one breath:

> "...none were found equal to the Hebrew youth... They surpassed their associates
> tenfold... The vigor and strength of their mental powers were unimpaired."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 25_

> "At the court of Babylon were gathered representatives from all lands, men of the choicest
> talents, men the most richly endowed with natural gifts, and possessed of the highest
> culture this world could bestow; yet amidst them all, the Hebrew captives were without a
> peer."
> — _EGW, Education, p. 55_

[→] **cause and effect:** the boys who would not defile themselves at the table are the boys
God made ten times wiser. Faithfulness in the small thing unlocked the great thing. **That
is the law of the whole series.**

> "And Daniel continued even unto the first year of king Cyrus." (Daniel 1:21)

[→] not a stray biographical note — DAR catches the single word "unto":

> "This is an instance of the somewhat singular use of the word unto, or until, which
> occasionally occurs in the sacred writings. It does not mean that he continued no longer
> than to the first year of Cyrus, for he lived some years after the commencement of his
> reign; but this is the time to which the writer wished to direct especial attention, as it
> brought deliverance to the captive Jews."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 31_

[→] the chapter ends pointing **forward** — toward a deliverance named two centuries in
advance:

> "Daniel lived all the days of the Babylonish captivity,-over seventy years,-and had the
> pleasure of knowing that Cyrus whose name the prophet Isaiah had mentioned nearly two
> hundred years before he had issued his wonderful decree for the deliverance of God's
> people."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 27_

[→] "unto Cyrus" is **prophetic framing**, not biography — the captive who stayed loyal at
the table lived to see the God of prophecy keep His word.

## Appeal

- The God who let His own temple be stripped — and then made four captive boys outshine an
  empire — is the God who turns **your** humiliation into His stage. He asks first not for
  brilliance but for **loyalty in the small daily thing** — the table, the appetite, the
  unseen choice.
- Daniel _"purposed in his heart"_ **before** the pressure came (Dan 1:8). Settle your
  loyalty now — on your knees, by principle — not in the heat of the test. _"If any of you
  lack wisdom, let him ask of God"_ (Jas 1:5); the same God who gave Daniel understanding
  gives it still to those who **live their prayers** (PK 486).
- Appetite was Eden's gate and the wilderness's first blow — and it is the last generation's
  test too. _"A voluntary self-control of appetite lies at the foundation of every reform"_
  (SDP 24). Begin there.

[CHAIN] — **Link forged: faithfulness first.** Before a single symbol is decoded, the method
is set — loyalty to God in the smallest thing, settled by prayer and principle, is what God
rewards with light. This is the posture every key ahead requires. Hold it.
