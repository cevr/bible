# Daniel 4 — The Tree and the Seven Times

**Nebuchadnezzar's tree is the man himself — the Bible says so. And the "seven times"
that pass over him are seven literal years, fulfilled in history, which hand us the
master key of all prophecy: a time is a year. Hold that unit; it unlocks the 2520, the
1260, and every prophetic clock ahead. The lesson the tree teaches is the one every
kingdom must learn — the Most High ruleth.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Daniel 4 defines
> its own tree, its own stump, its own seven times — and then hands us a unit we will
> spend the whole series spending. Watch the verse hand us the dictionary, line upon line.

---

## The setup — a king's own public testimony

- Daniel 4 is unique: a **state document Nebuchadnezzar himself published**, _"unto all
  people, nations, and languages"_ after his humiliation (Dan 4:1)
- [→] this is no third-party report — it is the king's signed confession, the proudest
  monarch on earth telling the world how God broke him:

> "Nebuchadnezzar the king, unto all people, nations, and languages, that dwell in all
> the earth; Peace be multiplied unto you. I thought it good to shew the signs and
> wonders that the high God hath wrought toward me." (Daniel 4:1-2)

> "The fourth chapter of Daniel is, in some respects, the most wonderful chapter in the
> Bible. It is a public document written by Nebuchadnezzar, king of Babylon, after his
> humiliation by the God of heaven... It therefore comes to us with as much freshness and
> vitality as though it were issued to the generation in which we live."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 50_

- the king dreams again; the wise men fail again; **Daniel alone reads it** (Dan 4:4-8) —
  the Daniel 2 pattern repeats: human wisdom hits its wall, God reveals
- [→] this is the **third time** God has reached for this one king — the image (Dan 2),
  the furnace (Dan 3), now the tree (Dan 4). Frame the chapter as the climax of God's
  pursuit of Nebuchadnezzar:

> "...we have the wonderful incidents recorded in this chapter, showing the still
> unceasing efforts of the Lord to bring him to a full acknowledgment of himself."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 92_

## [KEY🔑] The great tree = Nebuchadnezzar himself

The dream first. Read it off the page:

> "I saw, and behold a tree in the midst of the earth, and the height thereof was great.
> The tree grew, and was strong, and the height thereof reached unto heaven, and the
> sight thereof to the end of all the earth: The leaves thereof were fair, and the fruit
> thereof much, and in it was meat for all: the beasts of the field had shadow under it,
> and the fowls of the heaven dwelt in the boughs thereof, and all flesh was fed of it."
> (Daniel 4:10-12)

A tree filling the earth, fruit for all, shade for the beasts, nests for the fowls. **Now
— what does the tree mean? We do not assign meaning. The verse defines its own symbol.**

> "It is **thou, O king**, that art grown and become strong: for thy greatness is grown,
> and reacheth unto heaven, and thy dominion to the end of the earth." (Daniel 4:22)

[KEY🔑] **tree = Nebuchadnezzar and his dominion** — named outright. Daniel tells the king
plainly: _the tree is you._ This is Scripture handing us the dictionary, exactly as Dan 2
said _"thou art this head of gold"_ and Dan 7 said _"beast = kingdom."_ **Same key, third
cut.**

> "He did so, plainly telling the king that the tree seen in the vision was emblematic of
> Nebuchadnezzar himself, and his dominion."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 54_

- the fruit, shade, and meat for all = the way the king governed:

> "...Nebuchadnezzar ruled his kingdom in such a way as to afford the fullest protection,
> support, and prosperity to all his subjects? Really to accomplish this is the perfection
> of earthly governments, and the highest glory of any kingdom."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 87_

## [→] The tree as God's design for government — and Babylon's root

The tree teaches more than one man's greatness. Two **overlays** the guides draw out —
each carried by its own quote, not by the verse alone:

- [→] **OVERLAY:** the tree shows what national government is _for_:

> "To Nebuchadnezzar the king the true object of national government was represented under
> the figure of a great tree... This representation shows the character of a government
> that fulfills God's purpose—a government that protects and upbuilds the nation."
> — _EGW, Education, p. 175_

- [→] **OVERLAY:** Haskell reads the tree's **root** expansively — the Babylonian _system
  of principles_ persists below the surface:

> "Gradually the principles upon which it was founded—principles much older than the king,
> for they originated with Lucifer, and were a perversion of heavenly truths—had taken
> root."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 54_

- [DYK🔎] this is the **Haskell historicist overlay** — Babylon's root springing up afresh
  into mystical Babylon. It is a teaching connector, **not the verse's own words.** Mark
  it [→], never [KEY🔑]. The verse defines the tree as the king; Haskell extends the root
  as a lesson.

### [⟲] The tree is a recapitulated TYPE — same picture, different power

- [⟲] the tree-symbol is **not unique to Babylon.** Ezekiel drew **Egypt** as the same
  towering tree (Ezek 31):

> "In this prophecy Egypt is represented as a mighty tree towering above all the trees of
> the earth... All the fowls of heaven nested in its boughs; the hosts of earth dwelt
> beneath its branches."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 51_

- [⟲] **resolve it out loud:** a tree means _a great power_ — same literary type — but
  **which** power is fixed by each text. Egypt in Ezekiel 31; Nebuchadnezzar in Daniel 4.
  Never carry one text's referent into another. Here the verse says _Nebuchadnezzar_
  (Dan 4:22), and that settles it.

## [KEY🔑] The Watcher's decree — "let seven times pass over him"

A sentence falls from heaven: hew down the tree, but **leave the stump** —

> "Hew down the tree, and cut off his branches... nevertheless leave the stump of his
> roots in the earth... and let seven times pass over him. This matter is by the decree
> of the watchers... to the intent that the living may know that the most High ruleth in
> the kingdom of men." (Daniel 4:14-17)

And the verse states the seven times' **purpose** in its own words:

> "...they shall make thee to eat grass as oxen... and seven times shall pass over thee,
> **till thou know that the most High ruleth in the kingdom of men, and giveth it to
> whomsoever he will.**" (Daniel 4:25)

[KEY🔑] **the verse defines its own outcome:** the seven times run _"till thou know that
the most High ruleth."_ That is the in-text definition of what the seven times are _for_ —
not a span to be guessed at, but a discipline aimed at a confession.

> "The parting words of the angel as he left Nebuchadnezzar were, 'This matter is by the
> decree of the watchers ...to the intent that the living may know that the Most High
> ruleth in the kingdom of men, and giveth it to whomsoever he will.'"
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 53_

- [→] the prophetic eye quotes the decree verbatim:

> "Let his heart be changed from man's, and let a beast's heart be given unto him; and
> let seven times pass over him. This matter is by the decree of the watchers, and the
> demand by the word of the holy ones."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 516_

- [→] **OVERLAY:** the Watchers = angels executing divine decrees (carried by the guide
  quote, not as a bare key):

> "They are represented as demanding this dealing with Nebuchadnezzar. They see, as
> mortals never can see, how unseemly a thing is pride in the human heart."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 88_

## [⟲] The crux — three "seven times," never to be blurred

Here is the chapter's most important — and most easily mistaken — point. The phrase
"seven times" appears in three places in Scripture, and they are **not the same thing.**
Get this right and the whole prophetic clock keeps time; blur it and everything ahead
runs wrong.

**FIRST — Daniel 4's own sense: seven literal YEARS.**

- the chapter is **history, not symbol.** The seven times over Nebuchadnezzar = seven
  literal years, historically fulfilled (Josephus confirms it):

> "This is plain, literal narration; hence the time is here to be understood literally...
> A 'time,' then, denotes one year. When used in symbolic prophecy, it would, of course,
> denote symbolic or prophetic time. A 'time' would then denote a prophetic year, or, each
> day standing for a year, three hundred and sixty literal years."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 88_

[→] **THIS is what Daniel 4 contributes to the prophetic toolbox.** From one king's seven
literal years, DAR derives the **master key**: a time denotes one year — literally here,
and (where the prophecy is symbolic) 360 literal years. This is DAR's expositional rule
built _on_ the verse, not a symbol the verse defines of itself (so [→], carried by the DAR
quote above, never [KEY🔑]). This unit, not the 2520, is the chapter's headline payoff.

- tie it to the year-day texts:

> "After the number of the days in which ye searched the land, even forty days, each
> day for a year, shall ye bear your iniquities..." (Numbers 14:34)

> "...I have appointed thee each day for a year." (Ezekiel 4:6)

- [→] a prophetic **day** = a literal **year**; a prophetic **year** = 360 days. Daniel 4's
  seven _literal_ years are the **historical proof** that establishes the unit; the
  _symbolic_ use is reserved for later. DAR itself says so: _"there will be occasion to
  refer to this fact under chapter 7:25."_ (DAR 88) We meet it again at **D07.**

**[⟲] SECOND — a DIFFERENT instance: the Leviticus 26 "seven times" (the 2520).**

This is where Miller is the named authority — DAR dropped the link, so we pull the
pioneer:

> "That, being a matter of history, and a sample only, was fulfilled in seven years; but
> this, being a prophecy, will only be fulfilled in seven prophetic times, which will be 7
> times 360 years, which will make 2520 years."
> — _William Miller, Evidence from Scripture and History, p. 261_

- [⟲] **resolve it explicitly:** Daniel 4 = the literal **type**; Leviticus 26 = the
  **2520-year prophecy.** _Not the same event._ Daniel 4 supplies the **unit** (a time =
  a year); Leviticus 26 supplies the **prophecy** (seven prophetic times = 2520 years).
  To call Daniel 4's seven years "the 2520" is the very same-vs-different blur the series
  guards against — like confusing Daniel 2's feet with Daniel 7's horns.

- [⟲] the **2520's dates**, per Miller — commencing **677 BC** (Manasseh's captivity, 2
  Chron 33:11), 2520 − 677 = **AD 1843**:

> "I answer, In the year 677 before Christ... Then take 677 years, which were before
> Christ, from 2520 years, which includes the whole 'seven times,' or 'seven years,'
> prophetic, and the remainder will be 1843 after Christ."
> — _William Miller, Evidence from Scripture and History, p. 262_

- [→] note the dissent honestly: DAR sets the 2520 **aside** for Daniel 4 entirely,
  reading the chapter's seven times as literal only (DAR 88). We teach the 2520 **straight
  per Miller** — but as a _separate prophecy the unit unlocks_, never as the meaning of
  Daniel 4's own seven years.

**[⟲] THIRD — the 1260 (half of the 2520).**

> "Twice three and a half are seven, and twice 1260 are 2520."
> — _William Miller, Views of the Prophecies, p. 44_

- [⟲] the **time, times, and half a time** of Dan 7:25 / Rev 12:6,14 / Rev 13:5 is **half**
  the 2520. Preview only — the 1260 is decoded at its home, **Dan 7:25 (D07).** Resolve:
  the 1260 (Dan 7 / Rev 12 / Rev 13) is the **same span in three wordings**; the 2520
  (Lev 26) is the **whole** of which it is half.

[Q] Three different "seven times" — **one man's** seven literal years (Dan 4), **one
nation's** 2520 prophetic years (Lev 26), **one power's** 1260-year half (Dan 7 / Rev 12 /
Rev 13) — all hanging on the single unit Daniel 4 hands us. Never let the picture blur:
the verse here means **seven literal years.** Everything else is reached by [⟲], not read
into the verse.

## [KEY🔑] The stump — the kingdom kept sure, and mercy in judgment

The axe spares one thing. The decree leaves the stump, banded with iron and brass (Dan
4:15, 23) — and the verse defines what the stump means:

> "And whereas they commanded to leave the stump of the tree roots; **thy kingdom shall be
> sure unto thee**, after that thou shalt have known that the heavens do rule." (Daniel
> 4:26)

[KEY🔑] **stump = Nebuchadnezzar's preserved kingship** — the in-text definition. The tree
is cut down, but the root remains; the throne is held in trust and **restored** once the
king learns the lesson. The verse says it plainly: _thy kingdom shall be sure unto thee._

- [→] the mercy God mingles with judgment:

> "When order was given that this tree should be cut down, it was commanded that the stump
> of the roots should be left in the earth... that it might not be wholly given to decay,
> but that the source of future growth and greatness might be left. The day is coming when
> the wicked shall be cut down, and no such residue of hope be left them."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 88_

- [⟲] **DISTINGUISH — different, not same.** Haskell applies the stump _expansively_ —
  Babylon's root not destroyed, springing up afresh in every generation:

> "The principles upon which it was founded... had taken root."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 54_

- [⟲] flag this as a [→] **overlay**, not the verse's key. The verse itself (Dan 4:26)
  defines the stump **narrowly**: the king's restored throne. Do **not** conflate the
  literal preserved-kingship (the verse) with the typological persisting-error (Haskell's
  reading). Same word, two layers — keep them apart.

## Pride reaches its climax — the king repeats Satan's words

Twelve months of probation pass. Then the boast:

> "The king spake, and said, Is not this great Babylon, that I have built for the house of
> the kingdom by the might of my power, and for the honour of my majesty?" (Daniel 4:30)

- [→] pride had been ripening the whole year:

> "...all the time he was cherishing pride in his heart, and at length it reached a climax
> beyond which God could not suffer it to pass... The time had come for his humiliation."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 91_

- [⟲] **OVERLAY weave** — the boast echoes Satan himself:

> "He was repeating the thoughts, almost the exact words, of Satan, when he sought to
> exalt his throne above God."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 57_

- [⟲] resolve it: _"I will exalt my throne above the stars of God... I will be like the
  most High"_ (Isa 14:13-14) — **pride is the root transgression uniting the king and the
  adversary.** A typological echo, carried by the guide quote, not a verse key.
- and the sentence executes _"while the word was in the king's mouth"_ (Dan 4:31) —
  immediately.

## Seven years fulfilled — and the lesson learned

> "The same hour was the thing fulfilled upon Nebuchadnezzar: and he was driven from men,
> and did eat grass as oxen, and his body was wet with the dew of heaven, till his hairs
> were grown like eagles' feathers, and his nails like birds' claws." (Daniel 4:33)

- the prediction fulfilled **to the letter** — _"seven times shall pass over thee"_ (Dan
  4:32) ran its full course in history. **This is the history that proves the unit-rule.**

> "For seven years Nebuchadnezzar was an astonishment to all his subjects... Then his
> reason was restored and, looking up in humility to the God of heaven, he recognized the
> divine hand in his chastisement."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 520_

> "God allowed him to suffer seven years of deplorable degradation... he was brought to
> see his own weakness; he confessed his guilt, and acknowledged the God of heaven... He
> had learned that those who walk in pride, God is able to abase."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 59_

- [⟲] and this very fulfillment is what later grounds the **year-day method** — Miller
  listed it among the predictions verified in history:

> "Nebuchadnezzar's seven times (Daniel 4:13-16)... [were] fulfilled in accordance with
> the predictions."
> — _EGW, The Great Controversy, p. 323_

[DYK🔎] notice the chain: Daniel 4's seven _literal_ years come true exactly as foretold —
and _that track record_ is what licenses us to trust the same unit when prophecy later
uses it symbolically. The history proves the rule.

## The Most High ruleth — the proud monarch becomes a humble child of God

Reason returns; the king looks up and blesses heaven:

> "And at the end of the days I Nebuchadnezzar lifted up mine eyes unto heaven, and mine
> understanding returned unto me, and I blessed the most High... whose dominion is an
> everlasting dominion... and none can stay his hand." (Daniel 4:34-35)

- [→] this is the very lesson the seven times were sent to teach (Dan 4:25):

> "The lesson of humility was learned... He was ready to acknowledge that the Most High
> rules in the kingdom of men, and gives it to whomsoever he will."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 92_

> "The once proud monarch had become a humble child of God... Nebuchadnezzar had learned
> at last the lesson which all rulers need to learn—that true greatness consists in true
> goodness."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 521_

- [→] **OVERLAY for every reader, not only kings** — Daniel's counsel _"break off thy sins
  by righteousness"_ (Dan 4:27) is the lesson of life for all:

> "The power exercised by every ruler on the earth is Heaven-imparted... And to each the
> words spoken to Nebuchadnezzar of old are the lesson of life: 'Break off thy sins by
> righteousness, and thine iniquities by showing mercy to the poor; if it may be a
> lengthening of thy tranquility.'"
> — _EGW, Education, p. 174_

- [→] and so the man's life closes — God's pursuit succeeded:

> "...the still unceasing efforts of the Lord to bring him to a full acknowledgment of
> himself. And may we not hope that the most illustrious king of the first prophetic
> kingdom, the head of gold, may at last have part in that kingdom before which all earthly
> kingdoms shall become as chaff, and the glory of which shall never dim?"
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 92_

- [→] the image, the furnace, and the tree were **one pursuit** — three reaches of the
  same merciful hand for one proud king, and the third one held.

## Appeal

- The God who could humble the greatest king on earth **to the level of the beasts** — and
  then lift him back to a throne as a humble child — is the God who _"ruleth in the kingdom
  of men, and giveth it to whomsoever he will"_ (Dan 4:25).
- Pride is the one sin every heart shares with the fallen monarch **and** the fallen angel
  alike (SDP 57). The seven times were **mercy, not cruelty** — _"those that walk in pride
  he is able to abase"_ (Dan 4:37), and willing to restore.
- The same word stands to you: _"break off thy sins by righteousness"_ (Dan 4:27). The King
  of heaven still pursues, still mingles mercy with judgment, still leaves the stump. **Bow
  now, and the lesson need not be learned the hard way.**

[CHAIN] — **Link forged: a time = a year (the year-day unit) — and tree = Nebuchadnezzar,
stump = his restored kingdom, defined by the verse itself.** Daniel 4's seven _literal_
years prove the unit that unlocks the 2520 (Lev 26, Miller), the 1260 (Dan 7:25), and
every prophetic clock ahead. Hold the unit; keep the three "seven times" distinct.
