# Daniel 5 — The Handwriting on the Wall

**The prophecy doesn't just predict the future — it keeps its appointments. In one night
Babylon falls exactly as Daniel 2 and Daniel 7 said it would: gold gives way to silver,
the lion to the bear. And the Bible itself reads the verdict off the wall — MENE, TEKEL,
PERES: numbered, weighed, divided. The metals are no longer theory. They are history,
dated to the night a king despised the light he already had.**

> The rule of the road: a symbol means what Scripture says it means. Daniel 2 forecast the
> metals; Daniel 5 is the night they become a dated fact. Watch the prophecy pay off, line
> upon line.

---

## The setup — the last night of an empire that did not know it

- Years after Daniel 2's dream and Daniel 7's beasts, the **gold / lion kingdom is on its
  last night** (Dan 5:1) — the prophecy is about to be **proven**, not merely predicted
- Belshazzar feasts with a thousand lords; the **sacred temple vessels** are called for and
  profaned (Dan 5:1-4)

> "Belshazzar the king made a great feast to a thousand of his lords, and drank wine
> before the thousand... they brought the golden vessels that were taken out of the temple
> of the house of God which was at Jerusalem; and the king, and his princes, his wives, and
> his concubines, drank in them." (Daniel 5:1-3)

Haskell sets the scene — a doomed nation feasting in the dark:

> "It was the last night of a nation's existence, but the people knew it not... He ordered
> that the sacred vessels be brought from the temple to show that no being, human or divine,
> could raise a hand against him."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 68_

[→] frame for the whole session: **this is the chapter where Daniel 2's metals stop being
a forecast and become a dated fact.** DAR says it outright:

> "The chief feature of interest pertaining to this chapter is the fact that it describes
> the closing scenes of the Babylonish empire, the transition from the gold to the silver
> of the great image of chapter 2, and from the lion to the bear of Daniel's vision in
> chapter 7."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 94_

## [→] The sacred vessels — the line that crossed

The vessels are not a prophetic symbol — no verse defines them as one. They are the
**pioneers' reading of the narrative**: a standing divine witness, carried into captivity
yet still God's, and the act of profaning them is what crosses the line.

- [→] **overlay reading (Haskell):** the temple vessels are a continuing witness even in
  exile — guarded by angels from Jerusalem to Babylon — and their desecration is the act
  that triggers immediate judgment (Dan 5:2-4)
- [→] the principle generalizes: **profaning what God has set apart is the act that invites
  judgment.** Mark the discipline — this is _teaching_; the verse itself simply narrates the
  act (Dan 5:2-4). It carries no [KEY🔑] because no verse defines the vessels as a symbol.

[DYK🔎] **Not a [⟲] symbol-weave.** The vessels of Dan 1:2 (taken) and Dan 5:2-3
(profaned) are the **same physical objects** — narrative continuity inside one book, not a
prophetic-symbolic recapitulation. Do not mistake plain story-continuity for a symbol
returning across the canon.

> "It was the last night of a nation's existence, but the people knew it not... He ordered
> that the sacred vessels be brought from the temple to show that no being, human or divine,
> could raise a hand against him."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 68_

## The hand on the wall — judgment announced without spectacle

- a **bloodless hand** writes over against the candlestick; terror seizes the king (Dan
  5:5-6)

> "In the same hour came forth fingers of a man's hand, and wrote over against the
> candlestick upon the plaister of the wall of the king's palace: and the king saw the part
> of the hand that wrote. Then the king's countenance was changed, and his thoughts troubled
> him." (Daniel 5:5-6)

No thunder, no lightning — the quietest possible judgment:

> "No flashes of supernatural light, nor deafening peals of thunder, announced the
> interference of God... A hand silently appeared, tracing mystic characters upon the
> wall... Although he could not read the writing, he knew it was no message of peace and
> blessing."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 95_

The prophetic eye confirms the unseen Watcher behind the hand:

> "Little did Belshazzar think that there was a heavenly Witness to his idolatrous
> revelry... a bloodless hand came forth and traced upon the walls of the palace characters
> that gleamed like fire."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 524_

[DYK🔎] **Not a [⟲] symbol-weave.** The hand of Dan 5:5 is a **one-time historical
theophany** — it defines itself in the narrative. It is **not** the prophetic "hand" of the
visions, so no cross-book weave is required. Keeping the marker discipline clean is why it
gets no [⟲].

## Daniel's rebuke — known light despised

- the wise men fail; the **queen-mother** remembers Daniel (Dan 5:10-12); Daniel disclaims
  the rewards (Dan 5:17)

[DYK🔎] an auxiliary aside, never load-bearing: the "queen" here is the queen mother, and
"father" follows ancient custom:

> "Nebuchadnezzar is here called Belshazzar's father, according to the then common
> custom... Nebuchadnezzar was in reality his grandfather."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 96_

Daniel does not soften the charge — he rehearses Nebuchadnezzar's humbling and names what
Belshazzar did with that light:

> "And thou his son, O Belshazzar, hast not humbled thine heart, though thou knewest all
> this; But hast lifted up thyself against the Lord of heaven." (Daniel 5:22-23)

> "[Belshazzar] knew all this, yet he had not humbled his heart, but had lifted up himself
> against the God of heaven."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 97_

Haskell sharpens it to the heart's own severed line:

> "[T]hou knewest all this; but hast lifted up thyself against the Lord of heaven... he had
> severed the ties which bind heaven and earth together; and there was no way for that
> life-giving Spirit of God to reach him."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 71_

The prophetic eye confirms the accountability — wasted opportunity, and a king who could
plead no excuse for the light he had thrown away:

> "Many had been his opportunities to know the divine will... He wasted the opportunities
> graciously granted him."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 522_

> "...he must render an account of the stewardship entrusted him, and that for his wasted
> opportunities and his defiant attitude he could offer no excuse."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 524_

[→] **the theme the bare prophecy-proof would miss:** this is not arbitrary doom — it is
**light despised.** The hand wrote because the heart would not humble. (This carries the
Appeal.)

## [KEY🔑] MENE — God hath numbered thy kingdom

Now the chapter does what the method lives for: **the verse defines its own symbol.**
Scripture hands us the dictionary — we read the label, we do not interpret it.

> "This is the interpretation of the thing: MENE; God hath numbered thy kingdom, and
> finished it." (Daniel 5:26)

- [KEY🔑] this qualifies because **the verse itself states the interpretation** — the in-text
  key, exactly the Dan 2:38 ("thou art this head of gold") / Dan 7:17 ("these beasts... are
  four kings") pattern
- **MENE = numbered** — the days of the kingdom counted out and ended

> "Mene, numbered... God, whom thou hast defied, has thy kingdom in his own hands, and has
> numbered its days and finished its course."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 98_

---

## [KEY🔑] TEKEL — Thou art weighed in the balances, and found wanting

Again the verse defines its own symbol. The balance is read straight off the page.

> "TEKEL; Thou art weighed in the balances, and art found wanting." (Daniel 5:27)

- [KEY🔑] **verse-defined** — no overlay needed; Scripture states the meaning in the clause
  itself
- **TEKEL = weighed** — the king set on the scales of heaven and found short

God even speaks in language the king already knew:

> "It is a common belief among idolaters that the gods weigh deeds in balances... The
> language, therefore, was familiar to King Belshazzar... the words came with peculiar force
> because of their familiarity with religious customs."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 72_

DAR confirms the plain reading — pride weighed against vanity, and lighter:

> "Thou, who hast lifted up thy heart in pride... art weighed, and found lighter than
> vanity."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 98_

---

## [KEY🔑] PERES — Thy kingdom is divided, and given to the Medes and Persians

The third word defines its own symbol **and names the successor outright.**

> "PERES; Thy kingdom is divided, and given to the Medes and Persians." (Daniel 5:28)

- [KEY🔑] the verse **names Medo-Persia by name** — this is Scripture labeling the silver /
  bear before the weave even reaches it
- **PERES = divided** — Babylon's dominion handed to the next empire on the image

> "Thy kingdom, which thou didst dream was to stand forever, is divided between the foes
> already waiting at thy gates."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 98_

[→] note DAR's word **"didst dream"** — it points straight back to Daniel 2. This verse is
the hinge into the central weave: **the Bible has just named the kingdom that replaces the
gold / lion.**

## [⟲] The weave — the metals PROVEN: gold → silver, lion → bear

Here is the heart of the session, and where interpretive risk concentrates. Dan 5:28's
"given to the Medes and Persians" is the **same transition** Daniel 2 and Daniel 7 foretold
— and DAR ties all three pictures together explicitly.

**[⟲] gold head = lion = Babylon:**

> "Thou art this head of gold." (Daniel 2:38)

> "The first was like a lion, and had eagle's wings..." (Daniel 7:4)

- the **gold head (Dan 2:32, 38) = the lion (Dan 7:4) = Babylon** — _same kingdom, two
  pictures._ Daniel 5 is the night that closes **both** sequences at once.

**[⟲] silver breast = bear = Medo-Persia:**

> "...his breast and his arms of silver... another kingdom inferior to thee." (Daniel
> 2:32, 39)

> "And behold another beast, a second, like to a bear..." (Daniel 7:5)

- the **silver breast (Dan 2:39) = the bear (Dan 7:5) = Medo-Persia** — _same kingdom_, the
  successor the prophecy named and Dan 5:28 now confirms **by name**

DAR is the establishing quote — it equates the two sequences in one sentence:

> "The chief feature of interest pertaining to this chapter is the fact that it describes
> the closing scenes of the Babylonish empire, the transition from the gold to the silver
> of the great image of chapter 2, and from the lion to the bear of Daniel's vision in
> chapter 7."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 94_

[→] **guard the distinction** (the worked-example discipline): this is a **same-instance**
weave — one historical night closing **two** prophetic chains, **not** a recapitulated /
different instance. Do not blur it. And note the payoff: the forward-promise made back in
D02 — _"Daniel 5 will confirm this when Babylon falls in a single night"_ — is now paid in
full.

change hands — what does that track record say about the metals still future?

## The fall — Babylon ceases in a night; why Daniel survived

The interpretation and the conquest happen in the **same hour:**

> "In that night was Belshazzar the king of the Chaldeans slain. And Darius the Median took
> the kingdom..." (Daniel 5:30-31)

> "[While] Daniel was making known the fearful import of the heavenly writing, the Persian
> soldiery, through the emptied channel of the Euphrates, had made their way into the heart
> of the city... and with him the empire of Babylon ceased to be."
> — _Uriah Smith, Daniel and the Revelation, p. 99_

Haskell draws the principle from the wreckage:

> "In that night was Belshazzar slain, and the kingdom was given to Darius... Thus came to
> an end one of the proudest monarchies... When an individual or a nation fills up the cup
> of iniquity, and passes the limit of God's mercy, they are quickly humbled in the dust."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 75_

[DYK🔎] an auxiliary aside — prophecy meeting prophecy at the seam: why the conqueror
spared Daniel.

> "This left Daniel sole ruler in Babylon. He knowing that over one hundred years before,
> Isaiah had prophesied that Cyrus should take the kingdom, was ready to welcome him whom
> God had said should build the house of the Lord at Jerusalem."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 75_

Daniel knew Isaiah had named Cyrus more than a century in advance (Dan 5:31; cf. Isa
44:28-45:1) — one prophecy handing off to another at the very moment of the fall.

[→] **the metals proven before our eyes:** gold gave way to silver on a fixed night,
exactly as Dan 2:39 / Dan 7:5 said. The prophecy keeps its appointments.

## [→] The cup of iniquity — Babylon's fall as the pattern

The fall is not only past-tense vindication — it is a **pattern.** No verse names this as a
symbol, so it is a [→] overlay carrying its guide quotes, never a bare key.

- [→] **overlay reading (Haskell):** the filling-up of the cup of iniquity repeats in every
  nation, and applies eschatologically to "modern Babylon" (Rev 18)

The prophetic eye states the exhausted forbearance:

> "Belshazzar and his lords had filled up the measure of their guilt... No longer could
> God's restraining hand ward off the impending evil... God had at last found it necessary
> to pass the irrevocable sentence."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 530_

And states the historicist principle outright — Babylon's fall is the pattern every empire
repeats:

> "Prophecy has traced the rise and progress of the world's great empires—Babylon,
> Medo-Persia, Greece, and Rome... Each has had its period of test; each has failed."
> — _EGW, Prophets and Kings, p. 535_

- [→] **overlay reading (Haskell) — the great-controversy frame:**

> "Heaven and unfallen worlds watched the progress of affairs in this great city, for it
> was the battle-ground of the two mighty forces of good and evil. Christ and Satan here
> contended."
> — _S.N. Haskell, The Story of Daniel the Prophet, p. 69_

Mark the discipline: this is the controversy framework laid **over** the narrative, not the
verse's own words — a [→] overlay, not a [KEY🔑].

[⟲] a light forward gesture (not a full weave): the cup filled and _"Babylon is fallen"_
will return at Rev 14:8 / Rev 18 — **same pattern, end-time antitype.** We meet it fully in
Revelation.

## Appeal

- The God who named Babylon's successor centuries early — and the very night the gold would
  yield to the silver — holds **your** future too. The metals proven in Daniel 5 are the
  proof the rest of the prophecy is _"sure"_ (Dan 2:45).
- But the sharper appeal is the heart's. Belshazzar fell **not for lack of light but for
  despising the light he had** (Dan 5:22; DAR 97.2; SDP 71.1; PK 522.2). _"Thou knewest all
  this"_ is the indictment — and the most dangerous place to stand is **in the light,
  unhumbled.**
- God still writes warnings **before** judgment — the hand wrote so the king would _know_
  (DAR 95.3). _"We would have healed Babylon, but she is not healed"_ (Jer 51:9; PK 530.3).
  The mercy always precedes the sentence.
- [→] the call: **humble the heart while the candle still burns.** The cup is not yet full
  for you. _"To day if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts"_ (Heb 3:7-8).

[CHAIN] — **Link proven: gold → silver / lion → bear, dated to one night.** The metals of
Daniel 2 and the beasts of Daniel 7 are no longer forecast — they are history. Babylon
fell; Medo-Persia (Dan 5:28, the silver / bear) now rules. A prophecy that keeps its
appointments can be trusted for every link still ahead. Hold it.
