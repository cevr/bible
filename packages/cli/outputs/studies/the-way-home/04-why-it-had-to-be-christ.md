# Study 4: Why It Had to Be Christ

**Study 3 revealed what salvation is — the restoration of God's image. But a blueprint without a builder is just paper. Why Christ? Why not an angel? Why become a man? Was He truly one of us — or did He have an advantage we don't? And how can God forgive sinners without becoming unjust Himself? The answer to every question is the same Person.**

## Prep Reading

- Hebrews 1:1-6; 2:9-18
- Romans 3:25-26; 8:3-4
- Philippians 2:5-8
- John 1:1-3, 14
- Isaiah 53:4-6, 10-11
- 1 John 1:9

---

## The Problem Restated — Why Forgiveness Is Not Simple

Study 3 established the chain: Image = Character = Law = Love = Life. Sin shattered the image. The gospel restores it. But here is a question most people never ask:

**How can God forgive the guilty and still be just?**

- **The scandal of pardon**
  - "He that justifieth the wicked, and he that condemneth the just, even they both are abomination to the LORD" (Prov 17:15)
  - God's own word says: acquitting the guilty is an _abomination_. If a human judge pardons a murderer because he likes him, we call that corruption — not mercy.
  - And God Himself declared it: "The LORD God, merciful and gracious... forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty" (Ex 34:6-7). Study 3 showed this is God's self-portrait. Read it again: He _forgives_ — and He _will by no means clear the guilty_. Both in the same breath. How?
  - [→] So when God forgives sinners, how is He not breaking His own standard? Satan knew this problem. He pressed it.

- **Satan's legal argument**
  - Satan charged God: if the law is broken, the sinner must die — and if God pardons the sinner, He is not just. Justice and mercy cannot coexist.
  - "The soul that sinneth, it shall die" (Eze 18:20). That is the law's demand. There is no exception clause.
  - "For the wages of sin is death" (Rom 6:23). The penalty is not arbitrary — it is intrinsic. Sin and life cannot coexist, just as fire and water cannot.
  - [→] If God simply waives the penalty, He is unjust — Proverbs 17:15 condemns Him by His own word. If God enforces the penalty, every sinner dies. God needed a way to do _both_ — satisfy the law's demand AND save the sinner. That required a very specific Person.

## Why Not an Angel?

- **Angels are created — the law is the Creator's character**
  - "For unto which of the angels said he at any time, Thou art my Son, this day have I begotten thee?" (Heb 1:5)
  - "And again, when he bringeth in the firstbegotten into the world, he saith, And let all the angels of God worship him" (Heb 1:6)
  - Angels worship the Son. They are not His equals. They are not the Author of the law.
  - [→] The law is God's character (Study 3). Only the Author of that character has the standing to satisfy its claim. A creature paying the Creator's debt is a category error.

- **He took not on Him the nature of angels**
  - "For verily he took not on him the nature of angels; but he took on him the seed of Abraham" (Heb 2:16)
  - Paul says this as if settling a debate. Not angels — Abraham's seed. Human. Specific. Deliberate.
  - [→] The gap was between God and man. Only someone who _is_ both can bridge it. An angel is neither the offended Lawgiver nor the offending race.

- **No intercessor found — so God's own arm brought salvation**
  - "And he saw that there was no man, and wondered that there was no intercessor: therefore his arm brought salvation unto him; and his righteousness, it sustained him" (Isa 59:16)
  - God looked for someone who could intervene. No man qualified. No angel qualified. So He did it Himself.
  - [→] The law's demands can only be met by the One whose character it expresses. No created being has that standing.

[Q] **"Could God just create a new law?"**
→ No. The law is not a set of arbitrary rules God invented. It is a transcript of His character (Study 3). Changing the law would mean changing Himself. "I am the LORD, I change not" (Mal 3:6). "Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law" (Matt 5:18). God did not change the standard. He met it Himself.

## The One Who Could — God Becomes Man

- **In the beginning was the Word**
  - "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God" (John 1:1)
  - "All things were made by him; and without him was not any thing made that was made" (John 1:3)
  - He is not a created being. He is the Creator. The same One who spoke the law spoke the worlds into existence.

- **The Word made flesh**
  - "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us" (John 1:14)
  - "But when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law" (Gal 4:4)
  - Made under the law — subject to the very standard He authored. Not exempt. Not visiting. _Under_ it.

- **The great descent**
  - "Who, being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God: but made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: and being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross" (Phil 2:6-8)
  - Five downward steps: form of God → no reputation → form of servant → likeness of men → death of the cross
  - [→] He did not descend partway. He went all the way down.

- **Why both God and man?**
  - "For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace" (Isa 9:6)
  - A child _born_ — human. A son _given_ — divine. Mighty God and Prince of Peace in the same Person.
  - "For it became him, for whom are all things, and by whom are all things, in bringing many sons unto glory, to make the captain of their salvation perfect through sufferings" (Heb 2:10)
  - As God — He had the _standing_ to satisfy the law
  - As man — He had the _ground_ to represent the race
  - Neither alone is sufficient. Both together is the gospel.

[DYK🔎] The Hebrew word for atonement (כָּפַר, _kaphar_) means "to cover" — but its deeper sense is "to ransom, to reconcile." The root appears in the mercy seat (כַּפֹּרֶת, _kapporeth_) — the lid of the ark where God's presence met the blood. Atonement is not God looking away from sin. It is God drawing near _through_ the payment. Only someone with infinite standing could make a payment of infinite weight.

## Was He Truly a Man?

This is the crux. If Christ had an advantage we don't have, His life proves nothing for us. If He couldn't fail, the cosmic trial (Study 1) is theater. Satan's charges are only answered if Christ walked _our_ ground.

- **In all things made like His brethren**
  - "Wherefore in all things it behoved him to be made like unto his brethren, that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest" (Heb 2:17)
  - "In all things" — not most things. Not the easy things. All things.

- **Partaker of flesh and blood**
  - "Forasmuch then as the children are partakers of flesh and blood, he also himself likewise took part of the same" (Heb 2:14)
  - "Likewise" — the _same_ substance. Not a superior version. Not angelic flesh wearing a human costume.

- **Tempted in all points**
  - "For we have not an high priest which cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities; but was in all points tempted like as we are, yet without sin" (Heb 4:15)
  - He _felt_ the pull of temptation. "Cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities" — double negative for emphasis. He felt them.

- **Sent in the likeness of sinful flesh**
  - "God sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh, and for sin, condemned sin in the flesh" (Rom 8:3)
  - Not "likeness of flesh" — _sinful_ flesh. Paul is precise. Christ took the nature that had been damaged by four thousand years of sin.
  - [→] He condemned sin in the very territory where sin had reigned. He fought on occupied ground — and won.

- **He hungered, wept, bled, slept**
  - Hungered (Matt 4:2). Wept (John 11:35). Bled (Luke 22:44). Slept (Mark 4:38). Died (John 19:30).
  - These are not performances. They are the conditions of genuine humanity. He was not pretending.

- **Perfected through sufferings — the risk was real**
  - "Who in the days of his flesh, when he had offered up prayers and supplications with strong crying and tears unto him that was able to save him from death, and was heard in that he feared" (Heb 5:7)
  - "Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered" (Heb 5:8)
  - Strong crying. Tears. Fear. _Learned_ obedience. This is not the language of a staged performance. If He could not have failed, there is nothing to learn and nothing to fear.
  - [→] If the test was not real, it does not answer Satan's charge. The Son of God in human flesh genuinely depended on the Father — and the Father heard Him because He feared.

[Q] **"Did He have sinful propensities — or just sinful flesh?"**
→ Christ took human nature as it was after the fall — weakened, with the physical and mental effects of sin's inheritance. But He never consented to sin, never cherished a sinful thought. The distinction is between _condition_ and _consent_. He was subject to temptation from without and weakness from within — but He never yielded. "The prince of this world cometh, and hath nothing in me" (John 14:30). Nothing to grab. Not because He was shielded — but because at every point He chose the Father's will.

## How He Overcame — And What That Means for Us

Here is the key: **Christ did not rely on His divine prerogatives to resist temptation.**

- **"I can of mine own self do nothing"**
  - "I can of mine own self do nothing: as I hear, I judge: and my judgment is just; because I seek not mine own will, but the will of the Father which hath sent me" (John 5:30)
  - The Creator of the universe, walking in human flesh, says: _I can do nothing by myself._
  - He did not reach into His divine nature to resist temptation. He lived as a man dependent on the Father — the same dependence available to every human being.

- **Led by the Spirit**
  - "And Jesus being full of the Holy Ghost returned from Jordan, and was led by the Spirit into the wilderness, being forty days tempted of the devil" (Luke 4:1-2)
  - Full of the Holy Ghost. The same Spirit He promises to us (Acts 2:38; John 14:16-17). He overcame by the Spirit's power — not by latent divinity.

- **He answered as a man — with Scripture**
  - "It is written, Man shall not live by bread alone, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of God" (Matt 4:4)
  - He did not say "I am God, I command you to leave." He said "It is written." He fought with the same weapon He hands to us.

- **The significance for the cosmic trial**
  - "For as by one man's disobedience many were made sinners, so by the obedience of one shall many be made righteous" (Rom 5:19)
  - Adam's disobedience broke the race. Christ's obedience — in the _same_ flesh — proves the law can be kept. Not in heavenly conditions, but in earthly ones. Not by divine override, but by a man trusting His Father.
  - "I delight to do thy will, O my God: yea, thy law is within my heart" (Ps 40:8)
  - The law was not a burden to Him. It was His delight — because it was His Father's character. He kept it not by gritting His teeth but by loving His Father.
  - [→] This is the answer to Charge B from Study 1. Satan said the law cannot be kept. Christ kept it — in human flesh, by faith, through the Spirit. Not as God wearing a man suit. As a man clinging to God.

[ILL] Two students take the same exam. One has the answer key taped to his desk. The other studies, struggles, and passes honestly. Only the second student proves the exam is fair. If Christ overcame by divine power unavailable to us, He proved nothing except that God can pass God's own test. But He overcame as a _man_ — and that changes everything.

## Faithful AND Just to Forgive

Now the question from the beginning can be answered.

- **The heart of the problem**
  - Justice demands the penalty be paid. Mercy wants the sinner pardoned. These appear irreconcilable.
  - "The soul that sinneth, it shall die" (Eze 18:20). "For the wages of sin is death" (Rom 6:23). The law does not negotiate. And yet — "Have I any pleasure at all that the wicked should die? saith the Lord GOD: and not that he should return from his ways, and live?" (Eze 18:23). God does not _want_ the sinner to die. But the law _requires_ it. How can both be true?

- **The solution: the Lawgiver pays the law's demand**
  - "Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows... he was wounded for our transgressions, he was bruised for our iniquities: the chastisement of our peace was upon him; and with his stripes we are healed" (Isa 53:4-5)
  - "All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned every one to his own way; and the LORD hath laid on him the iniquity of us all" (Isa 53:6)
  - "When thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin" (Isa 53:10)
  - The wages of sin is death (Rom 6:23). The law demanded death. It got death — the death of the Lawgiver Himself.

- **This is why it had to be Christ — and no one else**
  - If I owe you a debt and a stranger pays it, that is generous but strange. If _you_ — the one I owe — absorb the cost yourself, the debt is satisfied by the creditor. No one is cheated. No one is bribed. Justice is met and mercy is given by the _same act._
  - Christ is both the offended Lawgiver and the offering. He does not ask a third party to suffer for a problem between God and man. He steps into the gap Himself.

- **Romans 3:25-26 — the definitive text**
  - "Whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in his blood, to declare his righteousness for the remission of sins that are past, through the forbearance of God; to declare, I say, at this time his righteousness: that he might be just, AND the justifier of him which believeth in Jesus" (Rom 3:25-26)
  - Paul says both words: **just** and **justifier**. Not one at the expense of the other. Both, simultaneously, in one act.
  - God is just — the penalty is real, fully paid in Christ's blood
  - God is the justifier — the sinner who believes goes free
  - [→] The cross is not mercy _instead of_ justice. It is mercy _through_ justice. The penalty is not waived — it is absorbed by the Judge Himself.

- **"Faithful and just to forgive"**
  - "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins, and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness" (1 John 1:9)
  - Most people read this as "faithful and _merciful_." But John says _just_. Forgiveness is not leniency. It is the application of a payment already made.
  - He is _faithful_ to His promise. He is _just_ because the penalty has been met. The cross turns forgiveness from a legal problem into a legal certainty.

- **Mercy and justice — not enemies but partners**
  - "Mercy and truth are met together; righteousness and peace have kissed each other" (Ps 85:10)
  - "Justice and judgment are the habitation of thy throne: mercy and truth shall go before thy face" (Ps 89:14)
  - Justice is not the opposite of mercy. They share a throne. Satan's strategy was to make them contradict each other — to force God to choose one or the other. The cross makes them embrace. In one act, God is fully just (the penalty is paid) and fully merciful (the sinner goes free).
  - "God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them" (2 Cor 5:19)
  - [→] Not imputing trespasses — not because they don't exist, but because they have been laid on Another (Isa 53:6).

[DYK🔎] The Greek word for "propitiation" in Romans 3:25 is ἱλαστήριον (_hilasterion_) — the same word used in the Septuagint for the mercy seat (כַּפֹּרֶת). Paul is saying: Christ _is_ the mercy seat. The place where God's presence met the blood of the sacrifice — that is Christ. The furniture of the sanctuary pointed to a Person.

## What the Cross Settled — Satan Unmasked

The cross did not only save sinners. It exposed Satan.

- **"Now is the judgment of this world"**
  - "Now is the judgment of this world: now shall the prince of this world be cast out" (John 12:31)
  - Christ spoke this about the cross. The cross is not just atonement for us — it is _judgment_ on Satan.

- **Satan's true character revealed — murderer**
  - "He was a murderer from the beginning, and abode not in the truth, because there is no truth in him. When he speaketh a lie, he speaketh of his own: for he is a liar, and the father of it" (John 8:44)
  - Christ named Satan's character: _murderer from the beginning_. But the universe needed to see it. At the cross, they did. Satan drove the conspiracy that killed the Son of God — the One who had done nothing but love, heal, and serve.
  - For millennia Satan had posed as an aggrieved reformer — someone with a legitimate complaint about God's government. At the cross, his true character was unmasked. He didn't just argue against God's law. He killed the Lawgiver.
  - "For this purpose the Son of God was manifested, that he might destroy the works of the devil" (1 John 3:8)
  - [→] The cross destroyed Satan's works by _exposing_ them. The universe saw who was really the murderer, and who was really the lover.

- **The accuser cast down**
  - "Now is come salvation, and strength, and the kingdom of our God, and the power of his Christ: for the accuser of our brethren is cast down, which accused them before our God day and night" (Rev 12:10)
  - Cast down — not by force, but by evidence. The cross _is_ the evidence.
  - "To the intent that now unto the principalities and powers in heavenly places might be known by the church the manifold wisdom of God" (Eph 3:10) — the cosmic audience from Study 1 watched. And what they saw at the cross settled their questions forever.

- **Principalities spoiled**
  - "Having spoiled principalities and powers, he made a shew of them openly, triumphing over them in it" (Col 2:15)
  - "In it" — in the cross. The instrument of Satan's apparent victory became the instrument of his exposure.

- **The four charges answered**

  | Charge (Study 1)          | What the cross reveals                                                                                                                                                                               |
  | ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
  | **God is selfish**        | He gave His own Son. "God commendeth his love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us" (Rom 5:8). A selfish God does not die for rebels.                                   |
  | **The law can't be kept** | Christ kept it — in human flesh, by faith. "By the obedience of one shall many be made righteous" (Rom 5:19). His life is the refutation.                                                            |
  | **God buys loyalty**      | Christ won loyalty while hanging on a cross — no bribery, no spectacle, no compulsion. "Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom" (Luke 23:42). The thief chose Him at His lowest moment. |
  | **God is unjust**         | The cross satisfies justice and extends mercy simultaneously (Rom 3:26). God did not bend the rules. He bore the cost Himself.                                                                       |

- **The verdict**
  - By His life — the law can be kept in human flesh. By His death — sin can be forgiven without destroying justice. By His resurrection — death has no final claim.
  - "Who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is set down at the right hand of the throne of God" (Heb 12:2)
  - The sacrifice is complete. The decisive evidence is given. The charges are answered. Now the High Priest applies what the cross secured — and that is the subject of Study 5.

## The Incarnation Is Permanent

One more truth, often overlooked:

- **He didn't take humanity temporarily**
  - "For there is one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus" (1 Tim 2:5)
  - Paul wrote this _after_ the ascension. Christ is _still_ called "the man." He did not shed humanity when He returned to heaven.

- **He is still our High Priest in human nature**
  - "We have such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens" (Heb 8:1)
  - "For in that he himself hath suffered being tempted, he is able to succour them that are tempted" (Heb 2:18)
  - He ministers from experience. He knows what it feels like because He still carries what it cost.

- **"He also himself likewise"**
  - The phrase from Hebrews 2:14 bears repeating: "he also himself _likewise_ took part of the same"
  - Likewise — in the same way. Not a modified version. The same.
  - And He keeps it. The incarnation is not a mission. It is an eternal commitment. The Creator permanently joined Himself to the creation He came to save.

[→] Study 3 said salvation is the restoration of God's image in man. Christ is the proof that it can be done — because He bears both images permanently. He is forever God-and-man. He did not take humanity on a temporary assignment. He married Himself to the race. And because He did, the bridge between heaven and earth (Study 2) can never be destroyed again.

## The Chain Extended

Study 3's chain:
**Image = Glory = Name = Character = Love = Law = Knowing God = Life**

This study adds:

- **Only the Author of the Image can restore it** — no angel, no created being has the standing
- **He restored it from within** — by becoming one of us, not by fixing us from outside
- **He overcame as a man** — not relying on divine prerogatives but on the Spirit available to us
- **His death satisfies justice** — making forgiveness _just_, not merely merciful
- **His life refutes Satan** — proving the law can be kept in human flesh
- **His incarnation is permanent** — the bridge never breaks again

## Appeal

The most staggering fact in the universe is not that God is powerful. It is that God became weak. That the Lawgiver submitted to His own law. That the Judge took the sentence. That the Creator entered the creation — not as a visitor, but as a permanent resident.

He did not send an angel. He did not change the rules. He did not lower the standard. He came Himself, took your nature, faced your temptations, bore your penalty, and kept the law you couldn't keep — all in human flesh, by the same Spirit He offers to you.

"He is faithful and just to forgive." Not faithful and lenient. Faithful and _just_. Because the price was already paid — by the only One who could pay it.

That is why it had to be Christ.
