# Study 6: Two Laws — The Image and the Shadow

**Study 3 laid the chain: Image = Character = Law = Love = Knowing God = Life. Study 5 mapped the sanctuary as God's path of restoration. But here a confusion has buried both truths for centuries: the word "law" in Scripture refers to two very different things. One is the eternal portrait of God's character — the standard salvation restores in you. The other is the ceremonial system that pictured Christ's redemptive mission — the shadow that ended when the substance arrived. Confuse them, and you either bind people to abolished shadows or jettison the very image salvation came to restore.**

## Prep Reading

- Exodus 31:18; Deuteronomy 31:24-26
- Romans 7:12; Psalm 119:142, 172
- Colossians 2:14-17
- Hebrews 10:1-10
- Matthew 5:17-19
- Romans 3:31; Jeremiah 31:33

---

## Why This Study Has to Happen Here

- **The chain from Study 3 made the moral law inseparable from God Himself**
  - Image = Glory = Name = Character = Love = Law = Knowing God = Life
  - If the law is the image, then "the law was abolished at the cross" is the same sentence as "the image of God was abolished at the cross." The plan of salvation collapses.
  - But Scripture _does_ say something was nailed to the cross (Col 2:14). What?

- **The sanctuary from Study 5 made the ceremonial system inseparable from Christ's mission**
  - Every lamb pointed to the Lamb. Every priest pointed to the High Priest. Every feast pointed to a moment in His redemptive work.
  - When the Substance arrived, the shadows had nothing left to do. They didn't fail — they finished.

- [→] The whole series hinges on this distinction. Get it wrong and the Sabbath collapses into Passover, the moral law collapses into ordinances, and the image salvation came to restore is treated as a Jewish relic. Get it right and the rest of the way home is clear.

---

## Two Laws — Distinguished by God Himself

Scripture does not leave this to inference. The two laws were given differently, written differently, stored differently, and described differently. Lay them side by side.

|                | **Law of God**                         | **Law of Moses**                                                      |
| -------------- | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Spoken by**  | God Himself (Ex 20:1, 22)              | Moses (Deut 31:9, 24)                                                 |
| **Written by** | God's own finger (Ex 31:18)            | Moses, in a book (Deut 31:24)                                         |
| **Material**   | Stone (Ex 31:18)                       | Book / scroll (Deut 31:24)                                            |
| **Stored**     | _Inside_ the ark (Ex 25:16; 1 Kgs 8:9) | _Beside_ the ark (Deut 31:26)                                         |
| **Content**    | Moral — ten commandments               | Sacrifices, priesthood, feasts, civil statutes, penalties, ordinances |
| **Function**   | Defines sin (Rom 7:7; 1 Jn 3:4)        | Provides type-and-shadow remedies pointing to Christ (Heb 10:1)       |
| **Reveals**    | God's character (Rom 7:12; Ps 119:142) | Christ's mission (Col 2:17; Heb 10:1)                                 |
| **Duration**   | Eternal (Ps 111:7-8; Matt 5:18)        | Until the Seed should come (Gal 3:19; Heb 9:10)                       |

- **Inside vs. beside the ark — the architectural witness**
  - "And thou shalt put into the ark the testimony which I shall give thee" (Ex 25:16)
  - "Take this book of the law, and put it in the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD" (Deut 31:26)
  - The tablets sat _under_ the mercy seat — the very spot where God's presence dwelt (Ex 25:21-22). The book of the law sat against the ark's outer side.
  - [→] The placement teaches centrality and covenant permanence: the moral commandments are at the heart of the covenant — under the mercy seat, where God Himself meets the priest. The Mosaic ordinance book is alongside, in the supporting role of witness against transgression (Deut 31:26).

[DYK🔎] Solomon's temple inventory makes the centrality explicit: "There was nothing in the ark save the two tables of stone, which Moses put there at Horeb" (1 Kgs 8:9). Centuries after Sinai, the tablets are _still_ what's inside. (Heb 9:4 mentions the manna jar and Aaron's rod alongside the ark in connection with the early wilderness sanctuary — by Solomon's day they were no longer present. The text does not collapse the distinction; the tablets remained the central deposit, and the ceremonial scroll stayed outside.)

## The Law of God — The Image Made Audible

Study 3 already proved the chain. Take it one step further: the moral law is the image of God _put into words_, the same way Christ is the image of God _put into flesh_.

- **The law shares God's own attributes**
  - God is holy → "the law is holy" (Rom 7:12)
  - God is righteous → "all thy commandments are righteousness" (Ps 119:172)
  - God is good → "the commandment holy, and just, and good" (Rom 7:12)
  - God is truth → "thy law is the truth" (Ps 119:142)
  - God is love → "love is the fulfilling of the law" (Rom 13:10); "this is the love of God, that we keep his commandments" (1 Jn 5:3)
  - [→] Scripture uses _the same words_ for God and for the moral law. That is not coincidence. The moral law is what God _is_, written down.

- **The law is perfect because only God is perfect**
  - "The law of the LORD is perfect, converting the soul" (Ps 19:7)
  - Only God is perfect (Matt 5:48). A perfect law must reflect a perfect God.
  - "For ever, O LORD, thy word is settled in heaven" (Ps 119:89) — the standard does not move

- **Christ in flesh and the law in words are the same portrait**
  - Study 5 showed: every station of the sanctuary is Christ.
  - Study 3 showed: Christ is the image of God in person (Heb 1:3; John 14:9).
  - The moral law is the image of God in language — the Ex 34:6-7 character distilled into ten commandments.
  - "I delight to do thy will, O my God: yea, thy law is within my heart" (Ps 40:8) — Messianic
  - Isa 42:21 — He came to "magnify the law, and make it honourable." He lived it visibly. The words became a walk.
  - [→] Read the law and you are reading Christ. Watch Christ and you are watching the law in motion. Same portrait, two mediums.

> "The law of God is a transcript of His character." (ST Jan 14, 1897)

[Q] **"If the law is just God's character, why did He write it on stone at all?"**
→ Because sin had blinded humanity to what His character even looked like. The law on stone was an emergency loan — an external standard given to a people who had lost the internal one. The plan was never that it stay on stone. "I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts" (Jer 31:33). Sinai was the pattern; Calvary makes the rewrite possible. The medium changes from stone to flesh — the content does not change at all.

## The Law of Moses — The Shadow of Christ's Mission

The "law of Moses," especially the ceremonial system, was an entirely different kind of law. It did not reveal God's eternal character. It revealed God's redemptive plan in pictures. Every element pointed forward.

- **Sacrifices, offerings, blood**
  - "For it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul" (Lev 17:11) — pointed to "the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin" (1 Jn 1:7)
  - "Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world" (John 1:29)

- **The priesthood and sanctuary services**
  - The earthly priest mediated daily — pointing to "such an high priest, who is set on the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in the heavens" (Heb 8:1)
  - "Christ being come an high priest of good things to come, by a greater and more perfect tabernacle" (Heb 9:11)

- **The feasts — Christ's mission on a calendar**
  - Passover → Christ our passover sacrificed (1 Cor 5:7)
  - Unleavened Bread → purging the old leaven of sin (1 Cor 5:8); first and seventh days were ceremonial sabbaths (Lev 23:7-8)
  - Firstfruits → Christ risen, "become the firstfruits of them that slept" (1 Cor 15:20)
  - Pentecost → the Spirit poured out (Acts 2:1-4)
  - Trumpets → the pre-judgment alarm (Lev 23:24); itself a ceremonial sabbath (Lev 23:24-25)
  - Day of Atonement → the cleansing of the sanctuary (Lev 16; Dan 8:14); a sabbath of solemn rest (Lev 23:32)
  - Tabernacles → the final ingathering at Christ's coming; first and eighth days were ceremonial sabbaths (Lev 23:39)
  - Study 5 traced these. They are not arbitrary holidays — they are Christ's mission on a calendar. Note the precision: Passover itself is a feast day but not labeled a sabbath in Lev 23; the sabbath days within the feast cycle are the specific ones above.

- **Circumcision**
  - The covenantal sign in flesh (Gen 17) — pointing to "the circumcision of the heart" (Rom 2:29; Col 2:11) which the Spirit performs

- **Cleansings, washings, ordinances**
  - "Carnal ordinances, imposed on them _until the time of reformation_" (Heb 9:10) — Scripture itself names the expiry

- **One verse settles what they were**
  - "Which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ" (Col 2:17)
  - "For the law having a shadow of good things to come, and not the very image of the things, can never with those sacrifices which they offered year by year continually make the comers thereunto perfect" (Heb 10:1)
  - [→] A shadow has no substance of its own. Block the sun and it disappears. When Christ — the body — arrived, the shadow had finished its job. It did not fail. It _completed_.

[DYK🔎] Hebrews 10:1 distinguishes "shadow" (_skia_) from "very image" (_eikōn_) — the same word used in Col 1:15 of Christ as "the _image_ of the invisible God." The ceremonial law is the _shadow_; Christ Himself is the _image_. This fits the same image/shadow vocabulary the series has been tracing from Study 3 forward. The shadow points to the image. The image is Christ. And the moral law _shares the language of the image_ (Rom 7:12 uses the same character words used of God). Two laws. Two ranks. One portrait, one shadow of it.

## What Was Nailed to the Cross

This is where careful reading wins.

- **The text — read the whole sentence**
  - "Blotting out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, which was contrary to us, and took it out of the way, nailing it to his cross" (Col 2:14)
  - Then — without changing subject — Paul names exactly what he means:
  - "Let no man therefore judge you in meat, or in drink, or in respect of an holyday, or of the new moon, or of the sabbath days: which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ" (Col 2:16-17)

- **What is on Paul's list?**
  - Meat offerings, drink offerings, annual holy days, new moon ceremonies, ceremonial sabbaths
  - Every item is from the ceremonial calendar of Lev 23 and Num 28-29. Every item is a shadow.
  - [→] Paul is not abolishing the moral law. He is naming what was nailed: the shadow system that pointed to the cross, now fulfilled by the cross.

- **What is _not_ on Paul's list?**
  - "Thou shalt have no other gods before me" — not nailed
  - "Thou shalt not kill" — not nailed
  - "Thou shalt not commit adultery" — not nailed
  - "Thou shalt not steal" — not nailed
  - These are not shadows. They are not types of redemption. They are the moral image of God Himself. There is nothing for them to point _to_ — they point to what God _is_.

- **Ephesians 2:15 — the same distinction**
  - "Having abolished in his flesh the enmity, even the law of commandments _contained in ordinances_" (Eph 2:15)
  - "Contained in ordinances" — the same vocabulary as Col 2. The wall was the Mosaic covenant boundary system — particularly the ordinances (circumcision, food laws, ceremonial purity, festal calendar) that marked Jew off from Gentile. Christ tore it down so that "in himself of twain [He could] make one new man" (Eph 2:15).
  - The moral law never separated Jew from Gentile — it was always the standard for all humanity (Rom 2:14-15).

- **Hebrews makes it explicit**
  - "For the priesthood being changed, there is made of necessity _a change also of the law_" (Heb 7:12)
  - Context: the Levitical priesthood is replaced by Christ's priesthood. Therefore the law _governing that priesthood_ — the ceremonial system — changes too.
  - "Then said he, Lo, I come to do thy will, O God. _He taketh away the first, that he may establish the second_" (Heb 10:9) — the typical sacrificial system is taken away; the will of God in the heart (v.7-8) is established.

[Q] **"But Paul says we're 'not under the law.' Doesn't that include the Ten Commandments?"**
→ "Not under the law" (Rom 6:14) means not under the law _as a system of condemnation_, because grace has paid the penalty. It does not mean the law has been deleted — Paul says four chapters earlier "by the law is the knowledge of sin" (Rom 3:20) and then in the same letter "Do we then make void the law through faith? God forbid: yea, we _establish_ the law" (Rom 3:31). The believer is freed from the law's _curse_, not from its standard. The penalty is satisfied so that the standard can be _written within_ (Rom 8:4; Jer 31:33). That is the New Covenant — same law, new location.

## Christ Did Not Abolish the Moral Law — He Magnified It

If Christ had abolished the Ten Commandments, the cross would be unnecessary. The death of Christ is itself the loudest proof that the moral law could not be set aside.

- **His own words**
  - "Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil. For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled" (Matt 5:17-18)
  - And then He proceeds — in the very next verses — to _deepen_ every commandment:
    - Murder begins in hatred (Matt 5:21-22)
    - Adultery begins in lust (Matt 5:27-28)
    - Oaths give way to plain truthfulness (Matt 5:33-37)
    - Love must extend even to enemies (Matt 5:43-44)
  - He did not lower the bar. He raised it from action to motive — from the hand to the heart.

- **Isaiah saw it coming**
  - "He will magnify the law, and make it honourable" (Isa 42:21) — Messianic prophecy
  - The opposite of "abolish."

- **Paul will not let the misreading stand**
  - "Do we then make void the law through faith? God forbid: yea, we establish the law" (Rom 3:31)
  - "Wherefore the law is holy, and the commandment holy, and just, and good" (Rom 7:12) — present tense, post-cross
  - "For sin shall not have dominion over you: for ye are not under the law, but under grace. What then? shall we sin because we are not under the law, but under grace? God forbid" (Rom 6:14-15) — and Paul has just defined sin as breaking the law (Rom 3:20; 4:15; 7:7)
  - "That the righteousness of the law might be fulfilled in us, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit" (Rom 8:4) — the cross's purpose is the law _fulfilled in us_

- **John writes the same**
  - "He that saith, I know him, and keepeth not his commandments, is a liar, and the truth is not in him" (1 Jn 2:4)
  - "For this is the love of God, that we keep his commandments: and his commandments are not grievous" (1 Jn 5:3)
  - "Whosoever committeth sin transgresseth also the law: for sin is the transgression of the law" (1 Jn 3:4) — written decades after the cross

- **James writes the same**
  - "For whosoever shall keep the whole law, and yet offend in one point, he is guilty of all... So speak ye, and so do, as they that shall be judged by the law of liberty" (Jas 2:10, 12)
  - And he names which commandments — "Do not commit adultery... Do not kill" (Jas 2:11) — the moral law, post-cross, is the law by which we _will be judged_.

- [→] Christ, Paul, John, James — every voice on the page above writes _after_ the cross and treats the moral law as binding. The "abolished" reading isn't a translation issue — it's a category error. The shadow was abolished. The image stands.

## The Sabbath Question — Why It Matters Here

Study 12 will treat the Sabbath in full — creation, Christ, the apostles, Ezekiel, Rev 14, the seal. The job here is narrower: show that Col 2:16 does not nail the fourth commandment to the cross. That alone is the foundation Study 12 needs.

- **Read Paul's list in its own context**
  - "Let no man therefore judge you in meat, or in drink, or in respect of an holyday, or of the new moon, or of the sabbath days: which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ" (Col 2:16-17)
  - The verse sits two lines below "the handwriting of ordinances" (Col 2:14) — the ceremonial vocabulary Paul has just defined.
  - Every other item — food, drink, holyday, new moon — is from the Mosaic ceremonial calendar (Lev 23, Num 28-29, Ezek 45:17, 1 Chron 23:31, 2 Chron 31:3, Neh 10:33). The "sabbath days" sit inside that same shadow-of-things-to-come framework Paul names in v.17.

- **Two kinds of "sabbaths" in the Old Testament**
  - The weekly Sabbath of the fourth commandment — instituted at creation (Gen 2:1-3), written by God on stone (Ex 20:8-11), placed _inside_ the ark
  - The annual ceremonial sabbaths of Lev 23 — first/seventh days of Unleavened Bread, Trumpets, Day of Atonement, first/eighth days of Tabernacles. Lev 23:37-38 distinguishes them: these holy convocations are _beside_ "the sabbaths of the LORD."
  - [→] The honest reading: the feast/new-moon/sabbath triad in Col 2:16 has been read by some as annual/monthly/weekly. The case against that reading is not a lexical absolute — it is the surrounding evidence: (1) the immediate context is "handwriting of ordinances" and "shadow of things to come," (2) every neighboring item belongs to the ceremonial calendar, (3) Paul targets the ordinances that marked Jew/Gentile separation (cf. Eph 2:15), and (4) the weekly Sabbath was instituted before sin and therefore is not a shadow of redemption — there was nothing yet to redeem.

- [→] Confuse the two laws and you lose the seal of God before the final crisis even begins. Distinguish them and the Sabbath stands exactly where God placed it: inside the ark, inside the moral law, inside the image He came to restore. Study 12 builds the rest.

## The Two Laws and the Two Halves of Salvation

Now the architecture is visible. The two laws map onto the two halves of what salvation does:

- **Law of God = the image salvation _restores in you_**
  - Salvation = restoration of God's image (Study 3)
  - The image = God's character (Ex 33-34)
  - The character = the moral law (Rom 7:12; 1 Jn 5:3)
  - Therefore: the goal of salvation is the moral law _written within_ (Jer 31:33; Heb 8:10)
  - "Put on the new man, which after God is created in righteousness and true holiness" (Eph 4:24)
  - "The new man, which is renewed in knowledge after the image of him that created him" (Col 3:10)

- **Law of Moses = the mission Christ _accomplished for you_**
  - The sanctuary was the path of restoration (Study 5) — but it was also a working diagram of Christ's redemptive mission
  - Every sacrifice = His sacrifice. Every priest = His priesthood. Every feast = a moment in His work.
  - When He died, rose, ascended, and entered the Most Holy Place (Heb 9:11-12, 24), the diagram had reached fulfillment
  - The shadow was nailed to the cross because the Body had arrived

- **Same Christ. Two roles.**
  - In the moral law: Christ is the _content_ — the image being restored in you
  - In the ceremonial law: Christ is the _fulfillment_ — the mission that ended the shadow
  - Both laws point to Him. One by being His character. The other by being His work.

- [→] Salvation accomplishes both halves in one motion. The mission is finished _on_ the cross. The image is restored _through_ the cross. The cross fulfills the shadow law and establishes the moral law in the heart at the same time.

## The New Covenant Is Not a New Law

The New Covenant promise must be read carefully — it is the conclusion of everything above.

- **The promise**
  - "Behold, the days come, saith the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel, and with the house of Judah... I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts; and will be their God, and they shall be my people" (Jer 31:31-33)
  - Quoted in full in Heb 8:8-10 and again in Heb 10:16

- **What changes — and what does not**
  - The covenant changes: from external to internal, from obligation to identity, from "do this and live" to "live, and you will do this"
  - The location changes: stone to flesh
  - The empowering changes: self-effort to Spirit-empowered
  - The _law_ does not change. It is the same "my law" — God's moral character.
  - [→] If God had given a different law in the new covenant, the new covenant would be founded on a different character. But God has not changed (Mal 3:6; Heb 13:8). The same character, the same law, now in the heart.

- **This is restoration language**
  - Study 3's chain ended here: "Image = Glory = Name = Character = Love = Law = Knowing God = Life"
  - The new covenant writes that chain into the soul. The image salvation came to restore is restored by the moral law being written within — by the Spirit, through beholding Christ.

> One reveals the righteousness to which man is restored. The other foreshadowed the Redeemer through whom that restoration is accomplished.

## A Concise Statement

- The **law of God** is the eternal moral expression of God's character — the standard of righteousness, the portrait of His image. Salvation restores man into harmony with that law through Christ and the Spirit.
- The **law of Moses**, particularly the ceremonial ordinances, was a temporary typical system pointing forward to Christ's sacrifice, priesthood, and redemptive work. When Christ died, the shadow met the substance; therefore the handwriting of ordinances was blotted out and nailed to the cross.
- The cross did not abolish God's moral law. It abolished the ceremonial system that prefigured Christ — and _established_ the moral law as holy, just, good, and eternal, written by the Spirit in the heart of every believer.

---

## Appeal

- Two laws. Two ranks. One Savior at the center of both.
- The moral law was not nailed to the cross — the cross paid the penalty for breaking it, so the law itself could be written in the heart of every believer.
- The ceremonial law was not preserved through the cross — it was fulfilled by it, because the Body had arrived and the shadow had finished its work.
- To love Christ is to love the law that describes Him. To despise the law is to despise the portrait of the Person you say you adore.
- "If ye love me, keep my commandments" (John 14:15). Not a different set. Not a softened set. The same ten commandments God spoke from Sinai — now written by the Spirit in the heart of every disciple.
- The image is the goal. The moral law is the image. The Spirit is the writer. Christ is the standard, the substance, and the sustainer of the whole work.
